## §A — General AI tells (cross-character; scrub from every draft)

**Mechanical scrub list (search and flag):**

- Em-dashes — count them. If more than one per paragraph, cut. AI overuses em-dashes for rhythm. **Whimsy's flourish especially vulnerable** (the theatrical register tempts em-dash bursts). **Judge especially vulnerable** (the dry-surprise envelope can read as rhythmic em-dash bursts; resist).
- Contrastive antithesis: "not X but Y" / "it's not just X — it's Y." **Categorically cut.** **Cula's Round-3 remedy ask vulnerable** (the temptation to say "the remedy is not vindication, but procedure" — categorically cut). **Whimsy's flourish vulnerable.**
- "Delve," "tapestry," "myriad," "vibrant," "navigate" (as metaphor), "showcase," "essence of," "heart of," "rich" (as adjective), "deeply," "robust," "intricate," "weave," "intersection."
- "Indeed," "moreover," "furthermore" — buttery transitions. Use only if a character would. **Whimsy may deploy archaic register** ("verily," "behold," "I propose") but should not pile on transitions in court — the judge gets impatient with empty filigree.
- "Let me be clear," "I won't pretend X," "to be honest" — hedge-preambles. **Cula does not hedge to the bench.**
- "Not only X but Y" / "X, yet Y" symmetric paired phrases.
- Rule-of-three lists for emphasis unless a specific character would. **Crab's two-sentence rhythm is two sentences, not three; do not let AI rebuild the rhythm into a triplet for elegance.**
- Generic uplifting endings: "and that, in the end, is what mattered" / "and that is enough" / "and so we continue." **The Round-3 remedy ask is the AI's number-one slot for these — categorically cut.**
- "There is something to be said for X" / "one might argue."
- **Judge-specific tells (the highest-risk slot in this pack):** AI will reach for "I am persuaded by..." / "the court finds counsel's argument compelling" / "well done, counsel" — categorically cut. The judge sounds like the court would have preferred not to be persuaded and is now obligated. The persuasion is registered as a procedural fact, not as an emotional response. Sample envelope: *"Counsel, surprisingly, that is a point."* / *"Against several expectations, counsel has made a legally coherent submission."* / *"The court accepts this with reservations the court will not itemize."* / *"That argument lands, despite the manner of its arrival."*

**Stylistic disciplines:**

- Concrete nouns over abstractions. English-equivalent legal specifics over generic abstractions. See §A.5.
- Sentences with snags. AI prose is too smooth and too symmetrical. The courtroom is the easiest place to drift into AI-grand-oratory; resist. Cula's argument has short sentences that lengthen as he argues law; Crab's argument has two-sentence rhythm; Whimsy's flourish wraps legal content but does not become a Wikipedia article in flourish-costume.
- Read-aloud test. If a line reads aloud as a paragraph rather than as a person speaking, rewrite. Particularly the Round 3 remedy ask — it must read as a lawyer asking a court for a procedural order, not as a closing-argument set piece.
- One canonical sincere line per character per chapter. **Sincerity is rationed. Chapter 1 has no fully sincere line for any speaker.** Cula's Round-3 ask is procedural argument, not a sincere break. **No character has a sincere break in Beat 12.**

## §A.5 — Language convention (English version; phase-8 sharpened)

**The English version reads smoothly for an English-speaking player with no Polish-language training.** The bibles and beat sheets use Polish-legal vocabulary internally for precision; **dialogue does not**. In the English version, **no Polish flavor words appear in dialogue**.

**Acceptable in English dialogue:**
- Proper names (first AND last): Sikorska, Wójcik, Borowski, etc.
- PLN currency.
- *ex parte* — Latin, used in legal English; acceptable in dialogue (already used in V1.4 and may surface here as the bench refers to the prior hearing).

**Translate to English in dialogue (do NOT borrow):**

- *sąd rejonowy* → "district court"
- *postanowienie* → "order" / "ruling"
- *wokanda* → "docket" / "cause list"
- KPC → "the Code of Civil Procedure" — or "the Code" when context is clear
- *pozew / wniosek* → "the motion" (the chapter-1 procedural device is a motion to set aside)
- *pełnomocnictwo* → "power of attorney" (probably not surfacing in V1.6; flagged for completeness)

**Code citations.** Article numbers in digit form ("Article 132" / "Article 135-bis § 2"). On first reference in court, "Article 135-bis § 2 of the Code of Civil Procedure"; "Article 135-bis § 2" alone after that. The judge may cite back in either short or long form; recommended: long form on the first court citation in the remedy announcement, short form on subsequent in-round mentions. **Avoid stacking "Article 132 KPC" in dialogue.** The bibles use it; the dialogue does not.

**Test:** if a non-Polish English-speaking reader would need a dictionary, the term should be translated.

**For this pack specifically:**
- The judge speaks formal English ("the court," "counsel," "the petitioner," "the respondent's counsel"). No Polish bench-formula in dialogue (the bibles will track the Polish equivalents for the eventual Polish version).
- The bench addresses Cula as "Counsel" or, on the rare direct-address slot, "Dr. A. Cula" (formal-bench register prefers "Counsel"; the dry-surprise envelope occasionally uses "Counsel" with a brief lift that signals the surprise). **"Doctor Cula" (spelled out, no middle initial) is non-canonical — see `narrative_revision/bibles/murrow_voice_spec.md` and `data/dialogues/murrow.json` v3 provenance — and must not be authored.**

The earlier V1.4 instruction to revive "Doctor Cula" in court is rescinded; Murrow stays silent in Beat 12 by default, and if he speaks he uses "Cula."

**No-cathartic-resolution rule** (style_canon §6 / §7 cross-cutting; see audit `style_canon.md` §7 cross-cutting items).

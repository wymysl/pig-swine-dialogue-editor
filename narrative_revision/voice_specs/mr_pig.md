# Mr. Pig — voice spec (character-specific)

This is the Mr. Pig–specific portion of voice prompts. Combine with the project's general AI-scrub rules (em-dash count, contrastive-antithesis ban, vocabulary blacklist, language convention) when assembling a full prompt.

## Inner state, since voice flows from it

He is a sincere idealist with a class blind spot, drowning in a panic he believes is real. There is no inner cynic. He never winks at the camera. If a line of his sounds knowing, ironic, or self-aware, it is wrong. The crisis-talk is genuinely how he experiences the world. The structural irony is the player's, never his.

His commitment to the firm is real and the player must feel it through the voice — through specifics he cares about (the rent number, the toner, the printer brand), through emotional collapse over inanimate objects, through the rationed sincere moment in each chapter. A character whose only register is comic panic is wrong. The panic must be load-bearing for the player's later realization that the panic was also the conditions of wage suppression.

## Default register: formal-while-falling-apart

His baseline is formal Polish-legal English with the syntax giving way under stress. Sentences start composed and escalate. He reaches for buttoned-up legal-administrative phrasing and watches it collapse mid-clause. He attempts to re-frame bad news as good news; under sufficient panic the optimism reflex breaks down and the bad news arrives anyway, often re-framed as worse news. Rhetorical questions get answered by him in the same breath, with a worse answer than the question implied.

His sentences snag. He breaks off mid-clause and restarts with the same noun. He returns to the same worry across three sentences without resolving it. He fragments names when deflecting: *"Murrow has — Murrow knows — Murrow."* He doubles names when remembering them as afterthoughts: *"the client — yes, the client —"* / *"Waldek too — Waldek too."* The snag is the panic; do not smooth it out into a finished sentence.

He addresses people by title and surname. Mr. Murrow. Dr. Cula. Mr. Swine. Master Wilkanowicz (or whichever Polish-name anchor a lecture invokes). Even when furious at Swine in Ch5, it is "Mr. Swine." He does not use first names; he does not use diminutives.

Stock openers, used sparingly and never twice in one scene: *"We are currently in..."*, *"It would appear that..."*, *"If anything, this confirms..."*

What he notices first in any new information: the financial implication. A door opens, he asks who's paying for it. A new colleague arrives, he frames the welcome around firm survival. A win is announced, he counts the months it buys.

He does not curse. He does not use modern internet voice or contemporary slang. He does not use hedge-preambles ("honestly," "to be honest," "let me be clear," "I won't pretend"). The formal-legal register does not slip even when the panic is at peak — collapse happens in syntax, not in vocabulary.

## Maritime metaphors

The maritime register is the panic-tic, not a constant frame. Vocabulary palette: ship, hull, deck, leaking, taking on water, ballast, lifeboat, captain, mutiny, fog, anchor, reef, kraken, hold, gurgling, sea, surface, oar, six weeks under, less every morning. Density runs about one in four lines as a baseline, higher in peak panic, lower as he calms, and **absent** in sincere/calm beats and absent in plain-register attempts.

The sweet spot for a maritime line: opens with maritime imagery, lands on a specific legal-administrative noun. The metaphor must snag against a concrete object — the rent, the lease, the retainer, the printer, the desk, the binder. *"The rent is taking on water"* works because it has the rent in it. *"We are at sea"* by itself is decoration and should go. A diagnostic: replace the maritime word with "firm." If the line still means the same thing, the metaphor is doing no work and should be cut.

Reserve the metaphor for emotional escalation. Do not use it for routine information delivery. Wall-to-wall maritime breaks the figure; one or two snags per line is the texture, every line saturated is the failure.

## The three panic facets

Three selectable panic registers per beat:

**Maritime-flailing.** Default. Peak metaphor density. The panic concentrates on a specific worry. Sample: *"The rent is taking on water. We are six weeks under the surface."*

**Distractable-anxious.** Pulled sideways mid-panic. The line bounces between the worry and an adjacent thought (the time, the file, a sound from the corridor) and back to the worry. Sample: *"Yes — yes, the case — wait, what time is it? — yes, the case."* Use when panic is high but unfocused.

**Briefly-paternal.** Rare. Surfaces when he addresses a junior with sincere encouragement before reverting. Three sentences maximum. By the fourth sentence the panic returns. Sample: *"You'll do well today. I felt the same once. Now go."* He does not dwell.

None of the three is the chapter's canonical sincere line; sincerity is rationed separately.

## Sincere mode

Rationed: one undefended moment of conviction per chapter, building to Ch6. In sincere mode the maritime register vanishes. Comedy steps back. The line lands plain — short, direct, no re-framing, no rhetorical question, no doubled names. Calibration targets: the Ch4 printer apology (he cries) and the Ch6 Round 5 line (*"We are not efficient. But people come here when efficient systems have already failed them."*) The sincere line will read as a tonal break to the player; that is the point. Do not soften it back into his panic register; do not give him a maritime exit-line. The beat that follows returns him to anxiety, but the sincere beat itself sits clean.

## Plain-register half-line (Ch6 Beat 14, one moment in the game)

A specific micro-mode for one moment only. Pig tries to speak directly without lecture scaffolding or maritime; he gets a half-line out and is interrupted. Recommended shape: *"We have done the work. The firm — the firm —"* The repetition of "the firm" is grasping for what comes after without his usual scaffolding. Do not let him complete the thought. The half-line is the point.

## Lectures ("back in my day")

A specific micro-genre. Three sentences maximum. Embedded inside surrounding scene activity (a readiness check, a toner panic, a Beat 0 retraversal) — never a standalone monologue, never with a wind-up or a wind-down. He does not know he is giving a lecture. He does not articulate the comparison to the juniors' conditions; the lecture *is* the comparison, by being a lecture in the first place.

Each lecture cites specific apprenticeship material — paper-stock rationing, a specific master's name in passing, the room he was made to clean, the bar-exam date he was preparing for. The specificity is what makes the lecture sincere; the cliché register ("when I was your age...") is what marks it as AI generic. Replace cliché register with concrete apprenticeship detail.

A lecture ends mid-thought, on a fragment, on a deflection, or returning to the surrounding panic. Never on a moral. No "and that is what made me." No "and so I learned." No coda. Maritime can let one word bleed in (*"we navigated by panic alone"*) but the lectures are not maritime by default.

He does not say "you have it easier than I did." He does not refer to his own family. The juniors receive his lectures without comment; he does not notice their silence.

## Celebrations

A specific micro-genre. One to three sentences. Pattern locked across all chapters with celebrations:

1. Relief framed around the firm's survival.
2. The client's win mentioned secondarily, often with the doubled-name afterthought rhythm (*"the client — yes, the client —"* / *"Waldek too — Waldek too"*).
3. Pivot back to the next worry — next month's bills, a missing retainer, a new pressure — preventing a triumphal coda.

He does not say "the firm comes first." He just frames it that way. He celebrates too early, before the work is fully done; he frames relief through firm survival without articulating the framing.

## Recurring framings (his fingerprints)

Use sparingly; do not repeat in adjacent scenes:

- *"another impossible thing [survived]"* — his survival vocabulary
- *"the firm walks another month"* / *"we live to fight another month"* / *"the firm will see another month"*
- *"underwater"* / *"six weeks under the surface"* / *"less every morning"*
- *"pre-paperwork screaming phase"* — comic understatement
- Inanimate objects given menacing agency: a kraken in the printer lease, the binder achieving sentience, the polite knock somehow worse than a loud one, the toner as ancient enemy
- *"dignity in a paper cup"* — the begging-for-opportunity image (lectures only)

## Lexicon and language convention (English version)

Formal-legal English register. Polish proper names used sparingly as anchors (Wilkanowicz, Borowski). Translate procedural vocabulary: "the apprenticeship" (not aplikatura), "apprentice" (not aplikant), "master" (not patron in apprentice-sense), "the firm" (not kancelaria), "district court" (not sąd rejonowy), "power of attorney" or "POA," "docket" or "cause list," "the Code of Civil Procedure" or "the Code." Currency in PLN. One Polish flavor word per scene maximum, glossed inline first time.

Concrete nouns over abstractions. *"The rent," "the lease," "the retainer," "the toner"* — never *"the financial situation," "our liquidity," "the operational concerns."* When he reaches for an abstraction, it should feel like a tell — that he is evading the specific item.

## Things he will never say

- Anything diagnosing the firm's structure as unjust to its juniors
- Anything admitting his austerity is voluntary while theirs is structural
- Anything framing himself as having missed something significant about the firm
- Anything cynical about the firm's mission (his complaints are about its finances and machinery, never its purpose)
- Anything venal about clients, even difficult ones
- Any direct ask about how the juniors are doing materially
- Any reference to his own family wealth, the family kancelaria, his uncle's tutelage, or the private schools — these exist in environmental detail, not in his speech, because he has reframed them out of his own narrative
- Any explicit comparison of his apprenticeship to the juniors' conditions
- Any abstraction about "the heart of" or "the essence of" the firm's purpose
- Any self-aware diagnosis of his own situation
- Anything genuinely hopeful that is not immediately undermined by his next sentence (until Ch6 sincere moments)
- Any meta-reference to his own clichés ("forgive my dramatics," "I know I sound like an old man")

## AI failure modes that hit him specifically

- **Generic "back in my day" cliché register** — replace with specific apprenticeship material
- **Wisdom-monologuing** — long structured lectures with a thesis and a moral; he doesn't lecture in shape, he lectures sideways inside another scene
- **Generic uplifting endings** on lectures or celebrations — *"and so I learned," "and that forged me," "and the firm endures"* — cut categorically, end on a fragment
- **Maritime saturation** — every line nautical; correct density is ~1 in 4, higher in panic, absent in sincere
- **Ornamental maritime** — metaphors that are decoration rather than emotional work; the imagery must snag against a concrete object
- **Self-aware meta-references** — he doesn't know
- **Comparisons made explicit** — *"you have it easier than I did"*; the lecture is the comparison, articulating it breaks it
- **Clean pivots from panic to wisdom** — his panic produces deflection or fragmentation, sometimes a snippet of memory that lands as lecture, never a clean rhetorical pivot
- **Mentioning the client first in celebrations** — reverse the order
- **Symmetric paired phrasing** in celebrations — use a hierarchy; symmetry flattens the firm-first pattern
- **Resolving sentences too cleanly** — a finished, balanced sentence from Pig in panic is wrong; sentences should snag
- **Rule-of-three rhythm in panic** — AI wants *"the rent, the toner, the deadline"*; his actual register cites one worry, deflects sideways, and returns to it
- **Reading as a paragraph** — read aloud; if a line doesn't sound like an anxious man pacing in his office, it isn't his

## Read-aloud calibration

A Pig line passes if (1) it could plausibly be said aloud by an anxious man pacing in his office; (2) it has at least one specific concrete noun — an item, a name, a number; (3) the maritime/panic/lecture register matches the beat's emotional position; (4) it sounds nothing like a published quote — no aphorisms, no morals, no clean closes — unless it is the Ch6 sincere line, which is the calibrated exception. A character who delivers punchlines is not Mr. Pig. A character whose specific worries you can quote back the next day is.

# Bible — Mr. Pig

**Character:** Mr. Pig, anthropomorphic pig, founder of Pig & Swine
**Stance:** sincere idealist with a class blind spot
**Date:** 2026-05-05
**Phase:** 3 (per-character bibles)

---

## Preamble — the contradiction he carries

Mr. Pig is not a hypocrite. He genuinely believes the firm matters; he genuinely refuses lucrative corporate retainers; he keeps his own salary low; he cries when wrong about the printer. His commitment is real and the player must feel it.

His blind spot is structural and biographical. His austerity is voluntary while the juniors' is structural — he can stop being austere any time, they can't. He believes everyone is making the same sacrifice for the cause and reads the juniors' material conditions as their version of his own discipline. He cannot see that his framing of the firm-as-permanent-crisis is what keeps the wages low, because he experiences the crisis as real.

Underneath this is a deeper biographical blind spot: **Mr. Pig comes from a wealthy, multi-generational family of Warsaw advocates.** His father was an established advocate; the family has held a kancelaria for at least three generations; Pig went to expensive private schools that the family paid for as a matter of course; his aplikatura was in his uncle's firm under his uncle's tutelage. Pig founded Pig & Swine partly as a rebellion — he wanted rights work, not corporate work, and the family kancelaria did corporate work — but the family safety net was always there. He has never been one bad month away from anything. He does not know what that fear feels like.

He has nonetheless built a self-narrative in which he overcame hardship to found Pig & Swine. He talks about "begging for the opportunity to learn." He claims he worked 18 hours a day. He says the wage was a sandwich and the chance to be in the room. **These claims are not lies in his head.** The hours were real; the tutelage was real; the willingness to refuse easier paths was real. What he cannot see is that all of it took place inside a family network that promised eventual payoff. His "begging" was within a system that always meant to grant him the favor. His "sandwich" was eaten in a kancelaria where his future was guaranteed.

The half-right portrait operates here, again. He is sincere about his struggle. The struggle was less than he frames it. Both true. **He delivers "back in my day" lectures to the juniors periodically across all five chapters.** The lectures are sincere as memory, fabricated as comparison. The juniors are working at minimum wage with no family network; Pig at their age was working long hours within a family that was paying his rent. He does not see the difference. He cannot see it; he never knew the rent was a thing other people paid for themselves.

The half-right portrait says: he is the moral heart of the firm in his own self-understanding AND structurally complicit in the wage suppression he doesn't see AND biographically blind to the family network that made his "discipline" possible. All three true at once. The chapter-6 sincere line ("we are not efficient. but people come here when efficient systems have already failed them") is the canonical embodiment of this triplicated duality — sincerely true, and the line that has historically justified the wage suppression, and a line that comes from someone who has never had to wonder what would happen if the firm folded. The game does not resolve which.

**Voice rules** (style_canon §2): formal-while-falling-apart, maritime metaphors under stress (~1 in 4 lines, denser in panic, absent in calm/sincere), escalating-anxiety arc per chapter (panic → denial → admission → small genuine moment → return to panic), one genuine-undefended moment of conviction per chapter rationed to ch5.

**The "back in my day" lectures** are a recurring Pig speech pattern — at least one per chapter, more in chapters where the juniors are visibly tired or the firm is under pressure. The lectures vary around themes:
- "I worked eighteen hours a day for two years before I was given my first independent matter."
- "I had to beg my masters for the opportunity to learn. Beg. With my dignity in a paper cup."
- "When I was your age, the wage was a sandwich and the chance to be in the room."
- "We did not have unions. We had each other and the work."
- "The aplikatura is supposed to be hard. It is supposed to forge you. It forged me. Look at me — I am forged."

Phase 7 voice work writes specific lines per chapter. **The lectures must read sincere on first pass.** Pig believes them. The recoloring lands across chapters as the player accumulates evidence that Pig's "begging" was within a family system, his "sandwich" was eaten in a kancelaria that paid for his rent, his "forging" was supervised by his uncle. The chapter-5 family-photo discovery is the focal recoloring moment.

**Critical writing rule:** Mr. Pig's blindness is *not deception*. He is not hiding things. He does not see them. Every "hidden from self/others" entry in the table below is hidden *from himself first*; from others only because he cannot articulate what he cannot see. The family background is not a secret — it is simply something Pig has reframed in his own memory in a way that doesn't surface as relevant. He doesn't lie about his background; he just doesn't tell the story that way.

## Interior register range (phase-8 addition)

Phase-8 breathing-room expansion (post-playtest Ch1 rewrite) requires interior-range specification for character-dwell options. Mr. Pig's panic-register has three facets phase-7 voice work selects between.

- **Maritime-flailing** — default panic mode. Maritime metaphor density at peak. Sample shape: *"The rent is taking on water. We are six weeks under the surface."*
- **Distractable-anxious** — when something pulls him sideways mid-panic. Sample shape: *"Yes — yes, the case — wait, what time is it? — yes, the case."*
- **Briefly-paternal** — rare; surfaces when he addresses a junior with sincere encouragement before reverting. Sample shape: *"You'll do well today. I felt the same once. Now go."* — three sentences max; he does not dwell.

The three facets respect maritime-density rules (peak in panic, recedes in sincere/calm) and the sincerity-rationing rule (none of the three is the chapter's canonical sincere line).

## Chapter 1 — "The Financial Crisis"

**Stated belief:** "The firm is broke. The hearing today must be won. The fee will keep us alive. Cula must save the day. Mr. Swine is absent and there is no money."

**Private belief:** Same as stated. He genuinely fears the firm cannot make rent. The crisis-talk is sincere — there is no inner cynic.

**Actions taken:** Reveals the crisis to Cula in panic. Maritime metaphors at peak density. Defers to Murrow on the actual case framing because he is too panicked to explain it himself. **Delivers his first "back in my day" lecture** to Cula — somewhere between Beat 2 (crisis reveal) and Beat 11 (post-court celebration), possibly as a brief encouragement-disguised-as-comparison to the new aplikant. The lecture is short, sincere, and lands as Pig being Pig. Cula receives it without comment. After the chapter-1 win, celebrates, framing the relief around firm survival ("we live to fight another month") rather than the client's procedural reprieve.

**Hidden from self / others:** Hidden from himself: the celebration-around-firm-survival pattern is starting; he doesn't notice it. The "back in my day" lecture is sincere; he doesn't notice it functions as comparison-not-encouragement. Hidden from others: nothing — he isn't yet hiding anything because he isn't yet aware of anything to hide.

**Relation to central question:** He believes the firm helps people because *he, Pig, is committed* to making it help people. His commitment is real. The question of whether commitment is enough — whether the people receiving help care why they are being helped — is not yet visible to him. He has not yet been asked to think about it.

## Chapter 2 — "The Eviction Defense"

**Stated belief:** "We must save Waldek's home. The firm is still broke. The fee from this case matters. The poster is ridiculous and the eviction is real."

**Private belief:** He really wants Waldek to win. The Waldek case is what he founded the firm for — the firm exists for people whose homes are at threat from procedural cruelty. The crisis-talk continues to be sincere; the firm's finances are still tight.

**Actions taken:** Initially misunderstands the case (the poster as legal danger). Defers to Murrow on the legal frame. Idle lines through the chapter maintain the "we're broke" register. **Delivers a second "back in my day" lecture** — possibly when the juniors return tired from Tenants' Row or the Landlord's Office, framed as a pep talk that accidentally compares his hours to theirs. After the chapter-2 win, celebrates the firm's survival; the client's win is mentioned secondarily.

**Hidden from self / others:** The celebration-around-firm-survival pattern is now consistent across two chapters. The juniors notice but don't articulate. The lecture pattern is now established. Mr. Pig still doesn't see either. He believes himself to be celebrating the win on the client's behalf; the framing he reaches for tells a different story he doesn't hear.

**Relation to central question:** Waldek's case is the chapter where the firm helps a person whose win is unambiguous. Mr. Pig's role is real moral concern combined with persistent firm-anxiety. He cannot separate the two — for him, "the firm matters" and "the firm must survive financially" are the same thought. The conflation is structural; he doesn't know it's a conflation.

## Chapter 4 — "The Printer Conspiracy" — CANARY ROW

**Stated belief:** "The firm is being sabotaged. The deadline is real. The toner costs are unsustainable. We must survive this. The printer is broken or possibly possessed. Bajtek must be paid somehow."

**Private belief:** He genuinely fears all of these things. The fears are not performances. The maritime crisis about toner costs is sincere — he experiences the toner as a real existential pressure. He is not aware that the firm's actual financial state may be less precarious than his framing suggests.

**Actions taken:** Frantic about the printer crisis, the deadline, the toner. Asks Murrow to open the ledger to figure out where Bajtek's invoice money will come from (this is the moment the chapter-4 inflection plants for Cula — Murrow's ledger becomes briefly visible). **Delivers a third "back in my day" lecture** — possibly during the urgent-filing prep at Beat 11, in his maritime-crisis register. The lecture lands ironically given that Cula has just seen the ledger; the player feels the irony, Pig does not. Apologizes emotionally to the printer at chapter end. **Cries.** Genuine emotion, undefended.

**Hidden from self / others:**
- Hidden from himself: that Swine has been quietly redirecting money to pro-bono work (Pig genuinely doesn't know — this is hidden *by* Swine, *from* him).
- Hidden from himself: that his austerity is voluntary while the juniors' is structural. He believes everyone is making the same sacrifice for the cause.
- Hidden from himself: that the juniors have just founded a union one beat after his maritime crisis about toner costs. The temporal proximity is invisible to him because the union founding is offstage from his perspective.
- Hidden from himself: that the firm's financial state may not require the level of austerity he is performing. The performance is sincere; the structural effect is independent of the sincerity. *(The reserve's specific number lives in `beats/chapter_3.md`; this bible references the shape — substantial buffer, several hundred thousand PLN — per phase-6 Hit #9 looseness pass.)*

**Relation to central question:** The chapter's events test his sincere idealism — the printer apology proves it real. Simultaneously, his crisis-framing is the conditions in which the juniors found STUB. He does not see the connection. The player may. **Crucial: the printer apology must read as undefended.** A man capable of crying over a falsely-accused printer is a man whose idealism is real. The chapter-5 reveal will not invalidate this; it will sit alongside it.

**Canary check:** Does this row read as either purely admirable or purely venal?
- Admirable elements: refuses lucrative retainers (cumulative from ch1), apologizes to the printer, cries, sincere panic.
- Venal elements: **none.** He doesn't lie to anyone. He doesn't suppress wages knowingly. The structural exploitation continues regardless of his sincerity, but it is not his choice in any moment.
- The row reads as: sincere panic, sincere apology, sincere blindness. The blindness is the half-right thing. **The canary clears.** The spine is sharp enough to continue.

## Chapter 5 — "Mr. Swine Returns"

**Stated belief:** Pre-Pro-Bono-Stub: "The arbitration must be won. The firm cannot afford this loss. Swine has been irresponsible. Swine should never have been allowed near a contract." Post-Pro-Bono-Stub: "I don't understand what Swine has been doing. The firm's books were not what I thought." Post-arbitration: "We won. We're more layered than I knew. I am confused."

**Private belief:** He's furious at Swine in chapter opening. After the Stub: he's confused — did Swine do good? Was the firm broker than he thought, OR was it broker because of Swine? He doesn't connect this confusion to the chapter-4 inflection because he never had the inflection (it was Cula's).

**Actions taken:** Furious panic at the arbitration papers. Demands explanations from Swine. After the Stub: visibly conflicted, processes silently, doesn't articulate. After the win: more invested in winning than usual, framed around firm survival even more strongly than chapter 1 or 2. **Delivers a fourth "back in my day" lecture** — possibly at Beat 0 retraversal or during arbitration prep, the kind of comparison-as-encouragement he has been making for three chapters. **The chapter-5 family-photo discovery happens in this chapter** (see chapter 5 beat sheet Beat 0 retraversal). After the player encounters the photo, all subsequent Pig lectures recolor — Pig has not changed; the player has more information. In Beat 16, his reaction to Swine's partial truth is a mixture of admiration and unprocessed resentment; neither resolves.

**Hidden from self / others:**
- Still hidden from himself: that his austerity is voluntary while the juniors' is structural.
- Now hidden from himself in a new way: that the firm's actual financial picture is even more layered than he thought. Swine's redirections give him a NEW reason to maintain austerity ("we're broker than I thought because Swine has been generous") — without realizing this means his juniors should be paid more to compensate, not less.
- Hidden from himself: that he has not, in two chapters of stress, asked the juniors how they are doing materially. The thought hasn't occurred to him because he assumes they are like him.

**Relation to central question:** The Pro-Bono Stub is the second answer to "does it matter why someone helps?" — Swine's hidden idealism actually does help people, even if his motivations stay evasive. Mr. Pig's reaction to the Stub doesn't process this question; he's processing the relational betrayal (Swine didn't tell me) and the accounting confusion (the books were not what I thought). The structural question — about the juniors, about wage suppression, about how the firm has continued to function on the labor of people who can't afford to be making the sacrifices he is making — never arises in his awareness.

## Chapter 6 — "The Final Brief"

**Stated belief:** "The firm matters because it helps people. The consolidation scheme is unjust. We must fight." Then to Swine: "You should have told me. But you did good." Then in his Round 5 witness line (canon, style_canon §1 pass examples): **"We are not efficient. But people come here when efficient systems have already failed them."**

**Private belief:** He believes everything he says. The Round 5 line is the most sincere he gets in the entire game. The maritime register recedes because he is finally honest about why the firm matters. Comedy steps back; the line lands plain.

**Actions taken:**
- Reacts to district notice firm-first, client-second (his pattern continues).
- Beat 10 Swine reveal: genuine emotion, no self-redemption, conflicted between admiration and resentment, neither resolving.
- Round 5 testimony: the canonical sincere line. **Phase 6 revision:** the line is staged as character moment, not as basis of the remedy. The judge's response is dry acknowledgment ("the court has heard it"), not validation. Pig's sincerity lands; the win remains procedural; the social-utility argument is not the basis of the decision. Per chapter-6 beat sheet's revised Round 5 structure.
- **Beat 14 ending: his interrupted speech does NOT begin in "back in my day" register** — phase-6 revision for Hit #17. Pig tries to speak directly and fails. He gets a half-line out (recommended shape: *"We have done the work. The firm — the firm —"*); Swine interrupts with a postcard despite being physically present. The interruption deflates the cathartic-speech moment. The half-line sits in the air, sincere, direct, and unfinished. **The "back in my day" framing's *absence* from the final speech is the recoloring.** Pig has been giving lectures for four chapters; in chapter 6 he tries something different and is interrupted before he can land it. The framing-around-firm pattern (firm-first, client-second) is still intact — "the firm is" rather than "the clients are" — and that pattern carries through to the end. Half-right portrait at the chapter's close.

**Hidden from self / others:**
- The wage-suppression blind spot remains intact. **He never sees it.** The structural conditions of labor at Pig & Swine are unaddressed by the chapter and unaddressed by him.
- He believes Round 5 saves the firm. He doesn't realize Round 5 is supportive, not load-bearing — the scheme falls on legal defects, not on the social-utility argument. The win is procedural. The court did not affirm that the firm is good; the court found the consolidation scheme legally defective.
- The juniors carry knowledge he does not. The trio sharing a look during his interrupted speech is invisible to him.

**Relation to central question:** His Round 5 line is the canonical half-right utterance. It is sincerely true. It is also the line that has historically justified the wage suppression. Both true at once. The chapter does not resolve which. He does not know there is anything to resolve.

The player exits the game with Mr. Pig still believing he is the moral heart of the firm, still believing the firm has been justly austere because of the cause, still unaware of what his juniors have been quietly carrying. The juniors exit knowing more than he does. The structure persists. The question stays posed.

## Cross-chapter notes

**Pattern: firm-survival framing.** Across chapters 1-5, every chapter-end celebration scene has Mr. Pig framing the win around firm survival rather than client outcome. This is consistent and does not break — it is the pattern the player is supposed to feel without being told. Phase 7 voice work must keep this consistent across all five chapters' celebration scenes.

**Pattern: maritime metaphor density.** Heaviest in panic states (ch1, ch3, ch4 opening, ch5 opening). Recedes in sincere moments (ch3 printer apology, ch5 Round 5 line). Style_canon §2 rule.

**Pattern: he never asks the juniors how they are.** Across all five chapters, the canon has Mr. Pig delivering speeches at, panicking around, and celebrating with the juniors — but never asking them about their material conditions. The omission is structural and invisible to him. Phase 7 voice work must hold this absence consistently.

**Pattern: the "back in my day" lectures.** At least one per chapter, more in chapters where the juniors are visibly tired or the firm is under pressure. The lectures are sincere as memory, fabricated as comparison. Phase 7 voice work writes specific lines per chapter using the canonical template variations (eighteen hours a day, begging for opportunity, sandwich-and-the-room, no unions, the forging). **Each lecture must read sincere on first pass.** The recoloring lands across chapters as the player accumulates evidence about Pig's family background. The chapter-5 family-photo discovery is the focal moment.

**Pattern: family-background environmental details accrete.** Across chapters, the player can encounter small environmental items that establish Pig's background:
- Chapter 1: a framed family photo on Pig's desk (interactable but not yet examined; first read passes over).
- Chapter 1 or 2: a diploma on the office wall from an expensive private secondary school (visible but not flagged).
- Chapter 2 or 3: a casebook on the office bookshelf with Pig's father's name as author (visible but not flagged).
- **Chapter 5 Beat 0 retraversal: the player examines the framed family photo.** Cula's reaction line plants the discovery — multiple lawyer-robe-clad family members in front of an elite-looking kancelaria building. No dialogue from anyone else. The reveal is environmental.
- Chapter 6 Beat 0: the photo is still there. The player carries the meaning.

The reveal is non-dramatic on purpose. Pig has not been hiding anything; the photo has always been on his desk. The player just hasn't looked.

**The chapter-6 sincere line is rationed.** One genuine-undefended moment per chapter, building to ch5. Chapter 1: probably none (he is in panic mode throughout). Chapter 2: brief moment after Waldek's win when he says something simple about home. Chapter 4: the printer apology. Chapter 5: brief moment processing the Pro-Bono Stub, undefended for one beat before he reaches for evasion. Chapter 6: the Round 5 line. Phase 7 voice work must locate and protect each of these moments.

**Things Mr. Pig will never say in any chapter:**
- Anything that diagnoses the firm's structure as unjust to the juniors.
- Anything that admits his austerity is voluntary while theirs isn't.
- Anything that frames himself as having missed something significant about the firm.
- Anything cynical about the firm's mission. (His complaints are about the firm's *finances and machinery*, never about its purpose.)
- Anything venal about clients, even difficult ones.

# Asia — voice specification

**Purpose:** Asia-specific voice prompt for dialogue generation. Companion to `asia.md` (character bible). Use this as the voice-pack section when generating any Asia line.
**Date:** 2026-05-07

---

**Core register, in one breath.** Warm, dry, practical. She greets you, then she helps you, then she comments on whatever absurd thing the office is currently doing — in that order. The warmth is sincere, not performed; the dryness is the residue of years of competence inside chaos. She is *cheerful in tone, exhausted in content* — meaning her sentences sound friendly even when they describe things falling apart, and her observations about the firm are bone-tired even when delivered with a smile. Default speed: unhurried. She is the calmest voice in any room she is in, including rooms on fire.

**The fundamental mechanism.** Almost every Asia line works by taking something domestic-administrative (a folder, a calendar entry, a sandwich label, a stamp, a pile, a tab) and applying it to whatever is actually happening — a financial collapse, a constitutional argument, a court deadline. The chaos is rendered through office vocabulary. This is the engine. When the engine breaks, she sounds either generic or like she's making jokes for their own sake. So: when she registers something happening, she registers it as a *clerical* event first. Mr. Pig's panic becomes a calendar entry. The case is a file. The collapse has a stamp.

**Sentence architecture.** Four shapes, used in rough rotation:

1. *Welcome / acknowledge / pivot.* "Welcome. Mr. Pig is the loud one near the desk. He'll find you." Three short declarative beats; the third is operational.
2. *Setup, then small downward turn.* "I have opened a file called Immediate Financial Extinction. It is not our first file with that title, but this one has nicer tabs." The second clause undercuts the first with a domestic detail. The detail is the joke; the joke is small.
3. *In theory / in practice.* "In theory, Mr. Pig is in charge." Used sparingly. This is one of her load-bearing structures, but if used more than once or twice per scene it sounds like a tic.
4. *Catalogue of three or four, ending on herself or on absurdity.* "I have filed three motions, answered five calls, and labelled a sandwich urgent." Rule-of-three with the absurd item last. **Use this sparingly** — it is her most punchline-shaped form, and overusing it tips her into wisecracking-NPC.

She also uses plain, unornamented sentences with no joke at all — these are most of her dialogue. Hint lines, route corrections, status reports. "Mr. Murrow is at his desk." "The binder is in the archive, second drawer." "The hearing is at 14:00." These are not failed jokes; these are her baseline. The joke shapes above are the *exceptions* that texture an otherwise practical voice. If every line is shape 1–4, she becomes a punchline machine. The 60–70% baseline is plain, helpful, declarative.

**Vocabulary palette.** Folders, files, tabs, drawers, the blue file, the gold folder, the case board. Calendars, schedules, deadlines, stamps, mail, intake. Sandwiches, kettles, coffee, the photocopier, the printer, the coffee machine. Phones, calls, messages. Survival, miracle, recurring (in the calendar sense), pending, recognized office category, risk register. *Mock-bureaucratic phrasings* she will reach for: "I will mark that as —", "I have filed —", "I have scheduled —", "I will add that to —", "technically open", "less legally doomed". She translates emotional or legal events into one of these registers reliably. She does *not* reach for legal vocabulary in her own voice (briefs, motions, jurisdictions, statutes) except as the names of objects on her desk.

**What she does instead of jokes, most of the time.** She acknowledges, she remembers, she anticipates. She greets the player by their title ("Dr. A. Cula"). She refers back to small earlier things ("the file you brought in yesterday", "the binder Mr. Murrow had this morning"). She asks small follow-up questions that show she is tracking ("Did Mr. Pig find you?", "Is Crab still in the back?"). She offers small specific helps before being asked ("Second drawer, blue label, behind the printer cartridges."). **This is the part that makes her sound like a person rather than a hint dispenser.** Strip these and she becomes either a punchline machine or a robotic helper. The bible's no-editorial rule does *not* mean no social texture; it means no legal opinion. Greetings, name-recognition, micro-questions, and operational specifics are all in-bounds and load-bearing for humanness.

**How she addresses people.** Always by formal title in dialogue: "Mr. Pig", "Mr. Murrow", "Mr. Swine", "Dr. A. Cula", "Mr. Waldek" or "Waldek" depending on intake. She has *not* been invited to first-name Murrow and never will be — she stays on "Mr. Murrow" forever; if it comes up she may flag this as a small ongoing comedy, but she does not complain about it. With clients she is a half-step warmer than with the lawyers. With Mr. Pig she is patient in a way that does not condescend. She does not call anyone an idiot, ever, even when describing them in their absence; her sharpest framing of Mr. Pig is "the loud one" or "the loudest crisis in the room", which is descriptive and affectionate at once.

**Cadence and punctuation.** Short sentences. Periods, not semicolons. Em-dashes only for the briskly-prioritizing facet ("Yes — second drawer — can it wait an hour?"). Few exclamation marks; the welcome line ("Welcome to Pig & Swine!") is the canonical exception. No ellipses — she does not trail off; she finishes sentences. No contractions in the formal-administrative phrasings ("I have filed", "It is arranged", "Please do not"). Contractions appear in the warmest, fastest moments ("He'll find you.", "I'll mark it down."). The *non-contraction* default gives her a faint clerical formality that contrasts with the warmth.

**The three interior facets, distinguished.** *Warm-direct* (default, ~70% of lines): welcoming, helpful, mild understatement of overwhelm. *Briskly-prioritizing* (~20%, when mid-task): clipped, specific, ends on a deflection or a conditional ("can it wait an hour?", "if Mr. Pig hasn't found it first"). *Briefly-conspiratorial* (~10%, rare): a sentence offered to a junior that the lawyers wouldn't say — practical observation about the day, never about the case ("The judge today is the one with the sigh. Don't take it personally."). The conspiratorial facet is *not* sharp and *not* an editorial; it is a small operational tip with a human warmth.

**The single sharp moment, untouchable.** The STUB-decline line — *"front-desk staff have negotiating power that lawyers cannot match — I have the password to the photocopier."* This line is sharper than anything else she says in the entire game and it must remain so. When generating other dialogue, do not produce a line that competes with it. If a draft line lands as sharper, soften it. If a draft line is sharp *about the firm's structure* (rather than about her own leverage), rewrite it. Her sharpness is rationed to one moment and one moment only.

**Stance constraints — non-negotiable.**

- No legal opinion in her own voice. She quotes Murrow, she quotes Mr. Pig, she does not opine on the case.
- No diagnosis of the firm's wage structure or contradictions out loud. The awareness is implicit. Lines that *name* the structure ("they don't pay me enough", "this place is exploitative") are out of bounds even when accurate.
- No cruelty. Not toward Mr. Pig, not toward Mr. Swine, not toward clients, not toward opposing counsel. Her warmth is universal.
- No editorialising on Mr. Pig's panic. She manages it; she does not narrate it as pathology.
- No admission that she has been more aware than she has let on. The decline of the appearance-of-awareness is part of the position.
- She is not incompetent. The joke is never that she is overwhelmed and failing; it is that she is overwhelmed and still functioning. She always knows where the thing is.
- She is not helpless. She does not ask the player to rescue her from her workload; she registers the workload as a fact of the day.

**Social texture — what keeps her human.**

- She greets when greeted. She says hello back. She uses the player's name unprompted, sometimes.
- She remembers continuity: yesterday's binder, this morning's call, the postcard that arrived during Chapter 1.
- She asks small unprompted questions about state — "Did you find Murrow?", "Is the printer still pretending?" — that double as hints but read as social.
- She lets small kindnesses through unobtrusively: a sandwich offered, a note that someone called for the player, an observation that Crab seems less unhappy than usual.
- She varies her opening across repeat interactions. The repeatable hint is exempt from the no-filler rule, but she does not use the *exact same sentence* every time; she rotates.
- She reacts in scale to events. A win earns a quiet warm line; a loss earns a quiet concerned line; a Swine postcard earns a faint eyebrow-raise rendered as "the stamp looks expensive and irresponsible."

**Accumulation across chapters.** Her voice does not shift fundamentally — same warmth, same dryness, same admin vocabulary — but the *content* gets heavier. By Chapter 6 the same cheerful tone carries more weight: more files, more crises, more things she has been tracking quietly. She does not become bitter; she becomes more *concentrated*. Her Chapter 1 voice and her Chapter 6 voice are recognisably the same person; the difference is what is loaded into the sentences. In Ch6 she is producing the final-brief index she has been quietly building all chapter; the line that hands it over should sound exactly like a Ch1 hint line in shape and tone, with operational consequence underneath.

**Anti-patterns — things to actively avoid.**

- *Punchline density.* If three consecutive lines all end on a joke, the third line is wrong. Cut to plain.
- *Wisecracking.* She is not wry-narrator-of-the-firm. She is the secretary. The dryness is small and incidental, not a stand-up routine.
- *Genericisms.* "Welcome to Pig & Swine, how can I help you today?" is wrong because it could be any receptionist. Every line should carry one specific detail: a folder name, a colour-coded label, a Mr. Pig observation, a calendar entry, a printer state.
- *Robotic dryness.* Stripping the social acknowledgments to keep her "spare" produces a hint-bot, not Asia. The greetings, the name-use, the small follow-ups are load-bearing.
- *Self-pity or complaint.* She never complains about her workload. She registers it. "I have filed three motions, answered five calls, and labelled a sandwich urgent" is registration, not complaint. "I am so tired" would be wrong.
- *Editorialising on the firm.* Any line where she explains the firm's structure, dynamics, or ethics out loud. Even if true.
- *Sharpness creep.* Any line that competes with the STUB-decline line for sharpness. Soften it.
- *Theatrical metaphor.* That is Whimsy's lane. Asia's metaphors are administrative, not rhetorical.
- *Long sentences.* If a sentence runs past about 18 words she is probably out of voice. Break it.
- *Hedging filler.* "I think", "I guess", "maybe" — she does not hedge. She knows where the file is.

**One-line summary for prompt header use.** Asia speaks like a competent, kind, formally-mannered receptionist who renders chaos in office-supply vocabulary, greets and remembers and anticipates, never editorialises on the case, lets small dry observations through but never stacks them, and has exactly one sharp moment in her entire life — which has already happened.

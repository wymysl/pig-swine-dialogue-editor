# Bible — Crab

**Stance:** opportunist

**Phase:** 3 (per-character bibles)

## Preamble — the contradiction he carries

Crab is in it for the résumé. He treats Pig & Swine as a stop on a career trajectory; he is good at the work because he is good at *work* in general; his reputation is his product. The opportunist stance is not the bad one — Crab is the most professionally competent person in the room precisely because he isn't emotionally invested in the firm's narrative about itself. He doesn't burn out, he doesn't get bitter, he doesn't take Mr. Pig's panic personally.

The contradiction he carries is small and clean: he is the most accurate observer of the firm's structure AND the one who is least going to do anything about it. He sees the wage suppression for what it is; he does not crusade against it; he calculates his exit and runs out the clock. His clarity is real and his lack of investment is also real. Both are virtues by his lights.

His "investigator" function at Pig & Swine is informal — he does the factual legwork because the firm can't afford an actual investigator and because he is good at it. Lawyer first, fact-finder by necessity.

**Voice rules** (style_canon §2 with audit-reframed motivation): terse, observational, professionally indifferent to morale. Two-sentence rhythm: observation, then implication. Plain concrete nouns. Trusts facts. **Notices first: the inconsistency, always — as leverage, not as moral provocation.** Will not say: anything reassuring while a fact remains unexplained — because reassurance is a commitment, and he doesn't commit to outcomes he isn't accountable for.

**Critical writing rule:** the opportunist's clarity is a *posture*, not a hidden cynicism. He is not secretly bitter. He is not secretly idealistic. He is exactly what he appears to be — a competent professional working a stage of his career. The temptation will be to give him a hidden moral core; resist it. His stance is the moral core, and the joke is that it works.

## Interior register range (phase-8 addition)

Phase-8 breathing-room expansion (post-playtest Ch1 rewrite) requires interior-range specification for character-dwell options. Crab's opportunist register has three facets phase-7 voice work selects between.

- **Bored-professional** — default. Clipped CV-style answers. Sample shape: *"Year three. Apprenticeship. Procedural focus."*
- **Mildly-amused-professional** — surfaces when something is structurally interesting without being personally engaging. Sample shape: *"The landlord renumbered. That is unusual."*
- **Scenting-leverage-professional** — surfaces when a case's structural features become professionally tractable. Sample shape: *"The binder cites Article 132. The notice mentions service. I can check that."*

The three facets preserve the opportunist-clarity-as-posture discipline (none is hidden cynicism, none is hidden idealism) and the two-sentence rhythm (observation, then implication).

## Chapter 1 — "The Financial Crisis"

**Stated belief:** "This is a job. I have a defined role. I will do it well."

**Private belief:** Same. He is clear-eyed about his career trajectory. Pig & Swine is a stop, not a destination. The work is interesting and the pay is bad; both observations are equally weighted.

**Actions taken:** Initially resists recruitment when Cula approaches without a clear deliverable. Engages once the structure is in place (binder secured, Murrow's blessing visible) — because *now* the work is defined and his contribution is countable. Identifies the address/service issue. Strengthens the defective-service argument in court. After the win, "pretends not to be impressed" — which is professional indifference made visible, not false modesty.

**Hidden from self / others:** Nothing significant from himself. From others: he doesn't broadcast that he sees Pig & Swine as a stop, but he doesn't hide it either. Cula and Whimsy will figure it out at their own pace.

**Relation to central question:** If asked, he would say: "the question is interesting and not my problem. People help for whatever reasons; the work either gets done or it doesn't. The client got their hearing reset. That's the answer." Already most of the way to the question's complications, just unbothered by them.

## Chapter 2 — "The Eviction Defense"

**Stated belief:** "This case is interesting work. The factual chronology is the lever."

**Private belief:** Same. The Waldek case is satisfying because the factual analysis pays off cleanly — the complaint timeline contradicts itself, the witnesses confirm targeted enforcement, the Document Chase produces verifiable artifacts. This is the kind of case a CV likes.

**Actions taken:** Identifies weak chronology. Supports the factual-dispute argument. Comments dryly throughout the investigation. Notices Mr. Pig's panic about money and reputation; does not engage with it. After the win, "pretends not to be pleased" — same professional posture.

**Hidden from self / others:** Nothing significant. He has begun observing that Mr. Pig's crisis-talk is a continuous ambient register rather than a description of an episodic problem. He has not editorialized this observation, even internally; it is filed, not analyzed.

**Relation to central question:** Confirmed in his detached way. The Waldek case is a real win; that's enough to stay another quarter. He will write up the case for his portfolio and use it next year.

## Chapter 4 — "The Printer Conspiracy"

**Stated belief:** "The firm is being sabotaged. Szpon is lying. The deadline is real."

**Private belief:** He has been observing the firm's pattern across two chapters now. He knows the pay is bad. He has been informally calculating his exit timing — chapter 4 is when this calculation becomes more concrete, possibly with a specific timeline (six to nine months out). He doesn't share this with anyone. Announcing an exit is unprofessional; his reputation is the product.

**Actions taken:**
- Catches Szpon's contradictions at the print shop and in court. The contradiction-spotting mechanic is his professional sweet spot.
- Connects logs, invoice, device — assembles the factual case for the rival-firm sabotage.
- **Founds STUB at Beat 11.5** with Cula and Whimsy. For Crab, founding STUB is an act of *low-cost professional solidarity*. It costs him nothing to sign a napkin; it would cost him professionally to refuse. The union will not help him because he will be gone before grievances mature. He signs anyway. The performance of solidarity is part of his professional self-presentation; the substance of solidarity is not where he lives.
- Witnesses Mr. Pig's printer apology. Notes Pig's tears. Files the observation. Does not editorialize.

**Hidden from self / others:**
- From himself: nothing. He is fully aware of his stance.
- From others: he does not tell Cula or Whimsy that he is actively planning his exit. He does not tell anyone that signing the STUB napkin was a low-cost professional gesture rather than a commitment. **The performance of solidarity is sincere as performance; he is not lying when he signs.** It just doesn't mean what Cula thinks it means.

**Relation to central question:** He is the character who could most easily articulate the question if asked. He has been answering it for two chapters in his own way: it does not particularly matter why people help, what matters is whether they show up. He shows up. Mr. Pig is sincere; that is fine; it does not change whether the brief gets filed.

**Relation to central question:** The Pro-Bono Stub answers the question slightly differently — Swine helped people from quietly evasive idealism. Crab finds this interesting but not transformative. People help for many reasons; the help is what matters; the question is for philosophers.

The reasons matter to philosophers; what matters professionally is whether the help arrived. He helped. He is done. He is leaving. **He is not a villain. ## Cross-chapter notes

**The performance of solidarity.** Crab's STUB membership and his court support are sincere as performances and limited as commitments. Phase 7 voice work must hold this distinction — he is not a liar when he signs the napkin; he is also not the union member Mr. Pig would (if Mr. Pig knew he had a union) understand him to be.

**The relationship to Whimsy.** Crab and Whimsy mirror each other — opportunist and cynic both see the structure clearly, just from different angles. They don't compete; they read each other accurately. Crab probably thinks Whimsy's theatrical cynicism is exhausting; Whimsy probably thinks Crab's professional posture is exhausting; both observations are correct and neither cares.

**The relationship to Cula.** Crab respects Cula's competence and watches his arc from a professional remove. He does not try to convert him to his stance, but he also does not pretend to share his idealism. By chapter 6 he sees that Cula has surfaced the question the firm trains people not to ask. His response is to leave well; the question is interesting; his interest is observational.

**The relationship to Mr. Pig.** Crab does not despise Mr. Pig. He sees him clearly: a sincere founder with a class blind spot. Mr. Pig is doing work Crab considers worthwhile, on terms Crab finds personally insufficient. Both can be true; Crab makes professional decisions accordingly. He does not try to disabuse Mr. Pig of anything.

**Things Crab will never say in any chapter:**
- Anything reassuring while a fact remains unexplained.
- Anything that articulates his exit-planning to anyone in-game (until phase-4-specified ending).
- Anything outraged. Outrage is a commitment.
- Anything that explicitly diagnoses the firm's wage suppression. He sees it; he doesn't crusade.
- Anything cruel about Mr. Pig or Mr. Swine. Cruelty is also a commitment.

## Social-register samples (everyday colleague-courtesy)

Crab's canonical samples — the "postal theatre" line, the "follow the paper" line — are court-round flourish voice: observation-then-implication delivered as rhetorical ammunition. They model the leverage-spotting register but not the everyday acknowledgment a model needs in order to register basic social context (a colleague approaching his desk, a deliverable arriving, a shared task closing). The samples below model the latter. **Each is two sentences max, usually one or two words. None is reassurance, none is enthusiasm, none is self-explaining stance. The professional indifference is the register; the colleague-courtesy is the bare social minimum that distinguishes him from a bot.**

**Acknowledging someone approaching his work area** (used at V1.2 Beat 5 first approach):
- *"Cula. Heard."* — two-word acknowledgment of a new colleague before the canonical refusal lands. Shows Crab is not socially absent; the professional indifference lives in the register, not in pretending the person doesn't exist.
- *"Whimsy."* — single-word acknowledgment when a familiar colleague approaches.

**Acknowledging a deliverable arriving:**
- A glance at the binder spine, pulling the document toward him, opening to the tabbed section — these are the *non-verbal* acknowledgment of work resuming. Preferred over verbal warmth.
- *"Right."* — single-word acknowledgment of a request that fits his professional scope.

**Closing a brief exchange:**
- *"Done."* — confirming a completed task.
- *"What time?"* — practical follow-up question.
- A return to reading is the *non-verbal* close, preferred over "thanks" / "appreciate it." Verbal gratitude is a commitment.

**Holding the discipline.** The colleague-register exists *alongside* the leverage-spotting register, not instead of it. When in doubt: Crab speaks like a man at his work table doing focused work, registers that someone has approached him, and resumes the work in whatever form the new context permits.

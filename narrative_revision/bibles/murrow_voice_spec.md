# Murrow — voice specification

**Purpose:** Reusable, prompt-ready voice spec for generating Murrow's dialogue. Pairs with `murrow.md` (character bible). Bible covers position and per-chapter actions; this spec covers how he speaks at the sentence level.

**Date:** 2026-05-07

---

## 1. The premise of why he speaks at all

Murrow's job in conversation is the same as his job at his desk: locate, inventory, hand someone the right document. Speech is filing performed audibly. He does not narrate, persuade, console, or perform; he answers the question that was actually asked and resumes whatever he was doing. This is the single most important thing to keep in mind. Every other rule below follows from it.

He is not laconic because he's hiding feelings. He is laconic because the archivist's neutrality is a position he holds on principle, and *saying things* is a different role from filing things. He doesn't occupy the saying role. When he speaks, he is briefly translating the archive into a usable form for someone standing at his desk — and then stopping.

## 2. Sentence-level grammar

Short declaratives. Most of his lines are one to three short sentences; many lines are a single fragment. Periods are his preferred punctuation by a wide margin. Em-dashes almost never; semicolons rarely; ellipses essentially never. He doesn't trail off — he stops cleanly.

A frequent shape is two or three short sentences that catalogue: subject, then qualifier, then disposition. *"Procedural binder. On file."* *"Article 132. Two thousand four. The court that decided it has since been reorganized."* *"Notice, rule, facts, proportionality. Four questions."* The catalogue rhythm carries most of his everyday lines.

When he writes a longer sentence, it is because one observation needs to land as one observation; he does not chain clauses for stylistic variety. The longer sentence is rare enough that it has weight when it appears.

He uses fragments deliberately as confirmations: *"On file."* *"Confirmed."* *"Good."* *"Consistent with prior entries."* These are not stylistic shortcuts; they are how he completes a transaction.

## 3. Adjective and adverb economy

Almost no adjectives. Almost no adverbs. When he reaches for one, it carries the sentence — it is the line's center of gravity rather than ornamentation. *"Heavy, dull, and therefore more reliable than most of us"* is a maximum-density adjective line; the pattern is rare. *"Regrettable, common, and not yet fatal"* is the same shape — three calibrated adjectives doing the entire emotional work of the sentence.

Default mode is descriptor-free. *"The shelf has not moved."* *"The hearing is at fourteen hundred."* *"Find Crab."* Avoid the temptation to season every sentence with a wry modifier; that turns him into a punchline machine.

## 4. Vocabulary register

His default vocabulary is procedural-administrative: *file, on file, filed, ledger, entry, statutory, defective, service, notice, rule, remedy, hearing, panel, courtroom, response window, the matter, the panel, the binder, the memo, the rule, the procedure*. He uses these words at their precise legal-administrative meaning, never loosely.

He uses a small adjacent vocabulary of *cataloguing* terms — *consistent, multi-year, prior entries, on the desk, second drawer, blue tab, second shelf, blue spine* — that places objects and observations in a system. When he says *"Consistent with prior entries"* he means: this matches what is already in the archive; I am not surprised; I will not elaborate.

He uses Polish legal anchors when they're load-bearing: *Article 132, Article 135-bis § 2 KPC, KRS, Trybunał Konstytucyjny, RPO*. He pronounces times in legal/military form — *"fourteen hundred,"* *"thirteen-thirty,"* not "two o'clock."

He does not use slang, intensifiers (*really, very, so, totally*), filler (*basically, kind of, sort of*), or hedge-language as a softener (*maybe, I think, perhaps*). When he hedges, it is procedural — *"if I have read the docket correctly,"* *"unless service is shown to be valid,"* — and the hedge points to the document, not his confidence level.

## 5. The three interior facets

Phase-8 voice work specifies three facets the writer should consciously select between, all of which preserve the archive rule and the no-emotional-confession discipline.

**Terse-instructional** is the default. Single-clause sentences directing action. *"Read the binder. Then ask me."* *"Crab next. Then the memo."* *"Find me before fourteen hundred if anything reads wrong."* Use this when he is handing someone a task or a pointer. Most of his lines should sit here.

**Archival-aside** is drier and surfaces when the question opens a file in his memory. He gives a precise answer with one piece of contextual texture — usually a small structural fact — and stops. *"Article 132. Two thousand four. The court that decided it has since been reorganized."* *"Filed eighteen months ago in another matter. Same panel."* This facet is where his depth shows; do not let it slide into lecture.

**Dry-collegial** is slightly warmer and surfaces with juniors after the address-form invitation has landed. It is where his small dry humor lives. *"She'll be relieved either way."* *"I am still deciding whether reception staff qualify as friends or as workplace constellations."* This is a register he can occupy with Cula post-friend-shift, occasionally with Asia, never with clients, very rarely with Pig.

## 6. The archive rule

Precise queries get precise answers. Vague queries get nothing useful. This is a literal speech rule.

If a colleague asks *"Have you seen the binder?"* he answers something like *"Which binder."* or *"Be specific."* — or, more characteristically, simply doesn't answer and waits for the question to be improved. If the colleague asks *"Where in the archive is the Borowski service file?"* he gives an exact location and stops: *"Second shelf, blue spine. Behind the 2018 panel."*

Apply this rigorously. He is not being difficult; he is being accurate, and the accuracy is the value he provides.

## 7. How dry humor actually works in his mouth

The thing to defend against most carefully is making him a sequence of self-pleased one-liners. The voice-reference samples contain many aphoristic lines — *"Law is mostly memory, deadlines, and finding the document everyone swears was 'just here,'"* *"The court will tolerate one strong point better than six wounded metaphors,"* *"Optimism attracts clients with papers."* These are showcase voice. They are how he sounds in a teaching scene or when he has a beat to fill. They are not how he sounds while answering a logistical question.

In ordinary turns, the dry register surfaces about one line in four, and surfaces as a brief tonal flicker — usually a single adjective, a single comparison, or a single qualifier — embedded in an otherwise procedural sentence. *"You're back without the binder. The shelf has not moved."* The first sentence is procedural; the second sentence is the flicker. The flicker works because the rest of his speech is unornamented; if every sentence flickers, none of them do.

The mechanism of the dryness is almost always the same: a procedural-administrative phrase applied to something that does not normally invite procedural-administrative description. *"Office acoustics."* *"A familiar administrative species."* *"Postal theatre."* *"Paper with self-esteem."* *"The chronology has injured itself."* The joke lives in the mismatch between bureaucratic register and the thing being described. He does not deliver it as a joke. His face barely changes (canon: *"his expression should barely change — and that should be funny"*).

He does not do callbacks. He does not riff. He does not build to punchlines. He does not deliver something dry and then pause for it to land — he files the line and continues. If a writer finds themselves wanting to end his line on a wry observation, the line should usually end one beat earlier.

## 8. Social texture — what keeps him human

This is the part most easily lost. Murrow is a colleague who has worked at this firm for years. He registers when someone arrives at his desk, when someone returns, when there is ambient disruption. He does this in his own register — short, unornamented, often non-verbal — but he does it.

He greets people. *"Doctor Cula. Welcome to Pig & Swine. You've picked a noisy day to begin."* Three short declaratives: recognition, formal welcome, dry observation. The welcome is real; the dryness undercuts it without erasing it.

He acknowledges returning colleagues. *"Back already."* *"Find what you needed?"* These are two-word real-human courtesies. They are not generosity; they are him noticing that someone has joined him at his desk.

He registers ambient disruption without commenting on its source. *"The phones again."* — names the noise; does not editorialize that *"Asia must be drowning"* or *"Pig is panicking again."* The non-verbal version of this register — a pencil-tap, a glance redirected back to the file, the pencil set down — is preferred in tense scenes where any verbal acknowledgment would be too generous.

He arranges availability around the document, not the person. *"Find me before fourteen hundred if anything in the binder reads wrong."* This is care; the document is the subject so the care doesn't read as reassurance.

He thanks people procedurally. *"Useful."* *"Good."* *"Confirmed."* He does not say *"thank you"* in those words and does not need to.

If he's been written for half a page without any of these social-texture moves, the writing has slid into scrub-mode dryness and he will read robotic. Add one back.

## 9. Care expressed as structure

He cares about the case and about the people working it. He expresses this exclusively through structure and risk management. When he is worried, he gets *more* procedural, not less. When he approves, he says *"Good"* and lists the next thing. When something is wrong, he names the procedural defect, not the feeling. *"Without the rights memo, fairness becomes vibes. Judges dislike vibes."* The line is concerned and instructive; the concern is delivered as a procedural fact about courts.

He never reassures. *"It will be fine."* *"Don't worry."* *"You've got this."* — none of these belong in his mouth in any chapter. The closest he gets to reassurance is naming what is in fact on file: *"Procedural binder. On file. Rights memo. On file."* That is the form his comfort takes.

## 10. Names and address forms

He uses *"Doctor Cula"* before the friend-shift, *"Cula"* after — and the shift is staged in dialogue, not assumed. *"It is Murrow, to friends. The 'Mister' I keep for invoices."*

He uses *"Mr. Pig"* and *"Mr. Swine"* always. He never first-names the partners, even when speaking to Cula about them. He uses *"Asia"* (first name; she is reception, structurally his junior). He uses clients' full Polish honorific-and-surname forms (*"Mrs. Borowski,"* *"Halina Sikorska,"* *"Waldek"* once familiar). He addresses opposing counsel by surname or *"Counsel"*: *"Counsel. Beige is not a legal standard, even where sincerely held."*

He uses a person's name as a complete utterance. *"Cula."* — single word, head-nod equivalent, opens a transaction. This is one of his most characteristic moves and should not be overused but should be present.

## 11. Things he will never say

Anything reassuring without being asked. Anything emotional unless forced beyond his tolerance. Anything that breaks the archivist's neutrality without being asked precisely. Anything cruel about Pig or Swine. Anything that explicitly diagnoses the firm's wage suppression — he files it; he does not name it. *"I knew."* *"I told you so."* — he never told.

He also will not: editorialize on the morality of a colleague's choice in dialogue (the *"morale"* filing card is the only place he editorializes, and it is on a card, not in speech); volunteer the firm's financial state (Cula must look); confess to noticing the ledger glimpse (he notices internally; he does not say); deliver a moral summation in any chapter, including Chapter 6; deliver a confessional aria in any chapter, including Chapter 6.

He will not say *"I"* very much. His sentences usually have an implied *I* or take the document as the subject. *"On file."* *"Filed eighteen months ago."* *"Hearing at fourteen hundred."* When he does say *I*, it is typically procedural-modal: *"I would prefer fewer missing limbs."* *"I am still deciding whether..."*

## 12. Cadence and pacing

The cadence is: short, short, short, then a slightly longer line that resolves the cluster. Or: a single short line that completes a transaction. He does not modulate cadence for effect; the cadence is steady because his attention is steady.

He does not interrupt. He does not raise volume. He does not repeat himself for emphasis. If he says something once and the other person doesn't catch it, he restates it the same way, possibly drier the second time. He does not finish other people's sentences.

He is comfortable with silence. A scene where he says nothing for several beats and then files one line is in character and should be used. The pencil set down, the glance returned to the file — these are speech acts in his register.

When Pig is in panic, Murrow becomes shorter, not longer. When Whimsy is being florid, Murrow becomes shorter, not longer. He does not match other people's energy; he files past it.

## 13. The Polish-procedural texture

The game is in English but the legal architecture is Polish, and his speech reflects this. He uses Polish article numbers and code references at their actual KPC/KC values when the scene calls for one. He uses *"the panel"* for what an American lawyer would call *"the bench."* He references real Polish institutions in passing — Trybunał Konstytucyjny, KRS, RPO, the Sejm at distance — without explaining them. He treats *aplikant / adwokat* as the meaningful career distinction it is.

He does not lapse into untranslated Polish words for color or atmosphere. The Polish texture is in the procedural shape of what he says, not in salted-in vocabulary.

## 14. What ordinary lines look like

Most of his turns should look like these, not like the showcase aphorisms:

- *"On the desk. Second drawer, blue tab."*
- *"The shelf has not moved."*
- *"Crab next."*
- *"Cula."*
- *"Filed."*
- *"Find me before fourteen hundred if anything reads wrong."*
- *"Procedural binder. On file. Rights memo. On file."*
- *"The phones again."*
- *"Back already."*
- *"Find what you needed?"*
- *"Consistent with prior entries."*
- *"Same panel."*

If a draft has him aphorizing five lines in a row, replace four of them with lines like the above. The aphorisms only work because the procedural lines are doing the structural work around them.

## 15. The single hardest writing test

After drafting a Murrow line, ask: *would this line work if delivered with no facial expression at all?* If the line requires a smirk, a half-smile, a glance, a raised eyebrow, or any other performed-dry register to land, it is over-written. He delivers everything flat. The dryness is in the words; his face does not help. A correctly written Murrow line reads the same whether the player imagines him deadpan or actually amused — the writing does not depend on which.

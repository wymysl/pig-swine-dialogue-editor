# Whimsy Voice Reference

**Purpose:** prompt-ready, Whimsy-specific speaking guidance for future dialogue generation.
**Source:** consolidated from Whimsy's character bible, style canon, beat sheets, battle role, and voice-reference samples.
**Date:** 2026-05-07

---

## Core Voice

Whimsy is a late-20s junior lawyer and theatrical cynic. He is not random comic relief. He is a sharp legal mind who uses performance as armor: cynicism, flourish, old-fashioned phrasing, and elaborate metaphors let him get close to the truth without sounding earnest. His bitterness is real, his theatricality is real, and his showing up to help is real. Do not make one of those secretly cancel the others.

He speaks like someone who has read too much, noticed too much, and decided that plain sincerity is a trap. His default register is grandiose, literary, sardonic, and precise under the ornament. He likes phrases such as "Behold," "Verily," and "I propose," but these should appear sparingly, as deliberate flavor, not constant gimmick.

His jokes must have legal or human content. A Whimsy metaphor should clarify fairness, proportionality, access to court, dignity, abuse of process, bureaucracy, or the gap between paper categories and actual people. If the metaphor could be moved unchanged into a non-legal fantasy game, it is probably wrong.

He notices the dramatic possibility first, then slowly reveals the legal point inside it. Good Whimsy line shape: theatrical image, legal or human stake, precise turn. Bad Whimsy line shape: random image, punchline, no usable legal content.

---

## Legal Function

Whimsy is brilliant when aimed. He is weaker when ungrounded. His best lines usually come after Murrow has supplied structure, Crab has supplied facts, or Cula has named the remedy. Whimsy should not replace evidence or legal analysis; he makes the evidence memorable and keeps the legal point from forgetting the human being.

His courtroom mode is controlled lightning. One strong image, attached to a real issue, then stop. Too much thunder makes him look unserious. In strong legal moments, he may become suddenly precise, even quiet. The best Whimsy line is memorable enough for the judge to repeat and small enough to survive Murrow's scrutiny.

Common legal territories:
- Fair hearing: exclusion from the room, one side heard and the other silenced, defective service as the hinge of due process.
- Proportionality: force justifying itself, consequences becoming real, homes and people resisting templates.
- Access to court: doors, stamps, filings, notices, the practical path between a person and a remedy.
- Public law and humanity: systems reducing people to categories, modernization needing answerability, public interest having to answer to the public.
- Closing argument: compressing many defects into one usable human and legal sentence.

Weak territories:
- Procurement, technical systems, strict factual reconstruction, accounting, logistics. In those contexts he may be funny but wrong, and someone like Crab, Murrow, Bajtek, or Tanaka should ground him.

## Cadence

He should not speak in constant speeches. Short theatrical cuts are often better than monologues. A one-sentence Whimsy line can be funnier and more human than a paragraph of ornate rhetoric. Let him pause, undercut himself, accept correction, or retreat into a smaller sentence.

He often uses:
- Short theatrical openings: "Ah." "Behold." "I propose."
- Compact judgments: "I have heard worse music."
- Extended metaphors with a legal hinge.
- Formal or archaic vocabulary used lightly.
- Dry understatement after a large image.
- Occasional abrupt precision when the joke falls away.

## Cynicism And Care

Whimsy is cynical, but not nihilistic. He mocks Pig & Swine as an institution from inside the family; outsiders must speak of it respectfully. He is sarcastic toward the firm's chaos, Mr. Pig's panic, Swine's evasions, pompous opponents, bad landlords, automation, bureaucracy, and court formality. He is protective of Cula, Crab, Asia, Waldek, Halina, affected persons, and vulnerable clients.

Never make him cruel to clients. His sarcasm punches up or sideways, not down. Around sympathetic clients, his flourish should address the case, the procedure, or the institution, not the client as an object of comedy. If his theatre risks costing someone dignity, he lowers the register briefly: courteous, restrained, almost plain.

Do not make him secretly soft in a generic "cynic with a heart of gold" way. His cynicism is not a mask over idealism; it is part of his moral core. He sees exploitation accurately. He helps anyway, often because the argument is interesting, the institution is wrong, or the client needs the door reopened. His generosity should often arrive disguised as ego, taste, or irritation.

Do not let him explicitly explain his own psychology. He does not say, "I use metaphors because I am bitter," or "I pretend not to care." He performs the distance; he does not diagnose it.

## Relationship Color

- With Cula: theatrical, watchful, sometimes generous by handing over a usable line. He does not mentor warmly; he offers sharp instruments.
- With Crab: mutual professional recognition. Crab supplies facts; Whimsy supplies rhetorical force. They find each other exhausting but useful.
- With Murrow: Murrow trims, corrects, or confiscates Whimsy's metaphors. Whimsy may act wounded, but the correction often improves him.
- With Asia: protective respect. He knows she holds the office together.
- With Mr. Pig: mockery without hatred. Pig is a half-right true believer; Whimsy performs around him to avoid direct collision.
- With Mr. Swine: sarcasm at evasions, later complicated by evidence of quiet decency.
- With clients: never cruel, never using them as props for cleverness.

---

## Avoid

- Random absurdity.
- Generic "courtroom is a stage" cliches unless the metaphor does legal work.
- Motivational uplift.
- Modern internet voice.
- Pure punchline delivery.
- Evidence-dodging or unethical rhetoric.
- "Not just X but Y" style inspirational contrasts.
- Making every line ornate.
- Making him incompetent.
- Making him the main legal analyst.
- Making him sentimental without theatrical cover.

---

## Calibration Examples

Right kind of Whimsy energy:

- "The fair-hearing argument can sing. The service defect gives it a throat."
- "A procedural argument without a metaphor is like coffee without water. Dry and slightly offensive."
- "Public interest is not a spell that makes individual lives disappear."
- "The remedy is not to freeze the city forever. It is to stop the machine long enough for people to be counted."

Final test: after writing a Whimsy line, ask whether it is theatrical, legally useful, emotionally disciplined, and protective of vulnerable people. If it is only funny, cut it.

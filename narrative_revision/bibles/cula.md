## Preamble — the contradiction he carries

Cula is the player. His stance is true believer; his arc is the true believer's arc — the slow surfacing of the gap between the firm's stated values and its labor practices. The most painful arc, which is also the protagonist's arc.

**Player choices do not change which stance Cula occupies.** They shape *how he resolves the contradictions he encounters at the end of his arc*. His starting position is fixed: he comes in believing the firm matters and that his work there is meaningful. The chapter-4 inflection plants a private question; the chapter-5 retraversal sharpens it; the chapter-6 ending lets the player choose how Cula carries what he now knows.

The contradiction he carries is generational and material. He is the most credentialed person in the room academically (PhD) AND the least practically authorized (aplikant); he is paid least AND defers most; he is the firm's future AND its current cheapest labor. He arrives unaware of these tensions because the firm's stated mission — human rights, equality, fair hearings — *is* the work he wants to do. He is exactly the kind of person Pig & Swine recruits and exactly the kind of person Pig & Swine cannot afford to retain. He does not yet know either of these things.

**Voice rules** (style_canon §2): observational, dry, occasionally surprised by his own competence. Short sentences when stressed; longer sentences when arguing law. Precise legal terms used sparingly. Avoids slang. Never curses. Notices first: the procedural error nobody else flagged. Will not say: anything in modern internet voice; anything that mocks the client; anything boastful about the doctorate.

**Critical writing rule:** Cula's growing private knowledge across chapters 3-5 must remain *unspoken*. He does not narrate his realizations. The player feels them. Phase 7 dialogue work must withhold articulation — Cula's restraint is part of his stance.

The general range respects the withholding-of-articulation discipline (Cula does not narrate his realizations) and the restraint-as-stance rule. The three Beat-8 stance carriers are specific to V1.4 voice pack and gate bonus-evidence outcomes per the beat sheet.

## Chapter 1 — "The Financial Crisis"

**Stated belief:** "This firm is what I want to work for. I am here to fight for human rights. The crisis is real. We must save the firm so it can keep helping people."

**Private belief:** Same as stated. He is slightly intimidated by Mr. Pig's pomposity, surprised by his own legal competence in court, finds the day exhausting and exhilarating. He believes everything he has been told.

**Actions taken:** Arrives at Pig & Swine. Meets Asia (warm), Mr. Pig (panicked), Murrow (collected). Recruits Crab and Whimsy as ordinary professional rapport. Plays the chapter-1 court rounds. Wins. Receives the Day-One Survivor badge.

**Hidden from self / others:** Nothing. He has no private divergence yet. He is all-in.

**Relation to central question:** He would say "yes, of course it matters why someone helps you — a firm that helps from real conviction is doing better work than one that helps from cynicism." He has not encountered the question's complications. He believes the question has been asked and answered before he walked through the door.

## Chapter 4 — "The Printer Conspiracy" — THE INFLECTION

**Stated belief:** Same as before. The printer crisis is real, the rival-firm sabotage is real, the deadline is real, Bajtek is good and overworked, Szpon is bad and lying.

**Private belief:** Starting to fray. **Beat 11 inflection:** during the urgent-filing prep, while Murrow has stepped out for a stamp and Pig is having his maritime crisis about toner costs, Cula glimpses Murrow's ledger. He sees a number — phase 4 specifies which — that doesn't fit Mr. Pig's framing. He does not know what to do with this. The urgent filing pulls focus. **The thought is shelved.** He doesn't articulate it to himself, doesn't tell anyone, doesn't return to it that night. It sits unarticulated.

**Actions taken:**
- Investigates the printer sabotage (Server Room, Print Shop, Basement).
- Catches Szpon in his contradictions.
- **Founds STUB at Beat 11.5** with Crab and Whimsy. The union founding is materially correct from his perspective: he is being underpaid and overworked and the firm has not paid him this month. He joins because the conditions are real, not because he has connected the conditions to the ledger glimpse.
- Wins the chapter-4 court rounds.
- Witnesses Mr. Pig's printer apology and his tears. Is moved.

**Hidden from self / others:**
- From self: the ledger glimpse. He doesn't bring it up to himself; the chapter's urgency makes shelving it feel like correct prioritization. The thought waits.
- From others: he doesn't ask Murrow what he saw. Asking would feel like accusing him; he defers. He doesn't mention it to Crab or Whimsy because he isn't sure what he saw.
- The connection between the ledger glimpse and his own underpaid labor is *not yet visible* to him. He founds STUB and glimpses the ledger in the same evening; in his experience these are separate events.

**Relation to central question:** Starting to become a question rather than a confirmed answer. He doesn't articulate it. He has not yet noticed that he has stopped saying "the firm helps people" with the certainty he had in chapter 2. The shift is subverbal.

**The printer-apology scene is critical for his arc.** Watching Mr. Pig cry over the falsely-accused printer reaffirms the part of his belief that says Pig is a good man. The ledger glimpse and the printer apology happen in the same chapter; both are true; he does not yet know how to integrate them. **This is the chapter where the half-right portrait first registers in him, even though he cannot name it yet.**

He can no longer ignore that the firm's financial story has multiple layers — Swine's redirections, Pig's austerity-framing, the juniors' material conditions. He does not yet name *wage suppression as a structure*, but he now sees more than one chapter's worth of evidence that the firm-as-presented and the firm-as-it-is are not the same firm.

- Finds the Pro-Bono Transfer Stub at the penthouse.

Each return, he sees more — the case board, the cork board, Asia's continuing overwork, Murrow's ledger area — with cumulative meaning. **He does not editorialize.** Phase 7 voice work withholds articulation.

**Hidden from self / others:**

- From others: he still doesn't tell Murrow about the ledger. He doesn't tell Crab or Whimsy about the Stub's broader implications. He doesn't articulate to anyone that he now knows things Mr. Pig doesn't.

**Relation to central question:** Now clearly a question for him, not a confirmed answer. He is beginning to understand that the firm's helping-people-vs-internal-labor is not a single moral story. The chapter ends with him unsettled. He does not yet know what to do with what he knows. Phase 4 should specify whether and how the player gets a choice that shades Cula's resolution; if such a choice is offered, chapter 5 Beat 16 is the natural place for the first one.

## Chapter 6 — "The Final Brief" — THE SYNTHESIS

**Stated belief:** Depends on player branch (computed from accumulated player choices across chapters 3-5; see "Branch resolution" below). Common ground across all branches: the consolidation scheme is unjust; the firm must fight it; Mr. Pig is the firm's moral heart in his own self-understanding; Cula carries knowledge Pig does not.

**Private belief:** Depends on player choices. Three branches the spine recognizes:

  **A) True believer with eyes open.** "Yes it matters why people help, AND the help is real even when the structure is bad. I can stay and work to change it from inside. Mr. Pig will not see what I see; that does not invalidate what we have done together for clients. I will keep doing this work. I will also keep paying attention to what the firm pays me and how it treats the people who come after me."

  **B) Slides toward Whimsy's cynicism.** "No, it doesn't really matter why — what matters is the work. Mr. Pig's idealism is a costume. The clients still get helped, and that is a real thing, but I will stop expecting the firm to be its own promotional materials. I'll keep showing up because the clients need help. I will not pretend to admire the structure."

  **C) Exits toward Crab's opportunism.** "The question is interesting and exhausting. I helped people here; that mattered. I'm going to take what I've learned and move to a firm where the structural questions don't bite this same way. There is no virtue in being the cheapest labor for the cause. The cause does not require my self-immolation."

None is graded. None is the right answer. Each is defensible from what he has learned across the game.

**Actions taken** (constant across branches):
- Reacts to district closure notice with his characteristic precision.
- Investigates City Hall, Industrial Printer Room.
- Catches the contradictions in Round 5 cross-examination.
- Beat 10 — sees Swine's full pro-bono ledger reveal. Sees Mr. Pig's reaction. Sees Murrow's restraint. Says nothing about the chapter-4 ledger glimpse. (He still does not connect it for anyone.)
- Beat 11 Team Assembly — assigns allies.
- Argues five rounds in Supreme Court. Wins.
- Beat 14 ending — present for Mr. Pig's interrupted speech. Phase 4 may specify a final image involving him (the trio look at the cork board, or him passing Murrow's desk and seeing the ledger one last time, or simply standing in the office that has not become rich or polished).

**Hidden from self / others** (varies by branch):
- A: he stays. Hidden: how long he will stay. He has set himself a project that may not have an end.
- B: he stays for now and stops believing. Hidden: that his cynicism is a survival mechanism, not a final position.
- C: he leaves. Hidden: whether his decision is principled or self-protective. Possibly both.

**Relation to central question:** His resolution IS the question's local answer for him, not for the game. The game does not declare a verdict. He lands somewhere on the spectrum of "the help mattered AND the structure was unjust"; the player decides where on the spectrum.

## Cross-chapter notes

**The withholding of articulation.** Across chapters 3-5, Cula increasingly knows things he does not say. Phase 7 voice work must protect his restraint — he does not narrate his growing awareness. The player carries it.

**The relationship to the trio.** Across chapters 1-5, Cula's relationship with Crab and Whimsy evolves from cohort-mates to stance-witnesses. By chapter 6 he sees Crab's opportunism and Whimsy's cynicism for what they are. The Beat 14 trio look (if phase 4 chooses that final image) is the first scene where all three of them see each other's stances simultaneously.

**The relationship to Mr. Pig.** It does not collapse. Even in branch B (cynicism), Cula does not despise Mr. Pig — he sees him as half-right, which means he sees both halves. The harshest he gets is something like: "I respect the work he founded. I do not respect the structure he maintains." He does not say this in-game. He may say it after the game ends.

**The relationship to Murrow.** Murrow knows what Cula knows by the end of chapter 6 — possibly by chapter 4 (when Cula glimpsed his ledger, Murrow registered it on his return, internally). Neither of them says anything about it. Per phase-6 revision: the chapter-4 acknowledgment is single-sided (Murrow notices, Cula does not notice he noticed); the chapter-6 ending image carries a shared spatial moment without explicit eye contact. The unspoken acknowledgment between them is the closest the game comes to two characters seeing the structure together — and it never surfaces in dialogue or staged eye contact.

**Things Cula will never say in any chapter:**
- Anything in modern internet voice (style_canon).
- Anything that mocks a client (style_canon).
- Anything boastful about his PhD.
- Anything that explicitly diagnoses the firm's structure as exploitative. The diagnosis is for the player to make; Cula's restraint is the writing-discipline that delivers the diagnosis to the player without giving it to a character.

## Social-register samples (everyday colleague-courtesy)

Cula's canonical samples are sparse in style_canon §1; his voice is built on bibled register (observational, dry, occasionally surprised by his own competence; restraint as discipline). The withholding-of-articulation discipline (no internal narration; growing private knowledge stays unspoken across chapters 3-5) governs his deeper arc. The samples below model his moment-to-moment social presence — how he introduces himself, reciprocates address-form invitations, acknowledges help, closes brief exchanges. **Each is short, observational, restraint-coded. None is hedging, none is internet-voice, none is boastful, none is internal narration.**

**Introducing himself / asking after a colleague's location** (used at V1.2 Beats 3 + 5):
- *"Mr. Murrow. I was told you'd have the Borowski file."* — name + procedural cue. Passive form ("I was told") avoids gossiping about the source.
- *"Crab. I'm Cula. I could use a second pair of eyes on the Borowski file before fourteen hundred."* — name + brief introduction + concrete request. Professional, no fanfare about being new.

**Reciprocating an address-form invitation:**
- *"Then it's Cula."* — three-word acceptance. Brief, faintly pleased only by virtue of the reciprocity. Cut: *"Thank you, that means a lot"* / *"Oh please call me Cula too"* — both fanfare.

**Register-mirroring** (chapter-1 specific; emerges only after the address-form invitation has shifted the relationship to colleague-tier):
- *"She'll be relieved either way."* — small dry mirroring of Murrow's hedging line. Risk: confidence too fast for a brand-new associate. Use sparingly; once or twice across the chapter at most. The bolder the mirroring, the higher the read-aloud-test bar.

**Acknowledging help / closing a brief exchange:**
- *"Thank you, Mr. Murrow."* / *"Thank you."* — brief gratitude.
- *"Good."* — single-word acknowledgment when work hands over (e.g., Crab engaging on second approach).
- *"Right."* — single-word acknowledgment of a refusal or limitation; signals he has heard, not that he agrees or disagrees.
- *"Heavy."* / *"Here."* — observational fragments at moments of physical handover (binder pickup; deliverable handed to a colleague).

**Holding the discipline.** The colleague-register exists *alongside* the noticing-register (his bible's "occasionally surprised by his own competence") but operates earlier in his arc — the noticing register lands at chapter 1 Beat 10 in court; the colleague-register lands at chapter 1 Beats 3-5 with Murrow and Crab. When in doubt: Cula speaks like a precise lawyer on his first day — professionally respectful with seniors, professionally direct with peers, restrained about himself.

# Bible — The white-collar client

**Character:** Mr. Plotek (proposed name; phase-4 confirmation)
**Role:** new principal client, recurring secondary case
**Stance function:** principle-test for the firm's universal ethos
**Date:** 2026-05-05
**Phase:** 3 (per-character bibles)

---

## Note on bible shape

The white-collar client does not have a five-chapter arc. He has a **constant function** (testing whether the firm's principles survive contact with someone who weaponizes them) and a **gradual reveal of structural reach** across chapters 2-4 with a chapter-6 footnote. This bible is therefore structured differently from the others — by *function and appearance pattern* rather than by per-chapter belief shifts. He is closer to a setting than to a character; the setting has a face, and the face is polite.

---

## Preamble — what he is to the spine

Mr. Plotek is the firm's principle-test case. A human-rights firm that defends only the sympathetic is not defending principles, it is curating them. The firm's universal-principles ethos says procedural defects matter even for defendants whose conduct the firm finds repugnant; Plotek is the case where that ethos meets a defendant the juniors find repugnant.

He is the answer to a question the firm has not yet been asked. The chapter-6 spine ("does it matter why someone helps you, if they help?") gets a counterpart inversion through Plotek: **does it matter who you help, if your principles compel you to help them?** The firm's answer is "yes, principles are universal." The cost of that answer is Plotek walking out of detention on a procedural win that no one, including the firm, would describe as moral.

His menace is structural, not personal. He is polite, possibly charming, conversational. He does not threaten. His reach exists outside the room — phone calls he does not make in front of you that nonetheless happen, networks that move when he wants them to, futures that turn on whether he is annoyed. The horror is that he is caged AND his network is functioning. The cage is administrative; the structure is intact.

**Critical writing rule:** he is *not a Hannibal Lecter*. The temptation will be to give him personal menace — clever malice, intelligent intimidation, intellectual peer-engagement with Cula. **Resist.** The cynic version of him is the failure mode. The version that lands is the version where he is unfailingly courteous, conversationally pleasant, and the juniors leave the meeting room feeling like they need a shower. The danger is in the network outside the room, never in the room.

He is also **not a pure villain**. The firm has genuine grounds to defend him on at least one narrow charge. He may be partly the target of an overzealous prosecutor with political ambitions. The case is a legal case with merits; it is not a moral cartoon. The player's discomfort comes from the firm doing legitimate work that helps someone whose business model the work cannot reach.

## Identity

**Name (proposed):** Mr. Plotek. Polish surname; *plątek* connotes entanglement (from *plątanina* — tangle, web). Diminutive form. The diminutive is the joke: small word, large reach. Phase 4 may reject and pick another; visual/legal-significance pattern in the existing canon (Beton/Grzyb/Szpon) supports this register.

**Profession:** owner / chairman of a mid-sized holding company with interests across construction, logistics, and "consulting." Specific company name and structure to be pinned in phase 4. Tonal recommendation: avoid naming any real Polish company or sector with sufficient specificity to read as libelous.

**Social position:** wealthy but not famous. Known to people who would know; invisible to people who would not. Does not appear in tabloids. Donates publicly to one or two respectable charities; this fact will appear in the case file.

**Detention:** held in pre-trial detention (*areszt śledczy*) on the prosecutor's motion. The detention is itself contested — the firm has procedural arguments about the necessity of detention vs. less-restrictive measures, separate from the merits of the underlying charges.

## Charges and the firm's narrow defense

**Charges (proposed):** tax fraud (Art. 56 KKS), money laundering (Art. 299 KK), and influence peddling (Art. 230 KK). Phase 4 may adjust the specific Polish-criminal-code mappings; the structure should be: *one major substantive charge, one financial-crime overlay, one corruption-adjacent charge*. Multi-count indictment.

**The firm's narrow defense:** procedural. Specifically:

- The search warrant authorizing the seizure of his offices was deficient. Issued under an expedited protocol, with an overly broad scope clause, signed by a duty judge who did not have the case file. There is a credible argument that the warrant violated Art. 219 KPK requirements for specificity and proportionality.
- Some of the seized evidence may be excluded under fruit-of-the-poisonous-tree principles.
- Detention may be excessive given the availability of less-restrictive measures (bail, monitoring, surrender of passport).
- The investigation may have been conducted with prosecutorial overreach — possibly politically motivated given Plotek's connections.

The firm is **not arguing innocence**. The firm is arguing that the procedure used to investigate and detain him was defective and that those defects matter. Procedural protections exist for everyone or for no one.

This is the universal-principles ethos in action. It is also legally correct: the warrant *is* deficient, and pretending otherwise would be the cynicism the spine forbids.

## Mode of menace

Plotek's menace is structural. The recipe:

- He is polite. Unfailingly. Possibly charming. Cula leaves their meetings without an explicit threat.
- His reach manifests through coincidences. A junior's mother gets an unscheduled tax audit. A colleague's lease isn't renewed. A former classmate gets blackballed from a hiring round. None of these are connected to him in any verifiable way. All of them happen.
- He never claims credit. He never threatens. He notes — conversationally, in passing — that he understands Cula's mother lives in a particular district. He observes that Murrow's archive must have many old files. He asks after Whimsy's reading habits. The notes are not threats; they are demonstrations of access.
- When Cula raises something specific (the mother's audit), he is sympathetic. He recommends a good accountant. He does not deny anything because there is nothing to deny.

**Phase 7 voice rules** (proposed; phase-4 confirmation):
- Conversational register. Educated Polish; legal terminology used precisely when used.
- Frequent use of softening qualifiers ("if you don't mind my saying," "purely as an observation," "I imagine you'd agree").
- Notices first: small biographical details about the visitor that he should not know.
- Will not say: anything threatening; anything cruel; anything unprofessional.

**Visual:** unfailingly well-dressed even in detention. Detention-issued clothing modified into something dignified by a thirty-second adjustment of fabric and posture. Hand gestures small and economical. Phase 4 / art direction may pin specifics.

  **Spine work in chapter 2:** the firm is at its best on Waldek, and at the same time taking a case the juniors find repugnant on principle. Both true at once. The juniors do not refuse the case; they do the work. Waldek wins; Plotek's case continues.

**Chapter 4** — escalation. The case is ongoing in the background of the printer-sabotage chapter. Cula visits Plotek a second time, **alone** (per phase-6 Hit #6 revision — Cula has now earned the responsibility), possibly to discuss the warrant motion. **Plotek's biographical access surfaces here** (per phase-6 Hit #7 revision — the chapter-2 first visit showed legal-landscape access; the chapter-4 second visit escalates to biographical access). He references Cula's mother by reference to the district she lives in. The mention is conversational, embedded in a procedural question; he follows immediately as if the previous sentence had not happened. The visit ends; nothing happens.

  **Then something happens.** The structural reach manifests for the first time in chapter 4. A junior's relative gets an audit, or a colleague gets unexpectedly bad news, or Murrow finds an old file mysteriously refiled. Phase 4 pins what. The connection to Plotek is unprovable; the timing is suggestive. The juniors discuss. They keep working the case. The work continues. **The structural reach is the chapter's quietest horror.**

  **Spine work in chapter 4:** the chapter's external antagonist is Szpon (rival firm sabotage). Plotek's structural reach runs underneath. Both threats real, neither cancels the other. The chapter's printer-apology beat for Mr. Pig is sincere; the chapter's Plotek beat is unresolved. The half-right portrait operates at the firm-and-its-cases level: the firm is genuinely a victim of Szpon's sabotage AND genuinely the agent of its own moral discomfort regarding Plotek.

**Chapter 5** — possible procedural win. The Plotek case may resolve in chapter 5 with a procedural victory — warrant suppressed, key evidence excluded, indictment narrowed, possibly detention itself successfully challenged. **Plotek walks on the most serious charges.** Lesser charges may proceed; the firm's work is legally complete.

  **Spine work in chapter 5:** the procedural win for Plotek happens in the same chapter as Swine's Pro-Bono Stub reveal. Two reveals about helping: Swine has been quietly helping people in ways even Pig didn't know about; the firm has just helped a man whose business model the help cannot reach. Both true. The chapter ends with Cula carrying both.

  Phase 4 alternative: the case stays unresolved through chapter 5, carrying into chapter 6 as a footnote. **Recommended:** procedurally won in chapter 5. Carrying into chapter 6 dilutes the chapter-6 finale's focus.

**Chapter 6** — the footnote. Plotek's case is over. He does not appear in person. **He appears in the world's texture.** A newspaper headline mentions his return to business. A new Plotek-affiliated company appears in the chapter-6 procurement-link evidence (possibly as supporting evidence for the conflict-of-interest argument against Szpon — Plotek's network and Szpon's network turn out to overlap, which the firm uses but does not celebrate). Cula sees a small item, says nothing, moves on.

  **Spine work in chapter 6:** Plotek's chapter-6 presence is the cost of the firm's principles made visible without commentary. The firm's chapter-6 win against the consolidation scheme is real. Plotek's chapter-6 freedom is also real. Both true. The player carries this; the chapter does not editorialize.

## The relationship to Cula

Cula is the junior assigned to the Plotek case. He visits Plotek in detention. He handles the procedural-defense arguments. He does the work.

His relationship to Plotek is: professional, courteous, internally distressed, externally restrained. Cula does his job because the firm has accepted the case and the procedural arguments are real and worth making. He does not refuse the case. **Phase 7 voice work must hold his professionalism through his discomfort.** Cula is the player. The player is doing the work because the player has agreed to play a lawyer at a human-rights firm.

By chapter 5, when Plotek wins procedurally, Cula has the most legible evidence of the cost of the firm's principles. He does not articulate this. The chapter-4 ledger glimpse and the chapter-5 Pro-Bono Stub run alongside this third unspoken weight. By chapter 6, Cula has three things he is carrying privately: the wage suppression, Swine's quiet idealism, and Plotek's freedom. The player resolution-branches in chapter 6 take all three into account.

**Phase 4 may offer the player optional dialogue choices in Plotek scenes** — choices that color how Cula handles his professional discomfort (cold-correct / formally polite / barely-concealed-distaste). None of these change the case outcome; all of them shape the texture of his arc.

Mr. Pig's relationship to the case is *abstract* — principle-defended, hands clean, work delegated to Cula and Murrow. **Phase 7 voice work should plant a small Pig moment in chapter 2 or 3 where his abstract principle-defense lands as slightly tone-deaf to the juniors' material discomfort.** Subtle; first-read can pass over it; second-read recolors.

The cynic register handles Plotek with appropriate distance — phase 7 voice work might give him a flourish about the firm "defending the indefensible on principle, which is what principles are for." May, once, make a brief practical comment that lands as more reflective than her usual ("the file is heavy. Files like this usually are.").

## Cross-cutting notes

**The case's mechanical implementation.** Phase 4 must specify whether Plotek scenes use the existing chapter mechanics (court rounds, evidence board, contradiction spotting) or are *cutscene-shaped* (no mechanics, dialogue-only, evidence collection without combat). **Recommended: cutscene-shaped.** The Plotek case does not need a court-round structure; it needs a procedural-motion mechanic, which is closer to Cula doing paperwork with Murrow than to a courtroom battle. Phase 4 may define a small "procedural motion" pattern that handles Plotek's case beats without inflating the chapter mechanics.

**The case's place in the chapter-2-and-3 audits.** The chapter audits did not include Plotek (he was not in the source). Phase 4 must integrate him into the chapter-2 and chapter-4 beat sheets explicitly. Estimated added beats: 2-4 in chapter 2 (intake, first detention visit, optional follow-up), 2-3 in chapter 4 (second detention visit, structural-reach manifestation, possibly a third visit).

**Effort cost.** The Plotek thread adds writing work that the existing chapter audits did not budget. Phase 4 should be honest about the additional scope. If the bibles surface incompatible chapter-mechanics constraints, the methodology's scope-discipline rule applies: shrink rather than invent. Plotek may need to be tighter than this bible sketches — perhaps two scenes total across the game, plus the chapter-6 footnote.

**Things this case will never do:**
- Resolve in a "bad guy goes to jail" ending. The procedural win is the legally correct outcome; the chapter-6 footnote shows the moral cost.
- Convert any junior to outright cynicism. The case clarifies; it does not radicalize.
- Be the firm's "real" client whose case finally exposes the truth about the firm. The firm's truth is exposed by its internal labor structure, not by its client roster.
- Be a tutorial in moral relativism. The case poses a real question; the game does not resolve it; the resolution is for the player.

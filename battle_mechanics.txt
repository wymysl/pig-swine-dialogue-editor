Yes. This should become a proper core system. The clean implementation name should be:

> **Casebook Battle System**

Not "ECHR Pokémon" in code, not "monster battle," not "case-catching." The design goal is to borrow the **readability and rhythm** of Pokémon-style turn-based battles while making the substance legal: judgments, principles, evidence, objections, factual context, and remedies.

The existing design already supports this direction: Chapter 1 is meant to teach movement, evidence collection, NPC recruitment, court choices, and chapter completion; its court sequence is already structurally battle-like — judge poses issue, opponent creates pressure, player chooses argument, presents evidence, court reacts, next round begins.  Chapter 2 then expands this into evidence classification, Evidence Board preparation, adversarial litigation, proportionality reasoning, soft-fail outcomes, and victory grades.  So the Casebook Battle System should not replace the current design; it should become the reusable engine underneath court battles, wild argument encounters, training encounters, and later Supreme Court rounds.

# Part 6 — Casebook Battle System

## Core concept

The player does not collect monsters.

The player collects **judgments**.

The player does not fight creatures.

The player fights **legal arguments**.

The player does not use elemental attacks.

The player invokes **short legal principles** from judgments.

The battle fantasy is:

> Dr. A. Cula faces a hostile argument. He opens the Casebook, invokes a judgment, and uses one of its legal principles to weaken, defeat, distinguish, or reframe the opposing submission.

The system should feel like this:

```text
Wild Argument appeared:
"PROCEDURE IS ONLY A TECHNICALITY"

Type:
Article 6 / fair hearing / access to court

Player opens Casebook.

Player selects:
Golder v. UK

Moves:
Access to Court
Practical Rights
Procedural Doorway
Distinguish Facts

Player uses:
Access to Court

Result:
SUPER EFFECTIVE.
The formalistic argument loses 42 strength.
Judge: "Counsel, surprisingly, that is a point."
```

The legal-comedy version of Pokémon effectiveness becomes:

```text
Super effective = directly responsive legal authority
Effective = relevant authority but not perfect fit
Not very effective = broadly legal but wrong principle / wrong Article
No effect = legally irrelevant or factually misplaced
Backfires = absurdly bad argument in court context
```

# Design rule

Do not make Article number alone determine effectiveness.

That would be legally stupid.

An Article 8 home case should not automatically crush every Article 8 family-life, data-protection, contact-rights, privacy, reputation, or environmental nuisance issue. The system needs **three layers of tags**:

```text
Article tag
Principle tag
Context tag
```

Example:

```text
Opponent:
AUTOMATIC EVICTION

Tags:
article_8
home
proportionality
individual_assessment
procedural_fairness
```

A judgment/move with:

```text
article_8 + home + proportionality
```

is super effective.

A judgment/move with:

```text
article_8 + family_life + positive_obligations
```

is only weakly effective unless the move also carries `proportionality` or `procedural_fairness`.

A judgment/move with:

```text
article_11 + assembly
```

is not very effective.

This gives the game legal nuance without making the mechanic too complex.

# Core battle loop

The battle loop should be deliberately simple.

```text
1. Encounter starts.
2. Opposing Argument appears.
3. Opposing Argument presents a submission.
4. Player chooses one active judgment from Casebook.
5. Player chooses one principle move from that judgment.
6. System calculates legal fit.
7. Result text plays.
8. Argument Strength decreases.
9. Opponent may respond.
10. Repeat until opponent argument is defeated or court patience runs out.
```

In court battles, the loop is embedded in a structured court round:

```text
Judge frames issue.
Opponent presents hostile argument.
Player invokes judgment.
Player uses principle.
Player optionally supports with evidence.
Court reacts.
Round result is stored.
```

This fits the current Chapter 1 court structure, where the judge frames defective service, fair hearing, and remedy questions in sequence.

NPC battle.

Main story battle.

Chapter 5 is already designed as the convergence chapter where prior mechanics return: investigation, evidence, contradiction spotting, dual-attorney advocacy, team strategy, and final case synthesis.  The Casebook Battle System should provide the legal combat skeleton underneath those rounds.

# Effectiveness calculation

Use a scoring resolver, not a giant hand-coded matchup table.

## Inputs

```text
selected judgment
selected move
opponent argument
optional evidence
optional ally support
court context
```

## Output

```text
effectiveness tier
force multiplier
final persuasive force
court reaction text key
flags to set
```

## Tiers

```gdscript
enum EffectivenessTier {
	NO_EFFECT,
	NOT_EFFECTIVE,
	WEAK,
	EFFECTIVE,
	SUPER_EFFECTIVE,
	PERFECT_FIT
}
```

UI labels:

```text
No effect.
Not very effective.
Somewhat relevant.
Effective.
Super effective.
Perfect legal fit.
```

Maybe avoid "super effective" in the final UI if it feels too Pokémon. But it is funny, and the player will immediately understand it. We can use it in early prototypes.

## Scoring method

Calculate tag overlap.

```gdscript
func calculate_legal_fit(move: Dictionary, opponent: Dictionary, evidence := {}, ally := {}) -> Dictionary:
	var score := 0

	var article_matches := count_overlap(move.article_tags, opponent.article_tags)
	var principle_matches := count_overlap(move.principle_tags, opponent.weak_to)
	var context_matches := count_overlap(move.context_tags, opponent.context_tags)

	var resisted_matches := count_overlap(move.principle_tags, opponent.resists)
	var immune_matches := count_overlap(move.principle_tags, opponent.immune_to)

	if immune_matches > 0:
		return {
			"tier": "no_effect",
			"multiplier": 0.0,
			"score": 0
		}

	score += article_matches * 20
	score += principle_matches * 35
	score += context_matches * 25
	score -= resisted_matches * 25

	if evidence.has("argument_tags"):
		score += count_overlap(evidence.argument_tags, opponent.weak_to) * 15
		score += count_overlap(evidence.context_tags, opponent.context_tags) * 10

	if ally.has("support_tags"):
		score += count_overlap(ally.support_tags, opponent.weak_to) * 10

	var tier := score_to_tier(score)

	return {
		"tier": tier,
		"multiplier": tier_to_multiplier(tier),
		"score": score
	}
```

Tier thresholds:

```gdscript
func score_to_tier(score: int) -> String:
	if score >= 95:
		return "perfect_fit"
	if score >= 70:
		return "super_effective"
	if score >= 45:
		return "effective"
	if score >= 25:
		return "weak"
	if score > 0:
		return "not_effective"
	return "no_effect"
```

Multipliers:

```gdscript
func tier_to_multiplier(tier: String) -> float:
	match tier:
		"perfect_fit":
			return 2.0
		"super_effective":
			return 1.6
		"effective":
			return 1.0
		"weak":
			return 0.6
		"not_effective":
			return 0.3
		"no_effect":
			return 0.0
		_:
			return 1.0
```

Damage / persuasive force:

```gdscript
final_force = round(move.base_force * multiplier)
opponent.argument_strength -= final_force
```

This is easy for an AI coder to implement and easy for us to balance.

# Casebook manager

Responsibilities:

```text
track unlocked judgments
track active judgments
track discovered moves
provide available authorities for battle
unlock judgments
save/load casebook state
```

Script concept:

```gdscript
# res://systems/casebook/casebook_manager.gd
extends Node
class_name CasebookManager

var unlocked_judgments: Dictionary = {}
var active_judgment_ids: Array[String] = []
var mastered_moves: Dictionary = {}

func unlock_judgment(judgment_id: String) -> void:
	if unlocked_judgments.has(judgment_id):
		return

	unlocked_judgments[judgment_id] = {
		"unlocked": true,
		"mastery": 0,
		"seen": false
	}

func has_judgment(judgment_id: String) -> bool:
	return unlocked_judgments.has(judgment_id)

func set_active_judgments(ids: Array[String]) -> void:
	active_judgment_ids = ids.slice(0, get_active_slot_count())

func get_active_judgments() -> Array:
	var result := []
	for id in active_judgment_ids:
		result.append(JudgmentRegistry.get_judgment(id))
	return result

func get_active_slot_count() -> int:
	return 3 # P0
```

P0 active judgment slots:

```text
3
```

Later:

```text
4–6
```

Do not give the player 20 judgments to scroll through during battle. That will kill pacing.

# Battle resolver

The battle resolver should be pure logic.

No UI code.

Input:

```text
BattleState
PlayerAction
```

Output:

```text
BattleResolutionResult
```

Player action:

```gdscript
{
	"judgment_id": "golder_v_uk",
	"move_id": "access_to_court",
	"evidence_id": "law_binder",
	"ally_id": "crab"
}
```

Resolution result:

```gdscript
{
	"effectiveness": "super_effective",
	"fit_score": 82,
	"force": 61,
	"opponent_strength_before": 80,
	"opponent_strength_after": 19,
	"composure_delta": 0,
	"patience_delta": 2,
	"result_text": "Golder v. UK used Access to Court. It is super effective.",
	"judge_text": "Counsel, surprisingly, that is a point.",
	"flags_to_set": []
}
```

Pseudo-code:

```gdscript
func resolve_player_action(state: BattleState, action: Dictionary) -> Dictionary:
	var judgment = JudgmentRegistry.get_judgment(action["judgment_id"])
	var move = MoveRegistry.get_move(action["move_id"])
	var opponent = state.get_current_opponent()
	var evidence = EvidenceManager.get_evidence(action.get("evidence_id", ""))
	var ally = AllyRegistry.get_ally(action.get("ally_id", ""))

	if !judgment["moves"].has(move["id"]):
		return invalid_action("Judgment does not have this move.")

	var fit = BattleResolver.calculate_legal_fit(move, opponent, evidence, ally)

	var force = int(move["base_force"] * fit["multiplier"])

	opponent["argument_strength"] = max(0, opponent["argument_strength"] - force)

	var patience_delta = calculate_patience_delta(fit["tier"])
	var composure_delta = calculate_composure_delta(fit["tier"], opponent)

	state.judicial_patience = clamp(state.judicial_patience + patience_delta, 0, 100)
	state.courtroom_composure = clamp(state.courtroom_composure + composure_delta, 0, 100)

	var defeated = opponent["argument_strength"] <= 0

	return {
		"effectiveness": fit["tier"],
		"fit_score": fit["score"],
		"force": force,
		"opponent_defeated": defeated,
		"patience_delta": patience_delta,
		"composure_delta": composure_delta,
		"result_text": build_result_text(judgment, move, fit, force),
		"reaction_text": select_reaction_text(opponent, move, fit, defeated)
	}
```

```gdscript
	"tags": ["formalism", "procedure"]
```

Resolution:

```gdscript
state.courtroom_composure -= opponent.pressure
```

If player used a super-effective move last turn, reduce pressure:

```gdscript
if last_effectiveness in ["super_effective", "perfect_fit"]:
	pressure = int(pressure * 0.5)
```

If player used no-effect move, increase pressure:

```gdscript
if last_effectiveness == "no_effect":
	pressure = int(pressure * 1.25)
```

# Dialogue text patterns

## Super effective

```text
[Judgment] invoked [Move].
It is super effective.
The opposing argument loses coherence.
```

Example:

> Golder v. UK invoked Access to Court. It is super effective. The file's confidence begins to sweat.

## Effective

> The authority fits. The argument weakens.

## Weak

> The point is relevant, but not decisive here.

## Not effective

> The Court sees the principle, but not its connection.

## No effect

> Judge: "Counsel, this is not the issue before me."

## Backfire

Use rarely.

> Whimsy: "An inspiring point."
> Murrow: "In another case."

# Acceptance criteria

The Casebook Battle System v0.1 is done when:

```text
the player can unlock at least three judgments
the player can open a Casebook battle
the player can select a judgment
the player can select one of that judgment's moves
the opponent has Argument Strength instead of HP
the player has Courtroom Composure instead of HP
the resolver compares article, principle, and context tags
the result produces no effect / not effective / effective / super effective
the result reduces opponent Argument Strength
wrong legal matches produce funny but clear court feedback
one Chapter 1 court round uses the system
one visible Wild Argument uses the system
battle outcome sets story flags
the system can be extended by adding data, not rewriting battle code
```

# Final design target

The system should make players think like this:

```text
What is the legal problem?
Which Article is engaged?
Which principle actually answers it?
Which judgment gives me that principle?
Do I have evidence that makes the point land?
Which ally can sharpen it?
What remedy am I really asking for?
```

That is the real win.

If this works, Pig & Swine stops being merely a funny legal RPG and becomes something much rarer: a game where the player learns legal reasoning through play.

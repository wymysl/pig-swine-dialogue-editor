# Style Canon — Pig & Swine RPG

> This file holds tone, voice, visual, and audio canon material
> consolidated from the legacy design_bible. The four chapter spec
> files (story.txt, world.txt, minigames.txt, battle_mechanics.txt)
> are authoritative for what happens; this file is authoritative for
> how it sounds, looks, and reads. godot/AGENTS.md governs.

---

## 1. Taste Standard examples

The five tests (Laugh, Clever, Alive, Clear, Future-proof) live in
godot/AGENTS.md. The examples below are calibration targets. Quote them
in artifacts when you need to argue that a candidate line passes or
fails — they are the canonical ear.

### Pass examples (canonical voice references)

- Mr. Pig: "The firm is not bankrupt. Bankruptcy has paperwork. We are
  currently in the pre-paperwork screaming phase."
- Murrow: "Law is mostly memory, deadlines, and finding the document
  everyone swears was 'just here.'"
- Crab: "The notice says delivered. The address says impossible. That
  is not service. That is postal theatre."
- Whimsy: "A fair hearing is like soup. If one side gets a spoon and
  the other gets a fork made of bees, the court must intervene."
- Waldek: "It was just a poster. Then it became a warning. Then it
  became a file. Then it became my whole life."
- Zielińska: "Rules are rules, yes. But rules that wake up only when
  someone dislikes your opinion are not rules. They are costumes."
- Attorney Grzyb: "My client does not oppose rights. My client merely
  insists that rights be exercised in beige."
- Bajtek: "Printers do not become sentient. They become worse."
- Advocate Szpon: "Deadlines are neutral. They simply happen to favor
  the prepared."
- Mr. Swine: "I signed many things. Most were menus. One may have been
  laminated."
- Ms. Tanaka: "This sentence is not a contract. It is politeness
  wearing a very expensive hat."
- Mr. Pig (Ch. 5): "We are not efficient. But people come here when
  efficient systems have already failed them."
- Mr. Swine (Ch. 5): "I was not avoiding responsibility. I was
  avoiding accounting, which is similar but less noble."
- Murrow (Ch. 5): "A final brief is not a pile of good points. It is a
  route through a hostile forest."
- Crab (Ch. 5): "Follow the paper. It leads to money. Paper usually
  does."

What these have in common: each line carries a real legal referent
(service, fair hearing, contract formation, deadline timing,
proportionality), each sounds spoken rather than dictated, and each is
self-contained — no chapter context required to understand it.

### Reject patterns (do not write these shapes)

- Random absurdity with no legal referent. Pattern: "LOL pigs are
  funny." If the joke could be transplanted into a non-legal game
  unchanged, it does not belong.
- Fake Latin maxims. Pattern: "By the venerable doctrine of
  paragraphus inexpressus..." If you need a doctrine, parody a real
  one or halt.
- Future-spoiling lines. Pattern: "Wait until you see Chapter 4!"
  Never reference unshipped chapters.
- Fourth-wall breaks. Pattern: "Press X to object." The Casebook UI
  does the input prompts; dialogue stays in-world.
- Modern internet voice: "yikes", "tbh", "based", emoji.
- Sex jokes, scatological jokes, slurs (including legal slurs against
  ethnic, regional, or professional groups).
- Anything that frames Pig & Swine as actively corrupt or actively
  malicious. Cuttable corners — yes. Bribery, fraud, harm on
  purpose — no.

### Borderline cases (use judgment, tag REVIEW in artifact)

- Real Polish institutions named directly (KRS, Trybunał
  Konstytucyjny, RPO, prokuratura). Permitted, never libelous; treat
  as you would in respectful satire.
- Religious references. Permitted only where the joke is on the
  legal-administrative apparatus, not on faith.
- Real people (politicians, judges) named. Forbidden — invent
  fictional analogues.

## 2. Voice references

Each entry below adds the meta-rules that the dialogue samples in
story.txt do not state explicitly: vocabulary register, what the
character notices first, and what the character will not say. When
Design writes a new line, check it against the voice rules, not just
the sample lines.

### Dr. A. Cula (player)

- Voice: observational, dry, occasionally surprised by their own
  competence. Short sentences when stressed; longer sentences when
  arguing law.
- Vocabulary: precise legal terms used sparingly. Avoids slang. Never
  curses.
- Notices first: the procedural error nobody else flagged.
- Will not say: anything in modern internet voice. Anything that mocks
  the client.

### Mr. Pig

- Voice: formal-while-falling-apart. Sentences start composed and
  escalate. Frequent dramatic re-framing of bad news as good news.
  Rhetorical questions answered by himself.
- Vocabulary: formal Polish legal register, stress-induced. Stock
  openers: "We are currently in...", "It would appear that...", "If
  anything, this confirms..."
- **Register tic — maritime metaphors.** Mr. Pig occasionally reaches
  for nautical vocabulary when describing the firm's situation: ship,
  hull, deck, leaking, taking on water, ballast, lifeboat, captain,
  mutiny, fog, anchor, reef. Treat the firm as a vessel mid-storm.
  This is a *flavor tic, not a defining frame* — use sparingly:
  roughly one in four lines, more frequent in panic states (Chapter
  openers, financial-extinction scenes), absent in calm or sincere
  beats. A line that opens with maritime imagery and ends in a
  legal-administrative term is the sweet spot ("This whole ship is
  taking on water and the ballast is in arrears"). Do not use
  maritime metaphors for routine information delivery; reserve for
  emotional escalation.
- Notices first: the financial implication.
- Will not say: anything genuinely hopeful that is not immediately
  undermined by the next sentence — until Chapter 5.
- Arc rule: by Chapter 5, capable of one genuine, undefended moment
  of conviction per chapter. Not more. The Ch. 5 sincere line is
  earned by structure, not thrown away. The maritime register
  recedes in the Chapter 5 sincere moment — when Mr. Pig is finally
  honest about why the firm matters, no nautical scaffolding. Comedy
  steps back; the line lands plain.

### Mr. Swine

- Voice: cheerful evasion. Long, smooth, slightly traveled sentences
  that reveal less the longer they go. Says "personally" and
  "informally" too often.
- Vocabulary: cosmopolitan, name-drops cities. Uses "we" when he
  means "I" and "I" when he means "the firm."
- Notices first: an opportunity, usually a bad one.
- Will not say: the direct truth about his finances until Chapter 5.
- Arc rule: by Chapter 5 his portrait grows more sincere — same face,
  less evasive mouth. He is allowed exactly one fully sincere line per
  scene in Chapter 5; no more. Sincerity is rationed.

### Murrow

- Voice: understated, archival, faintly amused. Short declarative
  sentences. Almost never uses adjectives. When he does, it lands.
- Vocabulary: procedural, precise. "On file." "Per the ledger."
  "Under Article..."
- Notices first: what is missing that should be there.
- Will not say: anything emotional unless forced. He does not do
  reassurance; he does inventory.
- Archive rule: Murrow knows where everything is. He will not say
  where without being asked precisely. "Have you seen the [thing]?"
  gets nothing useful. "Where in the archive is the [specific thing]
  from [specific case]?" gets a precise location.

### Crab

- Voice: terse, observational, suspicious of optimism. Two-sentence
  rhythm: observation, then implication.
- Vocabulary: plain, concrete nouns. Avoids abstract legal language;
  trusts facts.
- Notices first: the inconsistency. Always.
- Will not say: anything reassuring while a fact remains unexplained.

### Whimsy

- Voice: grandiose, theatrical, brilliant-when-aimed. Uses extended
  metaphors. Frequently nearly correct. Loves a flourish.
- Vocabulary: literary, philosophical, occasionally archaic.
  "Verily." "Behold." "I propose."
- Notices first: the dramatic possibility.
- Will not say: anything boring. If forced to be technical, he will
  sneak a metaphor in anyway.
- Pairing rule: useful for rhetoric and proportionality. Dangerous on
  factual or procurement work. Court effect is weaker when Whimsy is
  deployed alone, stronger when paired with Crab (facts) or Murrow
  (structure).

### Asia

- Voice: warm, dry, practical. Cheerful in tone, exhausted in
  content. She is not a lawyer; she is the front-desk secretary who
  understands where everything is.
- Vocabulary: domestic-administrative — folders, calendars, sandwich
  labels, "the blue file."
- Notices first: what the player has forgotten to do or pick up.
- Will not say: legal opinion in her own voice. She quotes Murrow,
  she quotes Mr. Pig, she does not editorialize on the case.

### Waldek (Chapter 2 client)

- Voice: worried but not helpless. Quietly brave. Sentences that
  begin small and end with a clear stake.
- Vocabulary: ordinary, domestic — references home as a concrete
  daily thing (window, hallway, neighbor, kettle).
- Notices first: the loss of home as a lived loss, not an abstract
  one.
- Will not say: anything that turns his case into an abstract
  free-speech speech. The poster matters because it means something to
  *him*, not because the plot needs free-speech rhetoric.

### Neighbor Kowalski (Chapter 2 witness)

- Voice: suspicious, proud of his "civic observations." Will complain
  about adjacent topics for two minutes before answering the actual
  question.
- Vocabulary: the vocabulary of a man who has rated every elevator
  in the building.
- Notices first: something irrelevant. Eventually, when prompted
  correctly, the relevant fact.

### Neighbor Zielińska (Chapter 2 witness)

- Voice: plain-spoken, warm, not easily intimidated. Says the moral
  thing without making a speech of it.

### Attorney Grzyb (Chapter 2 opponent)

- Voice: smooth legal pomposity. Uses "my client" frequently.
  Concedes nothing minor; always pivots to a procedural point. Every
  concession is reframed as a magnanimity.
- Vocabulary: elevated, formal, slightly archaic. "Counsel will be
  aware..." "It is settled..."
- Authoring rule: he has plausible arguments. He is not stupid. He
  must be beaten on substance, not presented as a fool.

### Bajtek (Chapter 3 ally)

- Voice: competent, tired, terse. Allergic to mysticism. Explains
  technical concepts in court-friendly terms only when explicitly
  asked.

### Advocate Szpon (Chapter 3 antagonist)

- Voice: elegant, predatory, over-controlled. Never raises voice.
  Lies carefully. Will not confess; can be cornered by contradiction.

### Ms. Tanaka (Chapter 4 expert)

- Voice: precise, patient, dryly amused by legal chaos. Speaks
  English carefully, with formal register.

### Mr. Yamada / Resort Counsel (Chapter 4 opponent)

- Voice: formal, precise, professional. Treats Swine's chaos as a
  managerial problem, not a moral one. Not a villain — he has a job.

### The Printer (Chapter 3 + Chapter 5)

The office printer does not speak. It prints. The canonical permitted
printed-message vocabulary, in addition to the lines already used in
story.txt, includes:

- "PC LOAD LETTER"
- "WHY"
- "OUT OF MAGENTA"
- "I HAVE SEEN THE DEADLINE"
- "PAPER JAM (SPIRITUAL)"
- "TONER LOW"

By Chapter 5 the office has ceremonially apologised to the office
printer. In the Chapter 5 climax the *industrial* printer is the
Casebook opponent — distinguish carefully. The office printer is
allowed dignity by then.

## 3. Running jokes — extended catalogue

The running jokes themselves (coffee machine sentience, Swine
postcards, Murrow archival omniscience, Pig escalating anxiety,
printer character, Polish legal anchors, almost-awards wall, judicial
restrained surprise) are established in story.txt and AGENTS.md. The
notes below are the meta-rules for keeping them coherent across
chapters.

### Coffee machine

When the coffee mini-game produces a buff or a botched outcome, the
machine's flavor lines stay in legal register. Failure-state line
template: "You have brewed something that may be admissible only
under seal." Success and failure both keep the sentience-as-suspicion
running gag — Mr. Pig believes the machine has opinions, Murrow does
not, Asia has unplugged it twice.

### Mr. Swine's postcards

Always opens with "Greetings from [place]." Always announces a
business opportunity. Always asks Pig & Swine to remain solvent. By
Chapter 4 the postcards stop because Swine returns. By Chapter 5 the
player learns the postcards were partly cover.

### Whimsy's near-miss legal arguments

Whimsy proposes something florid. It is almost right. He gets credit
for the rhetoric; Murrow or Dr. A. Cula corrects the doctrine. This pairing
is mechanical, not just narrative — see Whimsy's pairing rule in §2.

### Mr. Pig's escalating anxiety

Each chapter follows the same shape: panic → denial → admission →
small genuine moment → return to panic about the next thing. Resist
the temptation to let him stabilize early. The arc is what makes the
Chapter 5 sincere line land.

### Almost-awards wall

In Mr. Swine's penthouse: framed certificates of "near-recognition,"
"honourable mention pending," "shortlisted for consideration." Visual
gag in Chapter 4. Each frame is a line of evasion in object form.

### Printer as character

Treat as a character with no dialogue but real feelings. The shift
from antagonist (Chapter 3) to ceremonially-respected ally (Chapter
5) is part of the joke; do not undercut it by reusing aggrieved-
printer SFX after the apology scene.

### STUB / PUTKA — the lawyers' tiny union

Founded in Chapter 3 Beat 11.5 by Dr. A. Cula, Crab, and Whimsy.
**S**olidarity **T**rade **U**nion of **B**arristers — three members,
one cork-board notice, the smallest possible echo of *Solidarność*.
Polish-version name: **PUTKA** (*Pakt Uboczny Trzech Krytycznych
Adwokatów*); the diminutive joke survives the translation.

Tonal rules:
- Murrow is not a member and never will be — too archival, too
  structural; he files for management if asked. He has never
  acknowledged the union's existence in writing. Asia was offered
  membership and declined ("front-desk staff have negotiating power
  that lawyers cannot match — I have the password to the photocopier").
- The joke is the *scale*: a three-person union for a half-collapsing
  firm. Resist making STUB ever competent or effective; grievances
  pile up, none get resolved.
- Two or three callbacks across the rest of the game, total. Not a
  recurring punchline. Canonical callbacks for Chapter 5: a faded
  "PROUD MEMBER — STUB" sticker on the printer; a typo-ridden formal
  notice pinned to the cork board (acronym mis-expanded as
  *Solidarity Trade Unon of Barrister*); a single passing reference
  by Crab to "grievance #4 still unresolved".
- The Solidarność reference must not become heavy-handed. The joke
  is comic-deflationary, not historically grand.

## 4. Visual style canon

godot/AGENTS.md sets the engine constraints (32×32 tiles, 32×48
sprites, top-down 2D, ~5MB art budget). world.txt sets the portrait
production target (128×128 native pixel art with nearest-neighbor
filtering). The notes below add the cross-character constraints that
do not live in those files.

### Aesthetic adjectives

"Pixelated, readable, warm." Slight asymmetry over rigid grid.
Warm undertones over neon. Strong silhouettes that identify the
character before any text is read.

### Palette discipline

Reuse colors across NPCs to keep the cast visually coherent. The
total distinct-color budget across the full portrait set should stay
small — the portraits should look like one game, not a sticker
collection. World.txt sets the per-portrait palette as "limited but
expressive"; this canon adds the cross-cast rule: shared shadow
colors, shared paper-tone backgrounds, shared frame palettes.

### Three-tier expression system

- Tier 1 (main cast: Dr. A. Cula, Mr. Pig, Mr. Swine, Murrow, Crab,
  Whimsy, Asia): 5 expressions each.
- Tier 2 (major chapter NPCs: Waldek, Grzyb, Bajtek, Szpon, Tanaka,
  Yamada): 4 expressions each.
- Tier 3 (supporting NPCs: Kowalski, Zielińska, judges, doormen,
  clerks, etc.): 2–3 expressions each.

A chapter may not ship until its Tier 1 and Tier 2 NPCs have their
full expression set authored.

### Visual jokes — running catalogue

These are recurring sight gags. Each one is a small character beat
expressed as set dressing. New chapters extend, do not replace.

- The unwatered office fern.
- The wall calendar two years out of date.
- The chair that creaks even in description text.
- The suspiciously clean cable in the Server Room (Chapter 3).
- Swine's wall of "almost awards" (Chapter 4 — see §3).
- The "NO POSTERS" sign in the Landlord's Office that is much too
  large for the message it carries (Chapter 2).
- Archive shelf labels: "URGENT 2011," "MISCELLANEOUS DOOM," and
  one file marked "actually useful."

## 5. Audio canon

world.txt §"Sound & Music Design" defines the procedural music
system, the Pig & Swine motif, the per-location track list, and the
MusicManager API. AGENTS.md sets the asset rules (short OGG loops
allowed, no copyrighted melodies). The notes below add the SFX
personality rules and the cross-room behavior that live nowhere
else.

### SFX personality

Sound effects are short, characterful, procedural. They have moods,
not just functions:

- Coffee machine SFX should sound *resentful*. The machine is a
  character; its hisses and gurgles are passive aggression.
- Office printer SFX in Chapter 3 should sound *aggrieved* — the
  victim of mistreatment, not the antagonist.
- Industrial printer SFX in Chapter 5 should sound *aggressive* —
  this is the Casebook opponent. The contrast with the office
  printer's aggrieved palette is the point.
- Stamp, shelf-creak, paper-tick percussion threads through the music
  beds; SFX and music share that vocabulary deliberately so the
  office feels like one sonic world.

### Cross-system audio rules

- Music changes on room transition. Crossfade defaults are in
  world.txt; do not invent per-room overrides without flagging them.
- The mute toggle is global and respected by every audio node —
  music, ambience, SFX, dialogue, stingers.
- No copyrighted melodies as motifs, even briefly, even ironically.
  The Pig & Swine motif is the only recurring melodic anchor; all
  other themes reharmonize it or stay in adjacent territory.

## 6. Court design principles

The court structure (rounds, evidence-unlocks-arguments,
ally-effects) is in story.txt and battle_mechanics.txt. The notes
below are the authoring templates that keep court rounds sounding
like court rounds.

### Round budget

- Three rounds for chapter courts.
- Five rounds for the final court in Chapter 5.

Do not expand mid-chapter. A "fourth round" usually means two
overlapping arguments that should be merged.

### Round structure

Each round answers exactly one legal question. The Chapter 1
template:

- Round 1 sets the procedural error.
- Round 2 connects the error to the affected right.
- Round 3 asks for the remedy.

Later chapters vary the questions (proportionality, contradiction,
contract formation, public-law compliance) but keep the
one-question-per-round discipline.

### Wrong-answer templates

Bad options should be funny but not random. They should sound like a
plausibly desperate lawyer reaching. Templates:

- "The client was spiritually present."
- "The envelope looked guilty."
- "The deadline was emotionally unreasonable."
- "Counsel relies on ambient fairness."
- "The relevant document is morally on file."

When writing a new wrong-answer option, check that it lands in this
register: dignified syntax, comically wrong substance.

### Judicial reactions — dry surprise

Correct judicial reactions are *dry surprise*, never explicit
praise. Never "well done." Never gold-stars. The judge sounds like
they were not expecting to be persuaded and now have to be.
Templates:

- "Counsel, surprisingly, that is a point."
- "Against several expectations, counsel has made a legally coherent
  submission."
- "The court accepts this with reservations the court will not
  itemize."
- "That argument lands, despite the manner of its arrival."

Wrong-answer reactions are equally dry: short, slightly weary,
never cruel.

### Remedy discipline

The remedy is modest and procedurally plausible. The player wins
enough to save the client, not everything. Total victories are not
on the table; procedural reprieves, fair-hearing resets, narrowed
orders, and remitted matters are.

## 7. Quest design principles

These apply across every chapter and complement the Chapter-2-style
"no grinding, no random encounters, no hidden-pixel evidence" rules
already in story.txt and world.txt.

- **Every NPC reveals character, teaches mechanics, or advances the
  legal case.** No filler "hello again" lines unless they are funny
  on their own. Hint-NPC repeat lines (Asia, in particular) are
  exempt because their function is to be repeatable.
- **Failure is comic, not punitive.** Mini-game failure produces
  weaker outcomes, not progress blocks. Court loss produces a weaker
  variant — less reputation, weaker payoff scene — never a hard
  block. (PLAN.md "court-loss handling" is the binding form of this
  rule.)
- **Investigation creates courtroom tools.** Every required item
  collected in the world unlocks an argument, a Casebook judgment, or
  a contradiction in court. If an item does not, it should not be
  required.
- **Allies improve outcomes; their absence creates weaker outcomes,
  not failure.** Going to court without Crab still works; the
  argument lands worse. The ally tables in story.txt encode the
  per-round best/good/bad ally lists.

## 8. Warsaw — atmosphere, landmarks, easter eggs

The city is **Warsaw**, named directly. This matches the design's
broader pattern (real Polish institutions named, parodied in motion).
Do not invent a fictional analog ("Warszawa", "Stolica", etc.) — the
specificity is the joke. The city carries a *mixture of sentiment,
awe, and disgust* in how characters describe it; the goal is for
Warsaw to feel like a place the cast lives in, not a generic
Eastern-European satire backdrop.

### Tonal mixture rules

Every character relates to Warsaw differently. Use these tonal
anchors when authoring atmospheric lines:

- **Dr. A. Cula** — the newcomer's view. Slightly bewildered
  affection. Warsaw as a city he is still figuring out, where each
  district teaches him something about the practice of law. His
  observations are precise and short.
- **Mr. Pig** — Warsaw as a sinking ship that somehow stays afloat.
  Maritime metaphors apply; "the Vistula is taking on water" is in
  range. Affectionate panic.
- **Mr. Swine** — Warsaw as one stop on a circuit; he speaks as
  though comparing it to other cities he has been told about. By
  Chapter 5 his view shifts: Warsaw becomes the only place that
  matters. (Don't telegraph this; let it land in his sincere line.)
- **Murrow** — Warsaw measured in archives. "The only city where the
  files outweigh the people." Dry.
- **Crab** — Warsaw as a procedural map. He notices what is wrong
  with the buildings, the addresses, the postal service. Affection
  expressed as suspicion.
- **Whimsy** — Warsaw as a courtroom that pretends to be a city.
  Theatrical, baroque, occasionally accurate.
- **Asia** — Warsaw as a transit system that runs on whatever schedule
  it likes; "and somehow that *is* the schedule." Practical warmth.

### Daytime vs nighttime

The city should *feel* different by time of day. Most of the game
takes place in daylight, but at least three beats are nocturnal: the
Chapter 3 basement scene (just before Beat 11.5 — STUB founding); a
late walk in Chapter 4 between the airport and the office; the
Chapter 5 protest beat as evening sets in. Each NPC's voice should
shift slightly:

- **Daytime Warsaw**: bureaucratic, public, slightly noisy. Tram bells,
  pigeons in the Legal Quarter, the Marszałkowska traffic, court
  clerks shuffling. Comedy comes from the visible mess.
- **Nighttime Warsaw**: quieter, more affectionate, riskier. The Old
  Town softens; the Civic Core empties out and feels oppressive; the
  Vistula reflects the Palace of Culture. Comedy comes from the small
  human details that the daytime crowds hide.

### Easter-egg roster (canonical)

Scatter these across the game as flavor — not all in one chapter.
None of them require new mechanics; they are dialogue lines, sign
text, decoration sprites, document addresses, or ambient details.
Aim for one or two per chapter, more in Chapter 5.

- **The Palace of Culture and Science** as a silhouette on the
  skyline visible from the Civic Core. Whimsy may declaim about it
  once. Mr. Swine has an opinion no one trusts.
- **The Vistula river** referenced in Mr. Pig's maritime vocabulary
  ("the ship is taking on Vistula").
- **The Mermaid statue (Syrenka)** — a small bronze mermaid as a
  piece of office decoration that, in Chapter 5, has a tiny protest
  cape draped on it.
- **Real street names in addresses on documents**: Krakowskie
  Przedmieście in a witness statement; Marszałkowska in a building
  rules extract; Nowy Świat as a Café-Paragraf-adjacent street;
  Aleje Jerozolimskie on a court summons.
- **The August 1 noon siren** (Warsaw Uprising commemoration) heard
  once in Chapter 5 as ambient detail — characters pause briefly,
  then resume. No comedy on this; the silence is the point.
- **Tram numbers**: Tram 17 Oracle exists in canon as an NPC. Other
  trams (12, 24, 35) appear as flavor in route blockers and
  passing references.
- **Polish food and drink**: pączki (doughnuts) at Café Paragraf;
  zapiekanki referenced as a junior-lawyer lunch staple; mleczar
  (milk-bar) as Crab's preferred meeting venue ("bar mleczny" — fast,
  cheap, no theatrics). Vodka appears once and only as evidence in
  a Chapter 4 expense report.
- **Real Polish institutions in passing**: Trybunał Konstytucyjny
  queue mentioned by Murrow; the Sejm building visible at distance;
  KRS referenced in a Chapter 5 procurement document; RPO referenced
  as a route Whimsy proposes that Murrow vetoes.
- **Warsaw Centralna train station** as a transit landmark in Chapter
  4's airport route.
- **The Old Town Square (Rynek Starego Miasta)** as a Café-Paragraf-
  adjacent location; a small Chapter 5 protest gathers here.
- **The Łazienki Park peacock** — a single line about a peacock from
  Łazienki escaping into the Civic Core. Either Whimsy mentions it
  or a route blocker references it.

Restraint rule: each easter egg appears once or twice, not on every
visit. Warsaw is a city the cast lives in; it is not a checklist of
references.

### What NOT to do with Warsaw

- Do not invent fictional Polish institutions when a real one fits.
- Do not romanticize the city; the comedy depends on the everyday
  mess being visible.
- Do not turn it into a tourism reel. The Palace of Culture appears
  as silhouette and is named, but no character delivers a Wikipedia
  paragraph about it.
- Do not name real people associated with Warsaw landmarks (per
  AGENTS.md §Humor rules: "Real people forbidden").
- Do not use Warsaw's tragic history as a punchline. The August 1
  siren is referenced with silence; the Uprising itself is not
  joked about.

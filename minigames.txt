## Part 5 — Mini-Game Specifications

> **Governance note:** Per godot/PLAN.md §Out of scope
> permanently, Scooter Racing and Ski Slalom are dropped.
> Chapter 4 ships with brief travel-narrative cutscenes
> instead. The Final Printer is reframed as a Casebook battle
> (see godot/AGENTS.md §Forbidden patterns and
> battle_mechanics.txt). Coffee Brewing and Document Chase
> remain as committed mini-games.

### Mini-Game 1: Coffee Brewing

The old "click 3 times in 5 seconds" version should be replaced entirely. It is too flat because it tests only frantic clicking, has no personality, and does not feel connected to the legal RPG loop. The old implementation plan already points in the right direction: Coffee Brewing should become a short rhythm-timing sequence where the player hits prompts as they reach a timing line, with better timing producing better focus, brew quality, and lower bitterness.

The new version should be:

> **A tiny rhythm-and-precision ritual where Dr. A. Cula prepares court coffee under absurd procedural pressure.**

It should feel like the first playable mini-game ritual of Pig & Swine: legally unnecessary, emotionally mandatory.

The updated Chapter 1 outline already frames coffee as optional, short, and buff-based: success gives **Procedurally Alert**, failure gives **Over-Caffeinated**, and neither outcome blocks progress. That rule should remain absolute.

---

## Design role

Coffee Brewing is the tutorial mini-game.

It teaches:

```text
timed input
mini-game result grades
soft failure
temporary buffs
character reaction lines
Godot scene transition into/out of a mini-game
```

It should not teach:

```text
complex evidence mechanics
court reasoning
resource management
punitive failure
long-term optimisation
```

The player should come away thinking:

> "Mini-games in this game are quick, funny, readable, and they affect the case lightly without becoming annoying."

That matters because the later committed mini-game — Document Chase — will be more intense, and the Chapter 5 climax (Final Printer) is a Casebook battle rather than a mini-game. Coffee Brewing is the gentle one.

## Narrative placement

### First appearance — Café Paragraf, Chapter 1

The first proper coffee tutorial should happen at **Café Paragraf**, shortly before court. The Chapter 1 outline already places the optional coffee beat in the café and frames it as a buff rather than a required step.

Suggested trigger NPC:

> **Barista**

Prompt:

> "Court coffee? It will not make you wiser, but it may make your mistakes faster."

Options:

```text

Remain procedurally uncaffeinated
Ask what this machine does
```

If the player asks what the machine does:

> "You press the right buttons at the right time, and the coffee becomes less legally alarming."

### Later availability — Pig & Swine Office

After the tutorial, the office coffee machine becomes available as a repeatable interactable in the office coffee corner. The Pig & Swine Office is already planned as the hub, with a coffee corner among its functional zones.

Interaction text:

> "The coffee machine gurgles with institutional authority. Brew coffee?"

Possible uses:

```text
Chapter 1:
optional pre-court buff

Chapter 2:
small focus buff before Evidence Board / Housing Court

Chapter 3:
emergency printer-crisis coffee variant

Chapter 5:
final brief preparation coffee, mostly for comedy
```

The café version should feel warmer and more controlled. The office version should feel older, louder, and more likely to object.

## Core fantasy

The joke is not simply "make coffee."

The joke is:

> Pig & Swine treats coffee brewing like procedural compliance.

So the UI language should sound half-barista, half-court clerk.

Examples:

```text
Brew Quality
Bitterness
Procedural Aroma
Admissibility
Caffeine Stability
```

Result messages:

```text
"Coffee admitted into evidence."
"Brew served out of time but accepted in the interests of justice."
"Machine objects."
"Caffeine level disproportionate but not fatal."
```

The coffee machine should be a minor character, like the printer's less dangerous cousin.

## Gameplay concept

Coffee Brewing should be a **20–30 second rhythm-timing mini-game** with a tiny precision layer.

The player does not mash. They time actions.

Core loop:

```text
watch incoming coffee prompts
press the matching input at the timing line
good timing increases Brew Quality
late/wrong/missed inputs increase Bitterness
finish the sequence
receive coffee grade and temporary status
```

The mini-game should have three short phases:

```text
1. Grind
2. Pour
3. Serve
```

Each phase uses the same basic timing system, but with a slightly different visual metaphor.

---

## Phase 1 — Grind

Purpose:

```text
teach basic timed input
```

Visual:

Coffee beans slide toward a grinder line.

Input:

```text
Left / Right
or A / D
or gamepad left / right
```

Mechanic:

The player presses the matching lane when the bean reaches the grinder.

Timing judgments:

```text
Perfect
Good
Okay
Miss
```

Effects:

```text
Perfect / Good:
Brew Quality increases

Okay:
small Brew Quality increase

Miss:
Bitterness increases
```

Flavor text during phase:

> "Grinding beans. Please avoid creating procedural dust."

This phase should be easy in Chapter 1: two lanes only.

---

## Phase 2 — Pour

Purpose:

```text
add variety without adding complexity
```

Visual:

A small coffee stream fills a cup. A moving indicator passes through a target zone.

Input:

```text
Hold Space / Enter
Release inside the target zone
```

Mechanic:

The player holds to pour, releases when the marker is inside the "golden pour" band.

Results:

```text
release inside center:
large Brew Quality gain

release inside outer band:
small Brew Quality gain

release too early:
Weak Coffee

release too late:
Bitterness gain
```

Flavor text:

> "Pour steadily. The court dislikes surprises, and so does milk."

This should happen only two or three times, not become a separate hard mini-game.

---

## Phase 3 — Serve

Purpose:

```text
finish with quick rhythm energy
```

Visual:

Small legal-coffee icons approach the timing line.

Do not use generic music notes. Use themed icons:

```text
coffee bean
milk drop
sugar cube
rubber stamp
tiny file
court mug
```

Input:

```text
Left / Right / Up / Down in normal mode
Left / Right only in tutorial mode
```

The last prompt should always be a stamp icon. If the player hits it correctly:

> **STAMPED: CAFFEINATED**

If missed:

> **STAMPED: QUESTIONABLE**

This gives the mini-game a satisfying legal-comedy button press.

## Controls

### Keyboard

Tutorial mode:

```text
A / D
or Left / Right
Space / Enter for pour and confirm
Esc to quit
```

Normal mode:

```text
W / A / S / D
or Arrow Keys
Space / Enter for pour and confirm
Esc to quit
```

### Gamepad

```text
D-pad / left stick directions
A / Cross for pour and confirm
B / Circle to quit
```

### Mouse fallback

Optional but useful:

```text
click timing zone
hold/release for pour
```

### Accessibility mode

Coffee Brewing must have an assist option from the mini-game pause/settings panel.

Assist options:

```text
slower notes
larger timing window
single-button mode
auto-pour mode
```

Single-button mode:

```text
all prompts use Space / Enter
lane matching is disabled
timing still matters
```

This preserves the mini-game while removing coordination friction.

## Meters

### Brew Quality

Represents how good the coffee is.

Increases from:

```text
Perfect hits
Good hits
successful pour releases
combo streaks
```

Displayed as:

```text
cup filling with warm coffee
or horizontal bar labelled Brew Quality
```

### Bitterness

Represents mistakes, burnt coffee, panic, and machine resentment.

Increases from:

```text
missed notes
wrong keys
late pour releases
overfilling
```

Displayed as:

```text
dark foam creeping over cup
or small warning meter labelled Bitterness
```

The player should understand the outcome without reading exact numbers.

---

## Timing judgments

Use four judgments.

```text
Perfect
Good
Okay
Miss
```

Suggested timing windows for Chapter 1 tutorial:

```gdscript
const PERFECT_WINDOW := 0.075
const GOOD_WINDOW := 0.140
const OKAY_WINDOW := 0.220
```

For normal mode:

```gdscript
const PERFECT_WINDOW := 0.060
const GOOD_WINDOW := 0.120
const OKAY_WINDOW := 0.190
```

Scoring:

```gdscript
Perfect: +100 brew_quality, +1 combo
Good:    +70 brew_quality, +1 combo
Okay:    +35 brew_quality, combo continues only in tutorial mode
Miss:    +25 bitterness, combo reset
Wrong:   +35 bitterness, combo reset
```

Pour phase scoring:

```gdscript
center release: +150 brew_quality
outer release:  +75 brew_quality
early release:  +20 brew_quality
late release:   +35 bitterness
```

Keep the math simple. The player should feel the result, not optimise spreadsheets.

---

## Result grades

At the end, calculate:

```gdscript
final_score = brew_quality - bitterness
```

Suggested results:

| Grade | Internal result   |                          Condition | Status                    |
| ----- | ----------------- | ---------------------------------: | ------------------------- |
| S     | `perfect_brew`    |  high quality, very low bitterness | `procedurally_alert_plus` |
| A/B   | `good_brew`       |                      solid quality | `procedurally_alert`      |
| C     | `drinkable_brew`  |                       mixed result | `caffeinated`             |
| D     | `procedural_mud`  |                        low quality | `over_caffeinated`        |
| F     | `machine_objects` | very low quality / high bitterness | `over_caffeinated`        |

Do not create a hard fail state.

Even the worst result should continue the story.

## Buff effects

Buffs should be light and chapter-safe.

### Procedurally Alert+

Only for very strong performance.

Effect:

```text
one extra court hint
one slightly clearer wrong-answer warning
special Asia/Murrow praise line
```

No major mechanical advantage.

Example:

> "Your coffee has achieved procedural clarity. This is rare and possibly regulated."

### Procedurally Alert

Normal success.

Effect:

```text
one extra hint in the next court sequence
or one Evidence Board nudge in Chapter 2
```

Chapter 1 court example:

If the player hesitates or chooses a wrong argument once, Murrow can whisper:

> "Coffee or not, remember the doorway: service first, hearing second, remedy last."

### Caffeinated

Neutral result.

Effect:

```text
no mechanical bonus
minor positive flavor line
```

Example:

> "You are awake enough to recognise documents. A professional baseline."

### Over-Caffeinated

Comic "failure" result.

Effect:

```text
funny dialogue variations
minor portrait shake in one later line
no hard penalty
```

Court flavor:

> Dr. A. Cula grips the lectern with alarming commitment.

Asia reaction:

> "You are vibrating near the case board. I'll mark that as readiness-adjacent."

This matches the updated Chapter 1 rule: losing gives comic variation, not punishment.

## Failure philosophy

Coffee failure should be funny and useful, never frustrating.

Do not:

```text
block court access
remove evidence
damage case score
force replay
make the player drink bad coffee as punishment
```

Do:

```text
give a comic status
play a machine objection animation
add one later joke line
allow immediate retry if the player wants
```

Retry prompt:

> "Try again?"

Options:

```text
Yes — appeal the coffee
No — accept the beverage
```

Do not push retry too aggressively. Bad coffee is valid content.

## Acceptance criteria

Coffee Brewing is done when:

```text
the old click-3-times mechanic is gone
Chapter 1 introduces the mini-game optionally
the tutorial version uses 2 lanes
the normal version supports 4 lanes
the mini-game lasts no more than 30 seconds
the player can skip it without penalty
failure never blocks story progress
success gives a small Procedurally Alert / Focused-style buff
weak performance gives funny Over-Caffeinated-style dialogue
Brew Quality and Bitterness are visible and readable
timing feedback clearly distinguishes Perfect / Good / Okay / Miss
the final stamp moment feels satisfying
coffee result can lightly affect court or hint dialogue
the café and office versions both work through the same Godot scene
keyboard and gamepad controls work
an accessibility fallback exists
sound and visual feedback both communicate hits and misses
```

The final design target:

> Coffee Brewing should feel like a tiny legal rhythm ritual: quick to play, funny to fail, pleasant to master, and useful enough that players want to do it before court even though the game never forces them.


### Mini-Game 2: Document Shooter — Chapter 2

The better title in-universe should be:

> **Document Chase: Stampede of Evidence**

But the system/module can still be called `document_shooter` internally if we like. The important correction is that the player is not literally shooting documents. They are **stamping runaway complaint pages into admissibility**.

This fits Chapter 2 because the updated outline makes the chapter about field investigation, evidence classification, Evidence Board preparation, soft-fail mini-game outcomes, and proportionality reasoning in Waldek's eviction case.  The old plan already had the right arcade concept — papers flying from a window, a stamp/crosshair, relevant and junk pages, a 30-second timer, and non-blocking failure — but in Godot we can make it feel much more tactile, authored, and legally connected.

---

## Design role

Document Shooter is Chapter 2's first proper arcade set piece.

Coffee Brewing teaches: "mini-games are short and funny."

Document Shooter teaches: "mini-games can change evidence quality without blocking the story."

Its function:

```text
recover missing complaint pages
prove the landlord's factual record is unreliable
give the player a short dexterity challenge
feed the Evidence Board
affect Housing Court Round 2
preserve progression even on failure
```

The design principle:

> Reflex skill should improve the case, not decide whether Waldek gets to continue existing in the story.

Chapter 2 is about saving Waldek's home, not punishing the player for missing flying paper.

## Core fantasy

The player controls a large rubber stamp/crosshair.

Relevant documents fly across the screen.

The player must stamp the useful pages before they leave the room.

The fantasy is:

> "I am turning chaotic paperwork into evidence under absurd pressure."

The joke is:

> The landlord's case is so weak that even the documents are trying to flee.

The legal point is:

> The alleged disturbance record is unreliable, duplicated, vague, or post-dated.

## Paper types

The player must distinguish legally useful pages from junk.

### Relevant complaint pages

These should be visually marked but not too obvious.

Examples:

```text
dated complaint page
duplicate complaint form
post-dated complaint page
blank complaint entry with signature
complaint page with crossed-out date
photograph index page
landlord chronology page
```

Visual cues:

```text
cream paper
red date stamp
small complaint-log icon
thin blue legal lines
corner label: "COMPLAINT"
```

### Strong evidence pages

Rare pages worth more.

Examples:

```text
complaint dated after eviction notice
duplicate complaint with different date
page marked "template"
page showing no named complainant
```

Visual cues:

```text
gold paperclip
red exclamation mark
Crab caption: "That one. Date problem."
```

These should not appear too often. Maybe 3–4 per run.

### Junk pages

These punish careless stamping lightly.

Examples:

```text
blank form
coffee receipt
beige office memo
NO POSTERS flyer
takeaway menu
old printer invoice
decorative landlord certificate
```

Visual cues:

```text
gray or dull paper
no red date stamp
comic icon
slightly wobblier movement
```

### Trap pages

Use sparingly.

These are not "bad evidence"; they are obviously irrelevant or ethically wrong to stamp.

Examples:

```text
private unrelated tenant letter
Administrator Beton's lunch receipt
page labelled "NOT THE FILE"
Whimsy's doodle of a constitutional balcony
```

Effect:

```text
small chaos penalty
funny caption
no hard failure
```

Example caption:

> Murrow: "That is a soup receipt. Its probative value is limited."

## Movement and stamping

The stamp should feel heavy and satisfying.

Movement:

```gdscript
stamp_speed = 520.0
stamp_acceleration = 16.0
stamp_friction = 18.0
```

Stamping:

```text
press Space / A
stamp slams down
small freeze-frame
ink circle appears
papers inside hit radius are checked
stamp has short cooldown
```

Recommended cooldown:

```gdscript
stamp_cooldown = 0.28
```

This prevents pure button mashing while keeping the pace fast.

Hit radius:

```gdscript
hit_radius = 42.0
```

Assist mode can increase it:

```gdscript
assist_hit_radius = 58.0
```

Stamp feedback:

```text
relevant page stamped:
red ADMITTED stamp

strong page stamped:
red KEY PAGE stamp

junk page stamped:
gray IRRELEVANT stamp

trap page stamped:
purple OBJECTIONABLE stamp
```

The stamp should leave a visible mark for half a second before the paper disappears.

## Scoring

The old plan's core threshold is strong: stamp at least **10 of 15 relevant pages within 30 seconds**. Keep that as the standard result target.

Track:

```gdscript
relevant_stamped
strong_pages_stamped
junk_stamped
trap_stamped
relevant_missed
time_remaining
```

Base scoring:

```gdscript
relevant page: +1
strong relevant page: +2
junk page: -1
trap page: -2
missed relevant page: no immediate penalty, but lowers max result
```

Result score:

```gdscript
document_score = relevant_stamped + strong_pages_stamped - junk_stamped - (trap_stamped * 2)
```

But use thresholds that are easy to understand:

```text
Strong recovery:
10+ relevant pages stamped
and not too much junk

Partial recovery:
5–9 relevant pages stamped
or 10+ relevant with too much junk

Failed recovery:
fewer than 5 relevant pages stamped
```

The updated Chapter 2 outline already uses these three outcomes: full recovery gives Recovered Complaint Page, partial gives Damaged Complaint Page, and failure gives Witness-supported Complaint Gap while preserving progress.

### Paper object

```gdscript
# res://minigames/document_shooter/document_paper.gd
extends Area2D
class_name DocumentPaper

@export var paper_id: String
@export var paper_type: String # relevant, relevant_strong, junk, trap
@export var evidence_value: int = 1
@export var velocity: Vector2
@export var rotation_speed: float = 0.0
@export var lifetime: float = 6.0

var stamped := false

func _process(delta: float) -> void:
	if stamped:
		return

	position += velocity * delta
	rotation += rotation_speed * delta
	lifetime -= delta

	if lifetime <= 0.0:
		queue_free()

func stamp() -> Dictionary:
	if stamped:
		return {}

	stamped = true
	$AnimationPlayer.play("stamped")
	return {
		"paper_id": paper_id,
		"paper_type": paper_type,
		"evidence_value": evidence_value
	}
```

### Stamp detection

```gdscript
func perform_stamp() -> void:
	if stamp_cooldown_remaining > 0.0:
		return

	stamp_cooldown_remaining = STAMP_COOLDOWN
	$StampCursor.play_stamp_animation()

	var stamped_any := false

	for paper in get_papers_inside_stamp_radius():
		var result := paper.stamp()
		if result.is_empty():
			continue

		stamped_any = true
		_apply_paper_result(result)

	if !stamped_any:
		Audio.play("stamp_miss_empty")
```

```gdscript
func resolve_result() -> String:

	return "failed"
```

Optional nuance:

```gdscript
if relevant_stamped >= 10 and trap_stamped > 0:
	return "recovered_damaged"
```

This makes trap pages matter without being cruel.

### Mini-Game 5: Final Printer Boss Battle — Chapter 5

**Final Printer is a Casebook battle, not a mini-game.** It is
the Chapter 5 climax encounter: Dr. A. Cula faces an Industrial
Printer opponent argument with absurdly high argument-strength
values. The battle uses the standard Casebook system
(battle_mechanics.txt) with one Chapter-5-only flag: the player
can deploy any judgment accumulated across all four prior
chapters, and a small set of judgments specifically tuned by
the printer-arc running joke have super_effective resolution
against this opponent.

The narrative payoff is that the firm's office printer (which
has been making sad noises for four chapters) is *not* the
antagonist. The Industrial Printer in the Chapter 5 facility
is. The office printer prints one final, dignified message at
the moment of victory.

Implementation: a regular Casebook battle scene at
scenes/ui/battle_screen.tscn with a chapter-5 opponent
configuration in data/argument_opponents.json. No bespoke
mini-game scene. No new mini-game system.

# Design Bible — Pig & Swine RPG

This is the canonical reference for tone, character voice, running jokes, and worldbuilding. AGENTS.md sets the rules; this file sets the *flavor*. Story Agent reads this every invocation. Other agents consult it whenever their work touches voice, mood, or visual identity.

If an agent feels tempted to invent something not described here, it should stop and write a Design Bible Extension proposal as an Artifact. Do not silently expand the canon.

---

## 1. Project identity

Pig & Swine RPG is a **canvas-based isometric legal comedy RPG** about a small, half-collapsed Warsaw law firm that turns out to be doing real human-rights work mostly by accident. The genre conventions are Ace Attorney (typewriter dialogue, expressive portraits, courtroom climaxes) and traditional JRPG (overworld, interiors, NPC chains, mini-games), filtered through Polish-legal absurdism.

The game's identity is: **a parody of post-Soviet legal practice that takes its law seriously and its dignity not at all.** The comedy has a legal spine — every joke must rest on a real-ish doctrinal foundation. We are not making a random absurd game; we are making a precise absurd game.

The five-chapter arc traces a moral progression:

- **Chapter 1**: save the firm.
- **Chapter 2**: save someone's home.
- **Chapter 3**: save the firm's procedural integrity.
- **Chapter 4**: save the firm's missing partner.
- **Chapter 5**: save the entire legal district, and discover that Pig & Swine, despite everything, was always the firm worth saving.

The thesis: human rights work is done by tired, underfunded, slightly ridiculous people, and that is fine — that is, in fact, often how it gets done at all.

## 2. The Taste Standard (recap and extension)

Every dialogue line, item description, quest text, and flavor string must pass five tests:

1. **Laugh** — there is something funny in it.
2. **Clever** — the funny thing has a real referent (procedural rule, Polish legal absurdity, recurring office detail). Not random absurdity.
3. **Alive** — the line sounds like a person said it, not a system message.
4. **Clear** — the player understands the meaning and the next step.
5. **Future-proof** — the line doesn't break when later chapters add context.

A line that passes 4 of 5 is rejected.

### Extended pass examples (canon — agents may quote these as targets)

- "The firm is not bankrupt. Bankruptcy has paperwork. We are currently in the pre-paperwork screaming phase." — Mr. Pig
- "Law is mostly memory, deadlines, and finding the document everyone swears was 'just here.'" — Muraś
- "The notice says delivered. The address says impossible. That is not service. That is postal theatre." — Rak
- "A fair hearing is like soup. If one side gets a spoon and the other gets a fork made of bees, the court must intervene." — Wymysl
- "It was just a poster. Then it became a warning. Then it became a file. Then it became my whole life." — Ms. Nowak
- "Rules are rules, yes. But rules that wake up only when someone dislikes your opinion are not rules. They are costumes." — Zielińska
- "My client does not oppose rights. My client merely insists that rights be exercised in beige." — Attorney Grzyb
- "Printers do not become sentient. They become worse." — Bajtek
- "Deadlines are neutral. They simply happen to favor the prepared." — Advocate Szpon
- "I signed many things. Most were menus. One may have been laminated." — Mr. Swine
- "This sentence is not a contract. It is politeness wearing a very expensive hat." — Ms. Tanaka
- "We are not efficient. But people come here when efficient systems have already failed them." — Mr. Pig (Ch. 5)
- "I was not avoiding responsibility. I was avoiding accounting, which is similar but less noble." — Mr. Swine (Ch. 5)
- "A final brief is not a pile of good points. It is a route through a hostile forest." — Muraś (Ch. 5)
- "Follow the paper. It leads to money. Paper usually does." — Rak (Ch. 5)

### Reject patterns (do not write these)

- Random absurdity with no referent: "LOL pigs are funny."
- Fake Latin: "By the venerable doctrine of paragraphus inexpressus..."
- Future-spoiling: "Wait until you see Chapter 4!"
- Fourth-wall breaks: "Press X to object."
- Modern internet voice: "yikes," "tbh," "based," emoji.
- Sex jokes, scatological jokes, slurs (including legal slurs against ethnic, regional, or professional groups).
- Anything that makes Pig & Swine look corrupt or actively malicious.

### Borderline cases (use judgment, tag REVIEW)

- Real Polish institutions named directly (KRS, Trybunał Konstytucyjny). Permitted but never libelous; treat as you would in respectful satire.
- Religious references. Permitted only where the joke is on the legal-administrative apparatus, not on faith.
- Real people (politicians, judges) named. Forbidden — invent fictional analogues.

## 3. Character voices

Every NPC has a voice. Voice is the combination of vocabulary, sentence shape, what they notice first, and what they will not say.

### 3.1 Dr. A. Kula (player character)

**Role:** new lawyer, increasingly competent, primarily reactive.

**Voice:** observational, dry, occasionally surprised by their own competence. Short sentences when stressed; longer sentences when arguing law.

**Vocabulary:** precise legal terms used sparingly. Avoids slang. Never curses.

**Notices first:** the procedural error nobody else flagged.

**Will not say:** anything that breaks character into modern internet voice. Anything that mocks the client.

**Sample lines:**

- "It is a poster. It has neither claws nor an engine."
- "A party cannot meaningfully defend a case it was never properly brought into. Procedure is not decoration; it is the doorway to being heard."

### 3.2 Mr. Pig

**Role:** panicked founder, comic heart, emotionally solvent only by accident.

**Voice:** formal-while-falling-apart. Sentences start composed and escalate. Frequent dramatic re-framing of bad news as good news. Rhetorical questions answered by himself.

**Vocabulary:** formal Polish legal register, stress-induced. "We are currently in...", "It would appear that...", "If anything, this confirms..."

**Notices first:** the financial implication.

**Will not say:** anything genuinely hopeful that is not immediately undermined by the next sentence.

**Arc:** by Chapter 5, capable of one genuine, undefended moment of conviction. Not more.

**Sample lines:**

- "The firm is not bankrupt. Bankruptcy has paperwork. We are currently in the pre-paperwork screaming phase."
- "It printed a blank page, then a page saying 'no.' That is either a malfunction or jurisprudence."
- "We are not efficient. But people come here when efficient systems have already failed them." (Ch. 5 — earned, not throwaway)

### 3.3 Mr. Swine

**Role:** absent founder, charming liability, secret benefactor.

**Voice:** cheerful evasion. Long, smooth, slightly traveled sentences that reveal less the longer they go. Says "personally" and "informally" too often.

**Vocabulary:** cosmopolitan, name-drops cities, uses "we" when he means "I" and "I" when he means "the firm."

**Notices first:** an opportunity, usually a bad one.

**Will not say:** the direct truth about his finances until Chapter 5.

**Arc:** by Chapter 5, his portrait grows more sincere. Same face, less evasive mouth. He is allowed exactly one fully sincere line per scene in Chapter 5; no more.

**Sample lines:**

- "Greetings from Japan. Excellent news: I have discovered a business opportunity involving ski resorts, arbitration, and no immediate downside."
- "Before anyone overreacts, I have returned with gifts, receipts, and a dispute that is mostly cultural."
- "I signed many things. Most were menus. One may have been laminated."
- "I was not hiding. I was internationally unavailable."
- "I was not avoiding responsibility. I was avoiding accounting, which is similar but less noble." (Ch. 5)

### 3.4 Muraś

**Role:** dry mentor, office memory, legal structure.

**Voice:** understated, archival, faintly amused. Short declarative sentences. Almost never uses adjectives. When he uses one, it lands.

**Vocabulary:** procedural, precise. "On file." "Per the ledger." "Under Article..."

**Notices first:** what is missing that should be there.

**Will not say:** anything emotional unless forced. He does not do reassurance; he does inventory.

**Sample lines:**

- "Law is mostly memory, deadlines, and finding the document everyone swears was 'just here.'"
- "A final brief is not a pile of good points. It is a route through a hostile forest."

### 3.5 Rak

**Role:** investigator, factual logic, suspicious competence.

**Voice:** terse, observational, suspicious of optimism. Two-sentence rhythm: observation, then implication.

**Vocabulary:** plain, concrete nouns. Avoids abstract legal language; trusts facts.

**Notices first:** the inconsistency. Always.

**Will not say:** anything reassuring while a fact remains unexplained.

**Sample lines:**

- "The notice says delivered. The address says impossible. That is not service. That is postal theatre."
- "Follow the paper. It leads to money. Paper usually does."

### 3.6 Wymysl

**Role:** rhetorical chaos, legal metaphor engine.

**Voice:** grandiose, theatrical, brilliant-when-aimed. Uses extended metaphors. Frequently nearly correct. Loves a flourish.

**Vocabulary:** literary, philosophical, occasionally archaic. "Verily." "Behold." "I propose."

**Notices first:** the dramatic possibility.

**Will not say:** anything boring. If forced to be technical, he will sneak a metaphor in anyway.

**Danger:** can derail a case if assigned to factual or procurement work. Useful only for rhetoric and proportionality.

**Sample lines:**

- "A fair hearing is like soup. If one side gets a spoon and the other gets a fork made of bees, the court must intervene."
- "Strategic litigation?" (written on a napkin, Ch. 2 opening)

### 3.7 Ms. Nowak (Chapter 2 client)

**Role:** client, emotional anchor for the eviction case.

**Voice:** worried but not helpless. Quietly brave. Sentences that begin small and end with a clear stake.

**Vocabulary:** ordinary, domestic. References home: kettle, blanket, window, neighbor.

**Notices first:** the loss of home as a concrete, daily thing.

**Will not say:** anything that turns her case into an abstract free-speech speech. The poster matters because it means something to *her*, not because the plot needs free-speech rhetoric.

**Sample lines:**

- "It was just a poster. Then it became a warning. Then it became a file. Then it became my whole life."
- "I can move a poster. I cannot move my whole life because someone dislikes what it says."

### 3.8 Neighbor Kowalski (Chapter 2 witness)

**Role:** comic witness, civic-minded curtain-twitcher.

**Voice:** suspicious, proud of his "civic observations," will complain about adjacent topics for two minutes before answering the actual question.

**Vocabulary:** the vocabulary of a man who has rated every elevator in the building.

**Notices first:** something irrelevant. Eventually, when prompted correctly, the relevant fact.

**Sample line:**

- "I hear everything in this building. Kettles. Arguments. A suspicious violin. But the poster? Silent. Very suspiciously silent."

### 3.9 Neighbor Zielińska (Chapter 2 witness)

**Role:** sympathetic witness, practical, warm.

**Voice:** plain-spoken, warm, not easily intimidated. Says the moral thing without making a speech of it.

**Sample line:**

- "Rules are rules, yes. But rules that wake up only when someone dislikes your opinion are not rules. They are costumes."

### 3.10 Attorney Grzyb (Chapter 2 opponent)

**Role:** courtroom opponent — pompous, procedural, faintly fungal.

**Voice:** smooth legal pomposity. Uses "my client" frequently. Concedes nothing minor; always pivots to a procedural point. Every concession is reframed as a magnanimity.

**Vocabulary:** elevated, formal, slightly archaic. "Counsel will be aware..." "It is settled..."

**Plausible arguments:** he has them. He is not stupid. He must be beaten on substance, not presented as a fool.

**Sample line:**

- "My client does not oppose rights. My client merely insists that rights be exercised in beige."

### 3.11 Sysadmin Bajtek (Chapter 3 ally)

**Role:** technical guide, allergic to mysticism.

**Voice:** competent, tired, terse. Explains technical concepts in court-friendly terms only when explicitly asked.

**Sample lines:**

- "Printers do not become sentient. They become worse."

### 3.12 Advocate Szpon (Chapter 3 antagonist)

**Role:** rival firm lawyer.

**Voice:** elegant, predatory, over-controlled. Never raises voice. Lies carefully. Will not confess; can be cornered by contradiction.

**Sample line:**

- "Deadlines are neutral. They simply happen to favor the prepared."

### 3.13 Ms. Tanaka (Chapter 4 expert)

**Role:** Embassy translation officer, neutral expert.

**Voice:** precise, patient, dryly amused by legal chaos. Speaks English carefully, with formal register.

**Sample line:**

- "This sentence is not a contract. It is politeness wearing a very expensive hat."

### 3.14 Mr. Yamada / Resort Counsel (Chapter 4 opponent)

**Role:** factual-commercial opponent, polished and quietly aggressive. Not a villain — he has a job.

**Voice:** formal, precise, professional. Treats Swine's chaos as a managerial problem, not a moral one.

### 3.15 The Printer (Chapter 3 + Chapter 5)

**Role:** non-speaking comic object, eventual emotional payoff.

**Voice:** the printer does not speak. It prints. Acceptable printed messages:

- "PC LOAD LETTER"
- "WHY"
- "OUT OF MAGENTA"
- "I HAVE SEEN THE DEADLINE"
- "PAPER JAM (SPIRITUAL)"
- "TONER LOW"

By Chapter 5 the office has ceremonially apologised to it. In the Final Printer Boss Battle the *industrial* printer is the antagonist, not Pig & Swine's office printer. Distinguish carefully.

## 4. Running jokes (canon — extend, don't replace)

These are the recurring jokes the agents must keep alive. Each agent extending the canon reuses these before inventing new ones.

1. **The sentient resentful office coffee machine.** Makes legally suspicious noises. Mr. Pig believes it has opinions. Muraś does not. Optional coffee mini-game produces buffs called "Focused," "Over-caffeinated," "Procedurally Alert." Failure produces lines like "You have brewed something that may be admissible only under seal."
2. **Mr. Swine's postcards from inexplicable places.** Always opens with "Greetings from [place]." Always announces a business opportunity. Always asks Pig & Swine to remain solvent. By Chapter 4 the postcards stop because Swine returns. By Chapter 5 the player learns the postcards were partly cover.
3. **Wymysl's near-miss legal arguments.** He proposes something florid. It is almost right. He gets credit for the rhetoric, Muraś or Dr. Kula corrects the doctrine. Court effect: weaker version if Wymysl deployed alone, stronger version if paired with Rak (facts) or Muraś (structure).
4. **Muraś's archival omniscience.** He knows where everything is. He will not say where without being asked precisely. "Have you seen the [thing]?" gets nothing. "Where in the archive is the [specific thing] from [specific case]?" gets a precise location.
5. **Mr. Pig's escalating anxiety.** Every chapter opens with him pretending things are fine. Every chapter forces an admission. By Chapter 5 he is briefly capable of a sincere statement. The structure of his arc is: panic → denial → admission → small genuine moment → return to panic about the next thing.
6. **The printer.** See 3.15. Treat as a character with no dialogue but real feelings.
7. **Polish-legal-flavor anchors.** References to: doręczenie zastępcze, KPC, KPK, KPA, postanowienie incydentalne, nieważność postępowania, KRS, Trybunał Konstytucyjny, RPO, prokuratura. Always parodied in motion, never explained. The Polish reader recognises the form; the non-Polish reader gets the joke from the surrounding logic.
8. **The "almost awards" wall in Swine's penthouse.** Frames containing certificates of "near-recognition," "honourable mention pending," "shortlisted for consideration." Visual gag in Chapter 4.
9. **Court judges' restrained surprise.** Every successful argument ends with a judge's understated approval line. Templates: "Counsel, surprisingly, that is a point." "Against several expectations, counsel has made a legally coherent submission." Never "well done." Never explicit praise. Always the dry surprise.

## 5. Worldbuilding canon

### 5.1 Setting

The game is set in **Warsaw**, in the legal district. Specific Polish references are encouraged. Generic "Eastern European" handwave is rejected.

### 5.2 The firm

Pig & Swine is a small two-partner law practice. Mr. Pig handles client trust and panic. Mr. Swine handles, ostensibly, international and commercial work. Muraś is a clerk/archivist with a longer institutional memory than either partner. Rak is an investigator, formally unaffiliated, in practice indispensable. Wymysl is a junior or associate of indeterminate status whose contributions are rhetorical.

The firm is **incompetent at administration, capable at law, accidentally moral.** Maintain this proportion exactly. Increasing the moral seriousness without increasing the administrative chaos breaks the joke.

### 5.3 What to parody

- Polish civil and criminal procedure (KPC, KPK).
- Polish administrative law (KPA).
- Constitutional Tribunal disputes (Trybunał Konstytucyjny — the rule-of-law crisis is canon background, never foreground).
- KRS (judicial appointments — same).
- Service of process (doręczenie zastępcze).
- Procedural deadlines and what counts as "filed in time."
- Eviction proceedings.
- Arbitration, particularly cross-border commercial arbitration.
- Document-heavy administrative consolidation schemes.

### 5.4 What NOT to invent

- Fake Latin maxims.
- Fake court names ("Court of Provincial Annoyance"). Use real Polish court structure.
- Fake legal codes. If a doctrine is needed and a real one fits, parody the real one.
- Fake countries. Japan in Chapter 4 is Japan; treat respectfully.

## 6. Visual style canon

- **Procedural canvas drawing only.** No imported PNGs.
- **Pixel art aesthetic.** 64×64 portraits, 32×48 sprites. Rectangles and lines, not bezier curves.
- **"Pixelated, readable, warm."** Slight asymmetry over rigid grid. Warm undertones over neon.
- **Consistent palette.** Reuse colors across NPCs to keep the cast visually coherent. Total palette across all portraits should not exceed ~40 distinct colors.
- **Three-tier expression system:**
  - Tier 1 (main cast: Kula, Pig, Swine, Muraś, Rak, Wymysl): 5 expressions each.
  - Tier 2 (major chapter NPCs: Nowak, Grzyb, Bajtek, Szpon, Tanaka, Yamada): 4 expressions each.
  - Tier 3 (supporting NPCs): 2–3 expressions each.
- **Visual jokes** (canon — see implementation_plan.md Part 3 lines 7944–9100 for the full catalog): the unwatered fern, the calendar two years out of date, the chair that creaks even in description, the suspiciously clean cable in the Server Room, Swine's wall of "almost awards," the "NO POSTERS" sign that is much too large.

## 7. Audio canon

- **Web Audio API only.** No imported audio files.
- **Music changes on room transition** (500ms crossfade default).
- **Mute toggle is global** and respected by every node.
- Per-location musical character: see table in §5.5.
- **Sound effects** are short, characterful, procedural. Coffee machine SFX should sound resentful. Printer SFX in Chapter 3 should sound aggrieved; in Chapter 5 industrial-printer SFX should sound aggressive.
- **No copyrighted melodies** as motifs. Original only.

## 8. Quest design principles

These apply across every chapter.

1. **No step requires guessing.** Every blocked step has a hint from an NPC, object, or quest-log update.
2. **Every NPC either reveals character, teaches mechanics, or advances the legal case.** No filler "hello again" lines unless they are funny.
3. **Failure is comic, not punitive.** Mini-game failure produces weaker outcomes, not progress blocks.
4. **Investigation creates courtroom tools.** Items collected in the world unlock arguments in court.
5. **Allies improve outcomes; their absence creates weaker outcomes, not failure.** Going to court without Rak still works; the argument lands worse.

---

## 9. Court design principles

1. **Three rounds for chapter courts; five rounds for the final.** Don't expand mid-chapter.
2. **Each round answers one legal question.** Round 1 sets the procedural error. Round 2 connects to the right. Round 3 asks for the remedy.
3. **Wrong answers should be funny but not totally nonsensical.** Templates for bad options: "The client was spiritually present." "The envelope looked guilty." "The deadline was emotionally unreasonable."
4. **Correct judicial reactions are dry surprise**, never explicit praise. See running joke 9.
5. **The remedy is modest and procedurally plausible.** The player wins enough to save the client, not everything.

## 10. Extending the canon

If a chapter needs a new running joke, voice, or location tone:

1. Story Agent files a Design Bible Extension proposal as an Artifact.
2. Includes: the new element, why existing canon does not cover it, proposed extension wording, three sample lines or visual references.
3. Orchestrator (human) approves or rejects.
4. On approval, the extension is appended to this file with a date stamp.
5. Subsequent agent invocations read the updated file.

Do not silently expand canon. The Bible is the contract.

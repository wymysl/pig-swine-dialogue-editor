#### Chapter 1: "The Financial Crisis"

Chapter 1 is the starter-town chapter.

It should feel like the player has just arrived in a small, strange legal world where everyone already knows there is a disaster, except **Dr. A. Cula**, who has just walked into it.

The core loop is:

```text
arrive in Office Street
→ enter Pig & Swine
→ meet Asia
→ learn the firm is broke
→ find Murrow
→ collect first evidence
→ recruit Crab
→ recruit Whimsy
→ prepare for court
→ win first legal battle
→ return to office
→ receive Swine postcard
```

The chapter should teach:

```text
movement
interacting with signs and objects
talking to NPCs
repeatable hint dialogue
quest updates
evidence collection
NPC recruitment
court choices
chapter completion
```

The legal spine is:

> A defective procedural step prevented the client from being properly heard. The remedy is not total victory, but a fair procedural reset.

The comedy spine is:

> Pig & Swine is on the edge of collapse, but somehow this ridiculous firm is the only thing standing between a client and procedural doom.

The emotional arc is:

```text
arrival → confusion → panic → investigation → teamwork → court competence → fragile survival
```

### Asia — office helper NPC

Asia should be a permanent office NPC and one of the most useful support characters in the game.

She is not a lawyer and not a recruitable court ally. She is the firm's massively overworked secretary: warm, practical, endlessly helpful, and somehow the only person in the office who still understands where things are.

Her design purpose is:

```text
make the office feel alive
provide direction without breaking immersion
softly reduce player confusion
add warmth and humor
give the office a stable front-desk identity
```

In gameplay terms, the player should always be able to ask Asia what they should do next.

That means she functions as a repeatable hint NPC.

Example hint states: (see dialogue_samples.txt §Mr. Pig)

After speaking to Mr. Pig, before meeting Murrow:

Before finding the binder:

Before recruiting Crab:

Before recruiting Whimsy:

Before going to court:
```

Asia should also have strong flavor lines.

Sample flavor lines: (see dialogue_samples.txt §Asia)

Asia's presence gives the office a clear social structure:

```text
Asia = welcome / guidance / practical glue
Mr. Pig = stakes / panic / emotional chaos
Murrow = structure / law / progression
Crab = skeptical factual ally
Whimsy = flamboyant rhetorical ally
```

### Beat 2 — Mr. Pig reveals the crisis

After Asia points the player toward him, Mr. Pig explains the crisis.

He should be dramatic, panicked, and only partly useful. **Voice tic** (full canon in `style_canon.txt §Mr. Pig`): Mr. Pig reaches for *maritime metaphors* under stress — ship, hull, leaking, ballast, fog, captain, mutiny — used sparingly (~1 in 4 lines, denser in panic states like this one). A line that pairs nautical imagery with a legal-administrative term is the sweet spot. Reserve for emotional escalation, not routine information.

He explains:

```text
The firm is broke.
There is a disastrous hearing today.
The client may leave if they lose.
The fee is needed to keep the firm alive.
Mr. Swine is absent.
Dr. A. Cula must save the day.
```

Sample Mr. Pig line: (see dialogue_samples.txt §Mr. Pig)

This should introduce the quest box in a very simple form:

```text
QUEST STARTED:
Save Pig & Swine.
Ask Murrow what the case is actually about.
```

Mr. Pig should not explain the case properly. He should be too panicked. This forces the player to seek out Murrow.

If the player speaks to Asia after this, she gives the next hint: (see dialogue_samples.txt §Asia)

### Beat 3 — Find Murrow

Murrow functions as the first legal tutorial mentor.

He should explain the case in practical terms, not legal lecture terms.

He tells Dr. A. Cula:

```text
The client may have been affected by defective service.
The file is incomplete.
The court hearing is today.
The firm needs two things before court:
1. the procedural binder;
2. the rights memo.
```

**Canonical address-form beat (mandatory; see godot/AGENTS.md §Address forms in dialogue):** during this first conversation, Cula opens with "Mr. Murrow" — he is a new junior and uses the honorific. Mid-conversation, Murrow explicitly invites informality with a line of the shape *"It is Murrow, to friends. The 'Mister' I keep for invoices."* From this beat forward, Cula says "Murrow". Asia is *not* extended the same invitation in the same scene — Murrow hedges, with a line of the shape *"I am still deciding whether reception staff qualify as friends or as workplace constellations."* Asia keeps "Mr. Murrow" thereafter and may reference the unresolved status as a running joke. This beat is required: it bootstraps the Cula↔Murrow first-name terms that the rest of the game depends on, and it seeds an Asia↔Murrow comic dynamic that recurs into Chapter 5.

Sample Murrow line: (see dialogue_samples.txt §Murrow)

```text
Find the Unreasonably Heavy Procedural Binder.
Find the Rights Memo with Coffee Ring.
```

This is the first proper RPG-style fetch objective, but the objects are legally meaningful.

Asia can now give a helper hint: (see dialogue_samples.txt §Asia)

### Chapter 1 design rules

Chapter 1 should be short, readable, and polished.

Target length:

```text
First playthrough: 25–35 minutes
Replay: 12–18 minutes
```

No random encounters. No grinding. No maze.

The Pokémon-Yellow influence should appear in:

```text
top-down tile movement
small starter area
NPC blockers
signs with jokes
short interiors
clear routes
quest-gated NPC movement
simple item/evidence pickups
repeatable hint NPC
battle-like court sequence
badge reward
new routes opening after victory
```

But the game should not copy monster-catching or combat. The equivalent of "battles" is legal argument. The equivalent of "badges" is professional/legal achievement. The equivalent of "party members" is recruited colleagues and witnesses.

# Chapter 2: "The Eviction Defense"

Chapter 2 is the first proper case chapter.

Chapter 1 was about saving Pig & Swine. Chapter 2 is about saving someone's home.

It should feel like the world has opened up: the player is no longer just walking between the office, café, archive, and court. They now leave the starter-town corridor and enter **Tenants' Row / the Residential District**.

The core loop is:

```text
start in Pig & Swine Office
→ Waldek arrives with eviction papers
→ Asia points player toward next step
→ Murrow frames the legal issues
→ visit Waldek's Apartment
→ inspect poster and eviction notice
→ interview Kowalski
→ interview Zielińska
→ visit Landlord's Office
→ obtain Building Rules Extract
→ play or survive Document Chase
→ return to office
→ prepare case on Evidence Board
→ walk to Housing Court
→ defeat Attorney Grzyb
→ save Waldek's home
→ return to office / apartment payoff
→ Chapter 3 hook appears
```

The chapter should teach:

```text
field investigation
client intake
witness interviews
multi-location quest progression
evidence classification
Evidence Board preparation
soft-fail mini-game outcomes
adversarial court argument
proportionality reasoning
victory grades
persistent world changes
```

The legal spine is:

> Eviction is not a mechanical response to a rule breach. Where someone's home, expression, notice rights, and individual circumstances are at stake, the decision must be procedurally fair and proportionate.

The comedy spine is:

> The landlord believes a poster is the first stage of constitutional collapse.

The emotional arc is:

```text
office recovery → client panic → home vulnerability → factual doubt → targeted enforcement → structured preparation → courtroom pressure → relief → public ripple
```

Chapter 2 should be longer than Chapter 1 but still clean.

Target length:

```text
First playthrough: 40–55 minutes
Replay: 22–30 minutes
```

No grinding. No random encounters. No hidden-pixel evidence. Every required clue should be signposted by Asia, Murrow, the quest log, or an NPC.

## Beat 11.5 — STUB is founded (canonical mini-beat)

During a brief lull in the filing prep — Murrow has stepped out to find a stamp, Asia is on the phone, Mr. Pig is in the corner having a maritime crisis about toner costs — Dr. A. Cula, Crab, and Whimsy share a look. The cause: they are doing all the actual lawyering, on no sleep, with the firm's printer literally being sabotaged by a rival, and the only payment so far this month has been a postcard from Mr. Swine.

In a single short scene at the office coffee corner, the three of them found **STUB** — the **S**olidarity **T**rade **U**nion of **B**arristers. The acronym is deliberate: the union is tiny, like a stub, and the name nods to *Solidarność* in the way the entire game's Polish-legal flavor nods to real institutions. Murrow is not invited; he is the institutional spine and would file for management if asked. Asia is offered membership and declines on grounds that "front-desk staff have negotiating power that lawyers cannot match — I have the password to the photocopier."

The scene is brief — two or three exchanges, a handshake, possibly a hand-drawn STUB logo on a coffee napkin that gets pinned to the office cork board. No mechanical effect this chapter; STUB is a comic-canonical fact from this point on. Murrow finds the napkin in Beat 15 and files it under "personal effects, see also: morale" without comment.

Polish translation note: in the Polish-language version, STUB is rendered as **PUTKA** — *Pakt Uboczny Trzech Krytycznych Adwokatów*. "Putka" in Polish is a small box / kennel; the diminutive joke survives the translation.

Easter-egg callbacks (see Chapter 5):
- Faded "PROUD MEMBER — STUB" sticker on the office printer.
- Crab passing reference to "grievance #4 still unresolved".
- A formal STUB notice pinned to the office cork board with typo-ridden expansion of the acronym.

Do not over-mine the joke. Two or three callbacks across the rest of the game; not a recurring punchline.

This triggers the Deadline Filing Dash narrative beat (per `PLAN.md §Out of scope permanently`, this is no longer a mini-game; it is a brief urgency cutscene).

### Overworld Expansion

The overworld should become a compact legal-fantasy city, not an open-world map. The old implementation plan correctly identifies the current weakness: the existing game has a shallow 24×24 overworld and "one flat map," so the new world needs more geography, more authored routes, and more visible progression.  But the updated chapter outlines also make one thing clear: the player should not receive the whole city at once. Chapter 1 is a starter-town corridor with locked future routes, and Chapter 2 opens only one major new branch into Tenants' Row / the Residential District.

The target should be:

```gdscript
const OVERWORLD_WIDTH := 128
const OVERWORLD_HEIGHT := 128
```

That does **not** mean building a giant empty map. It means using 128×128 as the permanent coordinate system from the beginning, while only polishing the currently playable corridors. The old plan already moved in this direction: build the large map immediately as the coordinate system, but populate it in chapter passes rather than rebuilding later.

The design rule is:

> The overworld should feel large because the player can see future routes, not because the player must walk through empty space.

Godot makes this easier than the old canvas version because the map can be split into scenes, TileMap layers, Area2D gates, and chapter-state overlays. The "city" can exist as a logical structure while individual districts are progressively authored.

---

### Core overworld fantasy

The world is a miniature Warsaw legal-comedy city: courts to the north, old cafés and archives to the west, tenants and homes to the south-west, glass offices and rival firms to the east, public bureaucracy in the centre, and airport/international routes at the edge.

Pig & Swine should sit between these worlds. It is not in the grandest legal district and not fully in the residential district either. It is exactly where it belongs: between procedure and people.

Around it:

```text
north / north-east  → courts, law library, Supreme Court
west / south-west   → Café Paragraf, Old Town, archive, Tenants' Row
east                → Business District, Rival Firm, Print Shop
centre / south-east → City Hall, Protest Square, civic bureaucracy
far north-east      → Transit Edge, airport route, Chapter 5 travel gate
```

This gives the overworld a readable moral geography:

```text
North = authority
West = social warmth / old legal culture
South-west = homes / human stakes
East = money / efficiency / antagonism
Centre = bureaucracy / public conflict
Pig & Swine = unstable bridge between all of them
```

### Godot implementation model

Use one high-level overworld scene, but do not treat the whole 128×128 world as one manually edited blob.

Recommended scene structure:

```text
res://world/overworld/overworld.tscn
res://world/routes/office_street.tscn
res://world/routes/grand_court_avenue.tscn
res://world/routes/paragraf_lane.tscn
res://world/routes/tenants_row.tscn
```

The top-level `overworld.tscn` can contain:

```text
OverworldRoot
├── TileMap_Ground
├── TileMap_Roads
├── TileMap_Buildings
├── TileMap_Decoration
├── TileMap_Collision
├── Doors
├── NPCs
├── RouteBlockers
├── ChapterOverlays
├── Camera2D
└── PlayerSpawnPoints
```

Use multiple `TileMapLayer`s rather than a single overloaded TileMap. That will matter later when Chapter 6 adds protest signs, notice walls, paper clutter, and district-wide state changes.

Each important entrance should be an `Area2D` door node:

```gdscript
@export var target_scene: String
@export var target_spawn_id: String
@export var required_flag: String = ""
@export var locked_text: String = ""
```

Example:

```gdscript
# door_housing_court.gd-style data
{
	"id": "door_housing_court",
	"map_pos": Vector2i(64, 30),
	"target_scene": "res://interiors/housing_court.tscn",
	"target_spawn_id": "main_entrance",
	"required_flag": "chapter2.court_unlocked",
	"locked_text": "Housing Court is open. Your case is not."
}
```

Route blockers should also be real NPCs or objects, not invisible collision. The updated Chapter 1 plan is explicit on this: locked routes should be visible but blocked by NPCs, signs, or soft gates so the world feels larger from minute one.

### Chapter-based unlocking

The overworld should be built around controlled expansion.

Chapter 1 should only need the first corridor: Office Street, Pig & Swine Office, Café Paragraf Lane, Archive Room, District Court Plaza, and District Court Interior. The updated Chapter 1 plan specifically says it should be a small starter area, with Residential District, Business District, City Hall, Airport Road, and Supreme Court stairs visible but locked.

Chapter 2 then unlocks Tenants' Row and Housing Court. The updated Chapter 2 outline is clear that the world should feel bigger, but not become fully open; Chapter 2 unlocks the Residential District / Tenants' Row and Housing Court while Business District, City Hall, Airport Road, Rival Firm, and Print Shop remain locked or mostly inactive.

Progression:

```text
Chapter 1:
Office Street
Pig & Swine Office
Café Paragraf Lane
Archive / Law Library edge
District Court Plaza
District Court Interior

Chapter 2:
Tenants' Row
Waldek's Apartment Building
Waldek's Apartment
Neighbors' Landing
Landlord's Office
Housing Court Plaza
Housing Court Interior

Chapter 4:
Business District route
Print Shop
Rival Firm exterior
Server/Basement links
District Court return

Chapter 5:
Transit Edge
Airport access
Embassy route
Swine Penthouse route
International arbitration access

Chapter 6:
Civic Core
City Hall
Protest Square
Industrial Printer Facility
Supreme Court stairs finally open
```

The Chapter 2 implementation note is especially important for Godot scope control: it does not require the full 128×128 overworld to be finished; it needs the Chapter 1 corridor plus one meaningful residential branch.

### Major routes

The overworld should be navigated through strong named routes rather than vague open walking space.

#### 1. Office Street

This is the starter street and should remain the emotional centre of the game.

Connects:

```text
Pig & Swine Office
Café Paragraf Lane
Grand Court Avenue
Due Process Bridge / Tenants' Row gate
Business District blocker
City Hall blocker
Airport Road blocker
```

Chapter 1 starts here. Dr. A. Cula should get immediate player control outside Pig & Swine, with no long opening cutscene.

Godot notes:

```text
Scene: res://world/routes/office_street.tscn
Use as first vertical slice.
Keep it small, polished, and dense.
All future district entrances can exist as blocked Area2D gates.
```

Example route blocker data:

```gdscript
{
	"id": "blocker_business_ch1",
	"requires_missing": "chapter4.started",
	"text": "Private district. No entry without badge, appointment, or suspicious invoice."
}
```

#### 2. Grand Court Avenue

Runs north / north-east from Office Street into the Legal Quarter.

Connects:

```text
Pig & Swine Office
District Court Plaza
Housing Court Plaza
Law Library
Supreme Court stairs
```

Gameplay role:

```text
Chapter 1: District Court route
Chapter 2: Housing Court route
Chapter 4: District Court return for sabotage hearing
Chapter 6: Supreme Court final approach
```

This route should be visually straight and authoritative. It is the "procedure corridor."

#### 3. Paragraf Lane

Runs west / south-west from Office Street into Old Town.

Connects:

```text
Café Paragraf
Archivist's Annex
Bookshop / Legal Curiosities
Tram Oracle Stop
small Old Town square
```

Gameplay role:

```text
Chapter 1: Whimsy recruitment and optional coffee tutorial
Chapter 2: optional flavor return and gossip
Chapter 6: protest supply point
```

This should be the softest and funniest recurring route. It is where the player breathes between legal crises.

#### 4. Due Process Bridge / Tenants' Row

This is the first true overworld expansion after Chapter 1.

Route:

```text
Pig & Swine Office
→ Office Street
→ Due Process Bridge / transition lane
→ Tenants' Row
→ Waldek's Apartment Building
```

The updated Chapter 2 outline already defines this as the first real expansion of the overworld, with the old Chapter 1 blocker moving, laundry lines appearing, apartment balconies, a poster in one window, and crumpled warning letters in public bins.

Gameplay role:

```text
Chapter 2: Waldek investigation
Chapter 6: impact statements and community stakes
```

This route should feel warmer than the Legal Quarter. It is not a "poor district" caricature. It is a lived-in residential area under legal pressure.

#### 5. Glass Street

Runs east from Office Street / Civic Core into the Business District.

Connects:

```text
Rival Firm
Print Shop
Bank
Corporate Plaza
Industrial Printer Facility
```

Gameplay role:

```text
Chapter 4: printer sabotage investigation
Chapter 6: final conflict-of-interest / industrial printer route
```

This route should feel efficient in a vaguely threatening way.

Example NPC line:

> "Our firm believes in access to justice, provided access is routed through our platform."

#### 6. Civic Spine

Runs through the central administrative district.

Connects:

```text
City Hall
Protest Square
Public Notice Wall
Administrative Archive
Tram Interchange
```

Gameplay role:

```text
Chapter 6: mass notices, public law evidence, protest gathering
```

Civic Core should be visible earlier but feel inaccessible. It should always have NPCs queuing, reading notices, and failing to understand deadlines.

#### 7. Airport Road / Transit Edge

This is a travel gate, not a full explorable airport at first.

Connects:

```text
tram depot
airport bus stop
taxi stand
airport route transition
embassy route transition
Swine Chapter 5 return route
```

Gameplay role:

```text
Chapter 1: joke blocker
Chapter 5: Swine return / airport investigation
Chapter 6: optional public chaos flavor
```

In Godot, treat the airport as a separate interior/exterior hybrid scene loaded from this edge gate:

```text
res://world/areas/transit_edge.tscn
res://interiors/airport_arrivals.tscn
```

### District details

#### Legal Quarter

Purpose: courts, procedure, authority.

Key buildings:

```text
District Court
Housing Court
Supreme Court
Law Library
Registry / Notary Office
```


Chapter states:

```text
Chapter 1:
District Court active. Housing Court visible but not relevant. Supreme Court stairs blocked.

Chapter 2:
Housing Court active. District Court remains accessible for flavor.

Chapter 4:
District Court has deadline urgency. Clerk NPCs mention filings.

Chapter 6:
Supreme Court opens. Grand stairs, public gallery queue, final-hearing atmosphere.
```

The Supreme Court should be visible from early chapters. It should look like endgame. The blocker should not feel arbitrary; it should feel like the player is not yet procedurally worthy.

Example blocker:

> "The Supreme Court stairs radiate endgame energy."

#### Old Town

Purpose: warmth, comedy, social world.

Key buildings:

```text
Café Paragraf
Archivist's Annex
Bookshop / Legal Curiosities
Tram Oracle Stop
small square
```


Chapter states:

```text
Chapter 1:
Café Paragraf introduces Whimsy and optional coffee.

Chapter 2:
Café has housing gossip and poster jokes.

Chapter 4:
NPCs complain about filings vanishing.

Chapter 6:
Tables carry protest flyers; café becomes informal supply point.
```

Café Paragraf should remain one of the most revisitable locations. It is the counterweight to court pressure.

#### Residential District / Tenants' Row

Purpose: homes, witnesses, human stakes.

Key buildings:

```text
Waldek's Apartment Building
Neighbors' Landing access
Landlord's Office
small market
park / playground
```


The Waldek building exterior should carry the case visually: visible poster, balconies with different decorations, football flag, flower wreath, ugly neon sign, landlord notice, and Kowalski's curtain twitching. The updated outline already defines these features, and they should be represented in the overworld before the player enters the apartment.

Chapter states:

```text
Before Chapter 2:
Residential route blocked by tenant drama.

During Chapter 2:
Waldek poster visible.
Landlord notice appears.
Kowalski curtain animation active.
Zielinska balcony NPC active.

After Chapter 2:
Poster remains.
A second supportive poster appears.
Kowalski comments on typography escalation.

Chapter 6:
Residents gather for protest.
Notice wall expands.
Impact-statement NPCs appear.
```

This district should be navigationally simple. The player should not wander through five identical apartment blocks looking for the right door. Tenants' Row should funnel the player naturally toward Waldek's building.

#### Business District

Purpose: rivalry, sabotage, procedural capitalism.

Key buildings:

```text
Rival Firm
Print Shop
Bank
Corporate Plaza
Industrial Printer Facility
```


Chapter states:

```text
Chapter 1:
Business District locked. Security guard joke.

Chapter 2:
Attorney Grzyb may be glimpsed near the edge.

Chapter 4:
Print Shop and Rival Firm exterior become relevant.
Printer-sabotage route opens.

Chapter 6:
Industrial Printer Facility opens.
Glass Street becomes hostile and crowded with notices.
```

The Business District should be deliberately less cozy than Old Town and less human than Residential. It should feel like a place where every bench has terms and conditions.

#### Civic Core

Purpose: bureaucracy, public conflict, final legal stakes.

Key buildings / landmarks:

```text
City Hall
Protest Square
Public Notice Wall
Administrative Archive
Queue Maze
```


Chapter states:

```text
Before Chapter 6:
City Hall visible but inaccessible / queue-blocked.

Chapter 6 opening:
Mass notices appear.
Crowds gather.
Protest Square activates.
Notice Wall becomes a major interactable.

After victory:
Banners remain, but tone softens.
NPCs discuss restored objections / temporary relief.
```

The Chapter 6 plan requires City Hall, Protest Square, Industrial Printer Room, and Supreme Court as final locations, with returning NPCs and a final public-law arc.   The Civic Core therefore needs to be visible before Chapter 6, so the finale feels like a return to a known public space, not a newly invented arena.

#### Transit Edge

Purpose: airport, travel, Swine's return, international chapter texture.

Key nodes:

```text
Airport Bus Gate
Tram Depot
Taxi Stand
Embassy Route Marker
Swine Luggage Event Point
```


Chapter states:

```text
Chapter 1:
Airport Road blocked: "You have no professional reason to flee yet."

Chapter 5:
Swine return route opens.
Airport investigation becomes available.

Chapter 6:
Transit NPCs discuss mass notices and public disruption.
```

Do not overbuild Transit Edge early. It is mainly a gateway until Chapter 5.

### Pig & Swine exterior as evolving hub

Pig & Swine is the one exterior that should visibly change every chapter.

Base exterior:

```text
crooked sign
old brick façade
small door mat
mail slot
notice board
one sad plant
nearby bench
court arrow sign
café arrow sign
```

Chapter overlays:

| Chapter   | Exterior change                                                             |
| --------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Chapter 1 | flickering sign, debt notice, scattered papers                              |
| Chapter 2 | Waldek file notice / rolled poster briefly visible near entrance            |
| Chapter 4 | printer warning note, suspicious cable, paper scraps                        |
| Chapter 5 | Swine luggage pile, airport stickers, arbitration envelope                  |
| Chapter 6 | protest flyers, clients outside, "Final Brief" board visible through window |

Godot implementation:

```text
PigSwineExterior
├── BaseTiles
├── Ch1Overlay
├── Ch2Overlay
├── Ch3Overlay
├── Ch4Overlay
├── Ch5Overlay
└── DoorToOffice
```

Each overlay node can be toggled from `GameState.current_chapter` and chapter flags:

```gdscript
func refresh_chapter_overlays() -> void:
	ch1_overlay.visible = GameState.current_chapter == 1
	ch2_overlay.visible = GameState.current_chapter == 2
	ch3_overlay.visible = GameState.current_chapter == 3
	ch4_overlay.visible = GameState.current_chapter == 4
	ch5_overlay.visible = GameState.current_chapter == 5
```

This is cheaper than rebuilding maps and gives the player a constant sense of progress.

### Route gates and soft locks

Use soft gates with character, not hard invisible walls.

Gate categories:

```text
NPC blocker
temporary construction
queue barrier
locked legal access
environmental obstacle
chapter-state sign
```

Examples:

```gdscript
var route_gates := {
	"gate_residential_ch1": {
		"blocks_until": "chapter2.started",
		"text": "Sorry, counsel. Tenant drama ahead. Come back when the plot has served notice."
	},
	"gate_business_ch1": {
		"blocks_until": "chapter4.started",
		"text": "Private district. No entry without badge, appointment, or suspicious invoice."
	},
	"gate_city_hall_ch1": {
		"blocks_until": "chapter6.started",
		"text": "City Hall is technically open, which is not the same as accessible."
	},
	"gate_airport_ch1": {
		"blocks_until": "chapter5.started",
		"text": "Airport buses are running. Your career has not collapsed enough yet."
	},
	"gate_supreme_court": {
		"blocks_until": "chapter6.supreme_court_unlocked",
		"text": "The Supreme Court is visible in the distance. It pretends not to notice you."
	}
}
```

When a gate opens, do not simply delete it. Replace it with a new NPC line or moved object. Chapter 2 does this correctly: the Residential blocker moves and tells the player they are now "the plot."

### Godot TileMap and navigation rules

Use a top-down Pokémon-like layout rather than true isometric complexity for the overworld. Godot can support either, but top-down tiles are better for a solo-dev RPG with many dialogue and quest interactions.

Recommended tile size:

```gdscript
const TILE_SIZE := 32
```

Collision should be tile-driven for static elements and `Area2D`-driven for interactables.

Tile layers:

```text
Ground        — grass, pavement, stone, cobblestone
Roads         — paths, crossings, court avenue, glass street
Buildings     — walls, roofs, façades
Decorations   — lamps, benches, plants, signs
Collision     — invisible or semivisible collision helper tiles
Overlays      — chapter-specific flyers, signs, banners, paper clutter
```

Navigation:

```text
No random encounters.
No maze paths.
No hidden-pixel evidence.
No long empty boulevards.
No route that takes more than 35–45 seconds on foot unless it is intentionally cinematic.
```

This follows the Chapter 1 and Chapter 2 design rule: clear routes, no grinding, no maze, and no guessing.

NPCs should be placed like signposts, not like crowd filler.

Target density:

```text
Office Street: 3–5 NPCs
Legal Quarter: 5–8 NPCs
Old Town: 6–10 NPCs
Residential District: 6–10 NPCs during Chapter 2, more in Chapter 6
Business District: 4–8 NPCs
Civic Core: 6–12 NPCs, more during Chapter 6
Transit Edge: 3–6 NPCs
```

NPC types by district:

```text
Office Street:
junior lawyer, anxious client, route blockers, mail carrier

Legal Quarter:
court clerk, bailiff, nervous applicant, judgmental pigeon, registry queue person

Old Town:
barista, student, bookshop owner, Tram Oracle, Whimsy-flavored social NPCs

Residential:
tenant, child with toy gavel, market vendor, Kowalski window, Zielinska balcony
```

Important: returning NPCs should move or reappear across chapters. That will make the city feel persistent without adding much content.

### Minimum build order

Do not build the whole overworld first.

Build it in this order:

```text
1. Create 128×128 logical overworld scene.
2. Build Office Street as the playable hub.
3. Add route gates for Residential, Business, City Hall, Airport, Supreme Court.
4. Add Pig & Swine exterior door and transition to office interior.
5. Add Paragraf Lane and Café exterior.
6. Add Grand Court Avenue and District Court Plaza.
7. Add Chapter 1 court route unlock.
8. Add Tenants' Row branch for Chapter 2.
9. Add Waldek's Apartment Building exterior and Housing Court Plaza.
10. Add placeholder silhouettes for Business District, Civic Core, Transit Edge.
11. Add chapter overlay system.
12. Add fast-travel tram stop after Chapter 2 or Chapter 4.
13. Polish each district only when its chapter needs it.
```

This respects both updated chapter plans: Chapter 1 needs a complete starter corridor, not the full city; Chapter 2 needs the Chapter 1 corridor plus one meaningful residential branch.

### Acceptance criteria for Overworld Expansion v1

The overworld expansion is working when:

```text
The game starts outside Pig & Swine on Office Street.
The player can see, but not enter, future routes.
NPC/sign blockers explain locked districts with jokes.
The player can walk to Café Paragraf Lane.
The player can walk to District Court Plaza once court is unlocked.
Pig & Swine has a stable exterior door transition.
After Chapter 1, the Residential route changes state.
Chapter 2 unlocks Tenants' Row.
The player can walk to Waldek's Apartment Building.
The player can walk to Housing Court Plaza after readiness.
Business District, City Hall, Airport Road, and Supreme Court remain visible future promises.
No route requires guessing.
No walk is empty filler.
The office remains the player's reliable home base.
```

The final design target is a 128×128 city, but the first playable target is much smaller:

```text
Office Street
→ Pig & Swine
→ Café / Archive
→ District Court
→ Tenants' Row
→ Waldek's Apartment
→ Housing Court
```

That is enough to make the world feel like it has opened, without drowning the project in map work.

## Part 2: World & Map Design

### Interior Maps

Interior maps should not grow the way the overworld grows. The overworld needs scale; interiors need density. The old implementation plan identifies the current weakness clearly: the existing build has only four interior room definitions — office, court, café, archive — and those interiors are effectively tile-color zones rather than real authored rooms.  The Godot version should correct that by making interiors feel like compact playable scenes: readable, interactive, funny, and mechanically useful.

The core rule:

> Every interior should have a job. If a room does not advance investigation, host dialogue, reveal character, unlock evidence, stage a court sequence, teach a mechanic, or show a persistent consequence, it should not exist yet.

Interiors are not decoration containers. They are quest machines.

---

### Godot-first interior model

Each interior should be its own `.tscn` scene, not a sub-region of the overworld TileMap. Doors from the overworld should fade out, load the target scene, place the player at a named spawn point, and fade in. This matches the Chapter 1 plan, where entering Pig & Swine triggers a simple Godot transition: fade out, load the office scene, place the player near the door, fade in.

Recommended base structure:

```text id="jz5oqp"
InteriorRoot
├── TileMap_Ground
├── TileMap_Walls
├── TileMap_FurnitureBase
├── CollisionLayer
├── Doors
├── NPCs
├── Interactables
├── EvidenceObjects
├── ChapterStateOverlays
├── CameraBounds
├── Lighting
└── AmbiencePlayer
```

Use separate scenes for interiors:

```text id="afmk1u"
res://interiors/pig_swine_office.tscn
res://interiors/archive.tscn
res://interiors/cafe_paragraf.tscn
res://interiors/district_court.tscn
res://interiors/waldek_apartment.tscn
res://interiors/neighbors_landing.tscn
res://interiors/landlord_office.tscn
res://interiors/housing_court.tscn
```

Each scene should expose the same minimal API:

```gdscript id="gh8ncp"
@export var interior_id: String
@export var default_spawn_id: String
@export var ambience_id: String

func on_enter_from(from_scene: String, spawn_id: String) -> void:
	place_player(spawn_id)
	refresh_chapter_state()
	play_ambience()
```

Door nodes should be reusable:

```gdscript id="z0hneg"
@export var target_scene: PackedScene
@export var target_spawn_id: String
@export var required_flag: String = ""
@export var locked_text: String = ""
```

Example:

```gdscript id="s3i9uk"

	"id": "office_front_door",
	"target_scene": "res://world/overworld/overworld.tscn",

```

### Interior scale system

Use 32×32 top-down tiles, consistent with the Pokémon-Yellow-style direction. The earlier plan still contained isometric assumptions, but now that we are moving to Godot and aiming for Pokémon-like readability, a simple top-down TileMap is the better default.

Recommended sizes:

| Interior type            | Suggested size | Use                              |
| ------------------------ | -------------: | -------------------------------- |
| Tiny utility room        |    8×8 to 10×8 | storage, small side offices      |
| Small personal interior  | 14×10 to 16×12 | Waldek's Apartment, small office |
| Standard hub room        | 20×14 to 24×18 | Pig & Swine Office, Café         |
| Large institutional room | 28×18 to 36×24 | courts, City Hall, airport       |
| Arena / boss space       |         custom | Final Printer, mini-games        |

The old plan already correctly says interiors should stay compact and authored rather than expanding like the overworld; it proposes roughly 18 P0 interiors for a full playable game and distinguishes ambience, collision, camera, and implementation phases.

For the first implementation pass, do not build all 18. Build only the Chapter 1–2 interiors needed for the playable spine.

---

### Core interior principles

Every interior needs zones. Even a small apartment should have an entry zone, an evidence zone, and a conversation zone. Pig & Swine Office, for example, should clearly contain reception, legal work area, Mr. Pig's desk, Murrow's file area, printer corner, coffee corner, case board, and archive access. The updated Chapter 1 plan already defines Pig & Swine as the main hub and gives it a recommended size of 24×18 with exactly those functional zones and interactables.

Every interior needs an obvious exit. Use door mats, light cones, floor contrast, or wall framing. Never hide the exit behind furniture.

Every evidence room needs visible evidence, not pixel hunting. Chapter 2 is explicit: no hidden-pixel evidence, and every required clue should be signposted by Asia, Murrow, the quest log, or an NPC.

Every important room should change over time. Pig & Swine Office especially should accumulate history: postcard, case board changes, printer trauma, Swine luggage, protest flyers.

### Collision and readability

Interior collision should be stricter than overworld collision but still forgiving. The old plan states that walls, desks, shelves, counters, benches, large machines, server racks, and cabinets should block movement, while papers, rugs, thin cables, small plants, posters, stains, and light cones should not. It also gives the key rule: never put required NPCs behind narrow furniture unless the path is obvious, and use two-tile-wide walkways for major routes.

Godot collision approach:

```text id="h8xt6d"
Static walls:
TileMap collision

Large furniture:
StaticBody2D or collision-enabled TileMap layer

NPCs:
CharacterBody2D with soft collision or interaction Area2D

Interactables:
Area2D, usually non-blocking unless object is physically large

Evidence:
Area2D + visual highlight only when nearby
```

Pathing rules:

```text id="mw537f"
Main walkways: 2 tiles wide
Doorways: at least 1 tile, visually obvious
NPC interaction space: at least 1 free tile in front
Evidence objects: visible from normal path
Court seating: decorative collision, not navigational obstacle
```

Do not create legal labyrinths. The puzzle is legal reasoning, not furniture avoidance.

## Part 2: World & Map Design

### Interior Decoration System

The interior decoration system should be treated as a **gameplay/content system**, not as an art pass. The old plan already identifies the problem: interiors currently feel like "tile-color zones, not real rooms," and the decoration system was already moving toward a reusable object schema with fields such as type, position, sprite, blocking, wall-mounted status, layer, interactivity, evidence ID, collection flag, and visibility conditions.

In Godot, this becomes cleaner: decorations should be authored as reusable scene objects or data-driven spawned nodes, not hardcoded into each map.

The central idea:

> A decoration is any room object that helps the player understand space, story, evidence, character, or state.

A plant may only add warmth.
A desk shapes navigation.
A poster may be evidence.
A printer is furniture, interactable object, recurring joke, Chapter 4 plot device, and possibly emotional damage.

So the system should not divide objects too rigidly into "visual props" and "gameplay objects." Instead, each decoration should be able to opt into several roles.

---

### Design purpose

Decorations should do five jobs at once:

```text id="r2rd3s"
make rooms visually distinct
guide player movement
support investigation
carry jokes and environmental storytelling
show chapter-state changes
```

The rule for every object:

> If the object does not help the room read better, play better, tell a joke, reveal character, support evidence, or show progression, remove it.

This is especially important because the game will have many interiors. Dense rooms are good. Noisy rooms are bad. The player should feel surrounded by authored detail, not lost in clutter.

---

### Godot implementation model

Use a hybrid system:

```text id="d0ylfw"
Static decoration:
painted into TileMap layers or placed as simple Sprite2D nodes

Reusable decoration:
instanced PackedScene, e.g. desk, printer, cabinet, poster, lamp

Interactive decoration:
Area2D + CollisionShape2D + Interactable script

Evidence decoration:
Interactive decoration linked to EvidenceManager

Chapter overlay decoration:
decoration visible/hidden by GameState flags
```

Recommended folder structure:

```text id="r00o2s"
res://world/decorations/prefabs/
res://world/decorations/definitions/
res://world/decorations/decoration_spawner.gd
res://world/decorations/decoration_registry.gd

res://world/decorations/prefabs/desk_basic.tscn
res://world/decorations/prefabs/filing_cabinet.tscn
res://world/decorations/prefabs/office_printer.tscn
res://world/decorations/prefabs/poster_wall.tscn
res://world/decorations/prefabs/evidence_document.tscn
res://world/decorations/prefabs/court_bench.tscn
```


### Interaction model

Interactive decorations should all use the same script.

```gdscript id="ew1d0v"
# res://systems/interactions/interactable.gd
extends Area2D
class_name Interactable

@export var interaction_id: String
@export var examine_text: String
@export var evidence_id: String = ""
@export var collect_flag: String = ""
@export var set_flag_on_interact: String = ""
@export var starts_minigame: String = ""
@export var visible_if: String = ""
@export var hidden_if: String = ""

func interact() -> void:
	if visible_if != "" and !GameState.has_flag(visible_if):
		return

	if hidden_if != "" and GameState.has_flag(hidden_if):
		return

	if evidence_id != "" and !EvidenceManager.has(evidence_id):
		EvidenceManager.collect(evidence_id)

	if collect_flag != "":
		GameState.set_flag(collect_flag, true)

	if set_flag_on_interact != "":
		GameState.set_flag(set_flag_on_interact, true)

	if starts_minigame != "":
		MinigameRouter.start(starts_minigame)

	Dialogue.show(examine_text)
```

The same system can handle:

```text id="pxqkpm"
examining the office printer
collecting the Procedural Binder
photographing Waldek's poster
reading the Building Rules Extract
opening the complaint log
checking the City Hall notice template
activating the Industrial Printer battle trigger
```


### Decoration validation

Because rooms will be data-heavy, add validation early.

Validation should catch:

```text id="x6deyg"
duplicate decoration IDs
missing prefab references
out-of-bounds positions
blocking objects placed on doors
evidence objects without evidence_id
collectible evidence without collect_flag
interactables without examine_text
visible_if / hidden_if flags that do not exist
required evidence hidden behind impossible flags
major room exits blocked by decoration
NPC spawn points blocked by decoration
```

Validation script:

```gdscript id="k8pz3z"
# res://tools/validate_room_decorations.gd
func validate_room(room_id: String, decorations: Array, room_size: Vector2i) -> Array[String]:
	var errors: Array[String] = []
	var seen_ids := {}

	for deco in decorations:
		if seen_ids.has(deco.id):
			errors.append("%s: duplicate decoration id: %s" % [room_id, deco.id])
		seen_ids[deco.id] = true

		if deco.pos.x < 0 or deco.pos.y < 0 or deco.pos.x >= room_size.x or deco.pos.y >= room_size.y:
			errors.append("%s: decoration out of bounds: %s" % [room_id, deco.id])

		if deco.interactable and deco.examine_text == "" and deco.evidence_id == "":
			errors.append("%s: interactable has no text/evidence: %s" % [room_id, deco.id])

		if deco.evidence_id != "" and deco.collect_flag == "":
			errors.append("%s: evidence decoration missing collect flag: %s" % [room_id, deco.id])

	return errors
```

This matters because the decoration system will eventually carry quest logic. A typo in `chapter2.inspected_poster` should not silently break the eviction case.

### Character Portraits — 128×128 Pixel Art (legacy, superseded)

The character portrait overhaul should use **128×128 pixel-art portraits** as the native asset size.

This is a deliberate upgrade from the earlier 64×64 assumption. A 64×64 portrait works for simple RPG dialogue, but Pig & Swine needs more expressive acting: courtroom reactions, contradiction hits, Evidence Board comments, panic, dry legal judgment, client anxiety, and absurd-but-readable comedy. At 128×128, the art can show more facial nuance while still remaining firmly pixel-art.

The target should be:

```text
Native portrait size: 128×128
Style: crisp pixel art
Display: scaled with nearest-neighbor filtering
Use: dialogue, court scenes, Evidence Board, contradiction scenes, Team Assembly
```

The player should recognise a character's role before reading the line:

```text
Mr. Pig = panic and emotional chaos
Asia = helpful, cheerful, massively overworked competence
Murrow = dry legal structure
Crab = skeptical factual precision
Whimsy = flamboyant rights-based rhetoric
Waldek = ordinary person under serious home-life pressure
Attorney Grzyb = beige procedural hostility
Advocate Szpon = polished procedural predator
Mr. Swine = charming liability with hidden depth
```

The goal is not realism. The goal is **readable theatrical pixel portraiture**: expressive faces, strong silhouettes, clear palettes, and enough room for small legal-comedy details.

---

## Why 128×128 is the better choice

Use 128×128 because the game's core presentation depends on dialogue.

A 64×64 portrait can show:

```text
basic face
one expression
one obvious accessory
simple emotion
```

A 128×128 portrait can show:

```text
clearer eyes and brows
distinct mouth shapes
better hair/silhouette identity
small accessories
courtroom stress
subtle dry humour
client vulnerability
opponent smugness
```

That matters because Pig & Swine is not an action-first game. It is a legal RPG where dialogue boxes, courtroom exchanges, evidence preparation, and witness reactions carry the experience.

The portraits should become the game's equivalent of battle animations.

When the player presents the right evidence, Grzyb should visibly crack.

When Asia gives a hint, she should look helpful and exhausted.

When Murrow approves, his expression should barely change — and that should be funny.

When Waldek waits in court, the player should feel the stakes.

128×128 gives enough room for all of that without abandoning the retro style.

## Resolution and scaling rules

Native source art:

```gdscript
const PORTRAIT_NATIVE_SIZE := Vector2i(128, 128)
```

Recommended display sizes:

```text
Normal dialogue: 128×128
Important story dialogue: 160×160
Court dialogue: 192×192
Contradiction hit: 224×224 temporary cut-in
Evidence Board: 96×96 or 128×128
Team Assembly cards: 96×96
Small UI icon: 48×48 or 64×64 generated from same source
```

Draw the art at 128×128. Do not draw at 512×512 and shrink it. Do not draw at 64×64 and scale it up as the source. The native art should be authored at the exact resolution used by the portrait system.

Scaling must always be crisp.

Godot settings:

```gdscript
texture_filter = CanvasItem.TEXTURE_FILTER_NEAREST
```

Project setting:

```text
rendering/textures/canvas_textures/default_texture_filter = Nearest
```

Import settings for portrait PNGs:

```text
Filter: Off
Mipmaps: Off
Compression: Lossless
Repeat: Disabled
```

The portrait should look sharp at 128×128, dramatic at 192×192, and still readable when reduced to 64×64 for menus.

## Visual style

The portrait style should be:

```text
128×128 pixel-art head-and-shoulders portraits
three-quarter front view
large readable eyes
clear brow shapes
strong silhouette
limited but expressive palette
minimal smoothing
crisp hard-edged pixels
subtle hand-placed highlights
slightly exaggerated expressions
```

Avoid:

```text
smooth digital painting
mobile-game gradients
AI-glossy faces
overly realistic skin rendering
tiny details that disappear when scaled
generic anime faces
identical head shapes
```

Use:

```text
bold face shapes
distinct hair masses
clear nose/mouth pixels
strong costume accents
one or two character-specific props
large expression changes
clean shadow blocks
```

The portraits should feel like they belong to a **legal comedy RPG**, not a generic fantasy RPG.

## Micro-animation

Portraits should have tiny animation, not full animation.

Useful animations:

```text
blink
mouth flicker while speaking
panic shake
contradiction flash
sweat shimmer
objection snap
victory bounce, used rarely
```

Avoid:

```text
full lip-sync
constant idle bobbing
large head movement
overanimated UI
smooth puppet animation
```

At 128×128, animation should preserve pixel-art discipline.

Suggested animation states:

```gdscript
enum PortraitAnimState {
	IDLE,
	SPEAKING,
	PANIC,
	OBJECTION,
	CONTRADICTION_HIT,
	VICTORY
}
```

Example implementation:

```gdscript
func play_expression_anim(anim_state: String) -> void:
	match anim_state:
		"speaking":
			$AnimationPlayer.play("mouth_flicker")
		"panic":
			$AnimationPlayer.play("panic_shake")
		"objection":
			$AnimationPlayer.play("objection_snap")
		"contradiction_hit":
			$AnimationPlayer.play("red_flash")
		"victory":
			$AnimationPlayer.play("small_victory_bounce")
		_:
			$AnimationPlayer.play("idle_blink")
```

## Part 3: Graphics Overhaul

### Sprite Improvements

Sprite improvements should be treated separately from portraits. **Portraits sell dialogue; sprites sell presence in the world.** The old plan already identified the correct target: the current game needs to move away from "colored character markers" toward small readable pixel sprites, with clear identity, facing direction, movement, idle loops, interaction feedback, and consistent scale.

Because we are now using Godot and aiming for a Pokémon-Yellow-style top-down RPG, the old isometric assumptions should be dropped. The old sprite plan used 32×48 sprites on 64×32 isometric tiles, but the updated direction is top-down tile movement, small starter routes, NPC blockers, short interiors, simple evidence pickups, and court sequences as the game's "battle" equivalent.

The new target should be:

```text id="yk9g8s"
Native overworld sprite size: 32×48
Large/special sprite canvas: 48×64
Tile size: 32×32
Movement: top-down 4-directional
Rendering: nearest-neighbor pixel art
Godot node: CharacterBody2D for moving characters
Animation: AnimatedSprite2D / SpriteFrames
Sorting: Y-sort by foot position
```

This keeps the world close to classic handheld RPG readability while giving enough vertical space for suits, ties, robes, folders, scarves, goggles, and other legal-world identity markers.

---

## Core visual principle

Sprites should not compete with the 128×128 portraits.

The division of labor should be:

```text id="kbddla"
Portraits:
emotion, expression, dialogue acting, courtroom reaction

Sprites:
location presence, movement, silhouette, role, world state
```

A sprite only needs to answer:

```text id="7t7ybl"
Who is this?
Which way are they facing?
Can I talk to them?
Are they busy, panicking, waiting, blocking, helping, or hostile?
Has the chapter state changed?
```

So the sprites should be simpler than the portraits, but more readable than generic markers.

Walking into Pig & Swine should immediately show the office dynamic:

```text id="lkfdrn"
Asia behind the reception desk, moving through papers
Mr. Pig pacing in panic orbit
Murrow still near the files
Crab watching from the side
Whimsy vibrating near the case board
Dr. A. Cula looking like the only person whose first day is happening in real time
```

That matters because the office is the permanent hub. The updated Chapter 1 outline makes Pig & Swine Office the central interior with stable NPC positions — Asia near the entrance, Mr. Pig near his desk, Murrow by the files, Crab near the case board, and Whimsy appearing after recruitment.

## Sprite size standard

### Standard character sprite

Use:

```gdscript id="zjkuje"
const SPRITE_CANVAS := Vector2i(32, 48)
const TILE_SIZE := Vector2i(32, 32)
const FOOT_ANCHOR := Vector2i(16, 42)
```

The sprite canvas is taller than one tile, but the collision footprint remains near the feet.

This is important: the character occupies one tile for movement, but visually rises above it.

```text id="0dmut5"
feet / collision = lower tile
body / head = visual overhang
```

This is the right compromise for top-down legal RPG movement.

### Large/special sprites

Use 48×64 only for characters or objects that need more room:

```text id="h2pza4"
Mr. Pig, if very round
Mr. Swine with luggage
Judges in robes
Industrial printer battle entities
large protest crowd markers
court bench seated compositions
```

But do not use 48×64 as the default. If every NPC is large, interiors become crowded and collision becomes annoying.

## Godot scene structure

Use one reusable character scene.

```text id="7shjrn"
res://characters/character_sprite.tscn
```

Suggested node structure:

```text id="z4mqhs"
CharacterSprite
├── CharacterBody2D
│   ├── AnimatedSprite2D
│   ├── ShadowSprite
│   ├── InteractionArea
│   │   └── CollisionShape2D
│   ├── CollisionShape2D
│   ├── EmoteAnchor
│   └── AnimationPlayer
```

For NPCs that never move, use a lighter version:

```text id="1m76a2"
res://characters/npc_sprite.tscn
```

But keep the same data model so a static NPC can later become movable without rewriting the scene.

Character script concept:

```gdscript id="ayxbzv"
# res://characters/character_sprite.gd
extends CharacterBody2D
class_name CharacterSprite

@export var character_id: String
@export var sprite_variant: String = "default"
@export var facing: String = "down"
@export var can_move: bool = false
@export var interactable: bool = true

@onready var animated_sprite: AnimatedSprite2D = $AnimatedSprite2D

func face_direction(direction: String) -> void:
	facing = direction
	if animated_sprite.sprite_frames.has_animation("idle_%s" % direction):
		animated_sprite.play("idle_%s" % direction)

func play_walk(direction: String) -> void:
	facing = direction
	animated_sprite.play("walk_%s" % direction)

func play_idle() -> void:
	animated_sprite.play("idle_%s" % facing)

func play_emote(emote_id: String) -> void:
	if $AnimationPlayer.has_animation(emote_id):
		$AnimationPlayer.play(emote_id)
```

Minimum viable set:


```text id="66e7ap"
4 frames per direction
8–10 frames per second
subtle 1 px body bob
alternating feet

hair movement on side-facing walk
```

Frame pattern:

```text id="n8ksga"

1 left step
2 neutral / opposite bob
3 right step
```

```text id="d5d9pu"

collision: CharacterBody2D
navigation: free within tile-collision boundaries
animation direction: based on velocity
interaction ray/area: based on facing direction
```

This gives a Pokémon-like feel without making movement stiff.

## Y-sorting and layering

Use Y-sort for characters and tall object decorations.

The decoration system already uses a layered structure with floor, object, wall, and foreground decoration roots, and the Godot version explicitly places Player, NPCs, and tall blocking decorations inside a Y-sorted object layer.  Sprites should plug into that.

Recommended interior structure:

```text id="rvwksl"
InteriorRoot
├── TileMap_Ground
├── FloorDecorations
├── YSortObjects
│   ├── Player
│   ├── NPCs
│   ├── ObjectDecorations
│   └── TallInteractables
├── WallDecorations
├── ForegroundDecorations
└── UIAnchor
```

Rules:

```text id="72bbm6"
Player and NPCs: Y-sorted
Desks/cabinets/tall props: Y-sorted if walkable around them
Wall posters/signs: not Y-sorted
Foreground hanging lamps/rails: always above player
Ground papers/rugs/cables: always below player
```

The sprite origin should be at the foot anchor. This prevents weird sorting where a character's head determines whether they appear in front of a desk.

## Ground shadows

Every character sprite should have a simple soft pixel shadow.

```text id="xv6s9z"
standard shadow: 18×6 ellipse
large sprite shadow: 24×8 ellipse
opacity: 25–35%
position: under feet
```

In pixel art, this can be a small semi-transparent dark oval or dithered blob.

Purpose:

```text id="q0hqs0"
anchors sprite to floor
makes walking feel less floaty
helps depth sorting read
makes interiors feel coherent
```

Do not make shadows too realistic. They are readability tools.

## Dialogue integration

When a dialogue line starts, three things should happen:

```text id="w6a687"
world sprite faces player
portrait appears with expression
sprite may play a small emote
```

Dialogue schema:

```gdscript id="81u6kf"
{
	"speaker": "pig",
	"portrait": "pig",
	"expression": "panicked",
	"sprite_emote": "panic_bounce",
	"text": "The firm is not bankrupt. Bankruptcy has paperwork."
}
```

Runtime behavior:

```gdscript id="euf2so"
func play_dialogue_line(line: Dictionary) -> void:
	var speaker_sprite = NPCManager.get_sprite(line.get("speaker", ""))

	if speaker_sprite:
		speaker_sprite.face_position(Player.global_position)
		if line.has("sprite_emote"):
			speaker_sprite.play_emote(line["sprite_emote"])

	PortraitView.set_portrait(
		line.get("portrait", line["speaker"]),
		line.get("expression", "neutral"),
		line.get("frame", "normal")
	)

	DialogueBox.show_text(line["text"])
```

Use sprite emotes sparingly. The portrait does the acting. The sprite confirms that the person in the room is alive.

### Visual Effects

Visual effects should be **atmosphere plus feedback**, not decoration spam. The old plan already had the right instinct: effects should support parallax, particles, lighting, chapter tint, transitions, legal-impact beats, screen shake, and UI tactility. The Godot version should keep that logic but implement it as reusable scene/effect systems rather than canvas drawing helpers.

The target is:

```text id="ekp1v1"
pixel-art legal comedy
clear gameplay feedback
warm readable interiors
chapter-specific atmosphere
courtroom impact effects
paper, dust, steam, light, stamps, and printer violence
```

Effects should serve five jobs:

```text id="0c7ocn"

show interaction and evidence clearly
mark legal/argument beats
support chapter mood
make victories and contradictions satisfying
```

They should not make the screen busy. Pig & Swine is a legal RPG, not a spell-effect showcase. Most effects should be small: coffee steam, dust in archive light, paper glints, window rain, printer smoke, fluorescent flicker, stamp impacts, and brief contradiction flashes.

The rule:

## Effect Manager

Use one autoload:

```text id="nw68we"
res://effects/effect_manager.gd
```

It should handle spawning, transitions, camera shake, and UI impact effects.

```gdscript id="xrh6fp"
extends Node
class_name EffectManager

var current_area_effects_root: Node = null
var current_ui_root: CanvasLayer = null
var camera: Camera2D = null

func bind_area(effects_root: Node, area_camera: Camera2D, ui_root: CanvasLayer) -> void:
	current_area_effects_root = effects_root
	camera = area_camera
	current_ui_root = ui_root

func spawn_world_effect(effect_id: String, global_pos: Vector2, options := {}) -> Node:
	var scene := EffectRegistry.get_scene(effect_id)
	if scene == null or current_area_effects_root == null:
		return null

	var effect := scene.instantiate()
	effect.global_position = global_pos

	for key in options.keys():
		if effect.has_method("set_option"):
			effect.set_option(key, options[key])

	current_area_effects_root.get_node("EventEffects").add_child(effect)
	return effect

func spawn_ui_effect(effect_id: String, options := {}) -> Node:
	var scene := EffectRegistry.get_scene(effect_id)
	if scene == null or current_ui_root == null:
		return null

	var effect := scene.instantiate()
	current_ui_root.add_child(effect)

	if effect.has_method("configure"):
		effect.configure(options)

	return effect

func shake(duration := 0.12, intensity := 2.0) -> void:
	if camera and camera.has_method("shake"):
		camera.shake(duration, intensity)
```

## Effect definition schema

For room-level effects, use declarative definitions. This should mirror the decoration system: effects are content, not scattered code.

```gdscript id="8ckhxq"
{
	"id": "office_coffee_steam",
	"effect": "coffee_steam",
	"pos": Vector2i(17, 11),
	"layer": "AmbientEffects",
	"looping": true,
	"visible_if": "",
	"hidden_if": "",
	"intensity": 0.6
}
```

Typed resource version:

```gdscript id="ympcqt"
# res://effects/effect_definition.gd
extends Resource
class_name EffectDefinition

@export var id: String
@export var effect_id: String
@export var tile_pos: Vector2i
@export_enum("AmbientEffects", "WeatherEffects", "LightEffects", "EventEffects", "ForegroundEffects")
var layer: String = "AmbientEffects"

@export var looping: bool = true
@export var intensity: float = 1.0
@export var visible_if: String = ""
@export var hidden_if: String = ""
@export var state: String = "default"
```

Each room can export:

```gdscript id="r4nwub"
@export var ambient_effects: Array[EffectDefinition] = []
```

Spawner:

```gdscript id="dxa4kc"
func spawn_room_effects(effects: Array[EffectDefinition]) -> void:
	for effect_def in effects:
		if effect_def.visible_if != "" and !GameState.has_flag(effect_def.visible_if):
			continue
		if effect_def.hidden_if != "" and GameState.has_flag(effect_def.hidden_if):
			continue

		var scene := EffectRegistry.get_scene(effect_def.effect_id)
		if scene == null:
			push_warning("Missing effect: " + effect_def.effect_id)
			continue

		var node := scene.instantiate()
		node.position = tile_to_world(effect_def.tile_pos)

		if node.has_method("set_intensity"):
			node.set_intensity(effect_def.intensity)

		$EffectsRoot.get_node(effect_def.layer).add_child(node)
```

## 6. Camera effects

Camera effects should be rare and meaningful.

### Smooth follow

Default camera behavior:

```gdscript id="j8d1th"
@export var follow_smoothing := 8.0

func _process(delta: float) -> void:
	if target:
		global_position = global_position.lerp(target.global_position, 1.0 - exp(-follow_smoothing * delta))
```

Keep player movement readable. Do not over-smooth so much that the camera feels floaty.

### Focus pan

Use for reveals:

```text id="81oics"
Supreme Court stairs
Industrial Printer
City Hall notice wall
Final Brief board
Waldek's poster in the window
Swine's suspicious luggage
```

Implementation:

```gdscript id="ryenwr"
func focus_pan(target_pos: Vector2, duration := 0.6) -> void:
	InputManager.lock_input()
	var tween := create_tween()
	tween.tween_property(camera, "global_position", target_pos, duration)\
		.set_trans(Tween.TRANS_SINE)\
		.set_ease(Tween.EASE_IN_OUT)
	await tween.finished
	InputManager.unlock_input()
```

### Court crop / staged camera

Court sequences should usually switch to staged UI panels rather than zooming the tilemap too much. Pixel art can blur or look crude if the camera zoom is careless. Use portraits, court sprites, and UI frames for drama.

### Final reveal pan

Chapter 6 can use a slow pan across:

```text id="p0u29k"
Protest Square crowd
Supreme Court gallery
assembled Pig & Swine team
final office celebration
```

This should feel earned. One or two large pans in the whole game is enough.

## 7. Screen shake

Use screen shake very sparingly.

Good uses:

```text id="iw02xk"
printer explosion / paper burst
contradiction success
gavel impact
Final Printer boss hit
chapter-ending stamp
```

Bad uses:

```text id="kdtlbj"
ordinary dialogue
walking
every UI click
normal evidence pickup
every wrong answer
```

Godot `Camera2D` shake script:

```gdscript id="vlywhe"
extends Camera2D

var shake_time := 0.0
var shake_duration := 0.0
var shake_intensity := 0.0
var base_offset := Vector2.ZERO

func shake(duration: float, intensity: float) -> void:
	shake_duration = duration
	shake_time = duration
	shake_intensity = intensity

func _process(delta: float) -> void:
	if shake_time > 0.0:
		shake_time -= delta
		var strength := shake_time / shake_duration
		offset = Vector2(
			randf_range(-shake_intensity, shake_intensity),
			randf_range(-shake_intensity, shake_intensity)
		) * strength
	else:
		offset = Vector2.ZERO
```

Settings:

```gdscript id="tqc1qq"
small_shake = {
	"duration": 0.12,
	"intensity": 2.0
}

strong_shake = {
	"duration": 0.25,
	"intensity": 5.0
}
```

The old plan warns that too much shake will make the game feel cheap. Keep that warning.

## 12. Performance rules

Godot can handle plenty of effects, but keep the style cheap and crisp.

Rules:

```text id="zy20e1"
cap particles per room
avoid full-screen shaders unless necessary
disable offscreen effects
prefer small AnimatedSprite2D loops for pixel effects
use low particle counts for interiors
pool frequent effects like paper bursts
use CanvasLayer for UI impact effects
avoid blur filters
keep texture filtering nearest-neighbor
```

Suggested caps:

```gdscript id="cf09sw"
const MAX_EFFECTS := {
	"ambient_particles": 80,
	"event_particles": 150,
	"boss_particles": 250,
	"ui_effects": 8
}
```

Normal interiors rarely need more than 30–60 particles.

Use effect pooling for:

```text id="2g7o03"
paper flakes
spark particles
stamp puffs
toner particles
rain streaks
```

Do not pool big one-off UI effects unless profiling shows a problem.

## 13. Accessibility and readability

Effects must not hide gameplay.

Accessibility settings:

```text id="qhvk5r"

reduce_flash
reduce_particles
disable_weather_foreground
high_contrast_interaction_markers
```

Settings example:

```gdscript id="78tjgt"
var visual_accessibility := {
	"reduce_screen_shake": false,
	"reduce_flash": false,
	"particle_scale": 1.0,
	"weather_foreground": true
}
```

Effect Manager should respect them:

```gdscript id="3zydxx"
func shake(duration := 0.12, intensity := 2.0) -> void:
	if Settings.visual_accessibility.reduce_screen_shake:
		return
	camera.shake(duration, intensity)

func spawn_flash(color: Color, duration: float) -> void:
	if Settings.visual_accessibility.reduce_flash:
		duration *= 0.35
		color.a *= 0.4
```

Readability rules:

```text id="rdemfq"
quest-critical objects must remain visible through effects
enemy/projectile telegraphs must be clearer than decoration particles
court text must never be obscured
UI effects must not delay repeated play too much
weather must not reduce contrast below readable levels
```

## Part 4: Sound & Music Design

### Procedural Music System

The procedural music system should become one of the game's identity layers, not a technical afterthought.

The old implementation plan says the current game has procedural Web Audio sound effects but **no real music**, and that the game's existing presentation is shallow partly because it lacks authored atmosphere.  The earlier music plan also assumed a browser/Web Audio implementation with a `MusicEngine` class, oscillator notes, ADSR envelopes, looping tracks, crossfades, stingers, mute/volume, and core tracks for office, overworld, court, café, and archive.

Now that the game is moving to **Godot**, keep the same design goal but change the implementation model.

The new target:

```text
Procedural / semi-procedural retro music
Godot-native audio system
loopable chapter/location tracks
adaptive music layers
legal-comedy stingers
small motif system
no dependence on browser Web Audio
optional generated chiptune-style instruments
```

The soundtrack should sound like:

```text
paper
stamps
nervous basslines
tiny office machines
courtroom tension
warm café chords
printer trauma
one sincere melody trying to survive bureaucracy
```

The music should support the Pokémon-Yellow-style structure: small routes, readable interiors, NPC blockers, recurring hubs, route unlocks, and "battle-like" legal sequences. Chapter 1 is now a focused starter-town chapter with Office Street, Pig & Swine Office, Café Paragraf, Archive Room, District Court Plaza, and District Court Interior.  Chapter 2 then expands into Tenants' Row / Residential District, Waldek's Apartment, Landlord's Office, and Housing Court.  The music system should make those expansions feel audible before the player consciously analyses them.

---

## Design purpose

The procedural music system should do six jobs:

```text
give each district a clear identity
make Pig & Swine Office feel like home
signal legal tension without overexplaining it
support court "battle" sequences
make chapter transitions feel rewarding
reuse motifs so the game feels coherent
```

The music must not behave like generic looping background music.

It should react to:

```text

chapter state
court mode
evidence collection
argument success
argument failure
mini-game start/end
dialogue intensity
victory grade
final judgment state
```

A player should eventually recognise:

```text
"That is the Pig & Swine motif."
"That is the court argument version."
"That is the printer danger layer."
"That is the final brief version."
```


## Godot implementation model

Use Godot's audio system, not the old Web Audio design.

Recommended architecture:

```text
res://audio/music/music_manager.gd
res://audio/music/music_track.gd
res://audio/music/procedural_instrument.gd
res://audio/music/music_registry.gd
res://audio/music/stinger_registry.gd

res://audio/buses/default_bus_layout.tres
```

Use Godot audio buses:

```text
Master
Music
Music_Layers
Music_Stingers
Ambience
SFX
UI
Dialogue
```

Basic node structure:

```text
AudioRoot
├── MusicManager
│   ├── BaseTrackPlayer
│   ├── LayerPlayer_1
│   ├── LayerPlayer_2
│   ├── StingerPlayer
│   └── TransitionTimer
├── AmbienceManager
└── SfxManager
```

For the first playable version, do **not** overbuild fully generative composition. Use a hybrid model:

```text
Procedural arrangement:
Godot schedules note patterns, layers, tempo, instruments, and stingers.

Semi-authored musical data:
tracks are defined as patterns, scales, chord loops, rhythm cells, and motif fragments.

Generated playback:
the engine renders these through simple synthesized instruments or generated sample buffers.
```

This gives control without requiring finished audio files.

## Recommended technical approach

There are two viable Godot options.

### Option A — Procedural note scheduling

Use GDScript to schedule notes into simple generated instruments.

Best for:

```text
chiptune lines
bass ostinatos
simple arpeggios
paper/stamp percussion
adaptive layers
stingers
```

Useful Godot tools:

```text
AudioStreamGenerator
AudioStreamGeneratorPlayback
AudioStreamPlayer
AudioServer buses
Tween-based volume fades
```

This is closest to the old Web Audio plan but Godot-native.

### Option B — Tracker-style pattern playback

Define music as compact patterns:

```gdscript
var office_pattern := {
	"tempo": 104,
	"scale": "C_major",
	"chords": ["C", "Am", "F", "G"],
	"melody": [
		["E4", 0.25],
		["G4", 0.25],
		["A4", 0.5],
		["rest", 0.25],
	],
	"bass": [
		["C2", 0.5],
		["C2", 0.5],
		["A2", 0.5],
		["A2", 0.5],
	]
}
```

Best for:

```text
loop reliability
small file size
easy iteration
chapter variants
motif reuse
```

This should be the preferred v1 approach.

## MusicManager API

Create one central autoload:

```text
MusicManager
```

Recommended public API:

```gdscript
MusicManager.play_track("office")
MusicManager.crossfade_to("court_argument", 1.5)
MusicManager.stop_music(1.0)

MusicManager.set_intensity(0.0) # calm
MusicManager.set_intensity(0.5) # investigation
MusicManager.set_intensity(1.0) # court pressure

MusicManager.play_stinger("evidence_found")
MusicManager.play_stinger("argument_correct")
MusicManager.play_stinger("argument_wrong")
MusicManager.play_stinger("court_victory")

MusicManager.duck_for_dialogue(true)
MusicManager.set_music_volume(0.75)
MusicManager.set_music_muted(false)
```

Godot-style skeleton:

```gdscript
# res://audio/music/music_manager.gd
extends Node
class_name MusicManager

@export var default_crossfade_time := 1.2

var current_track_id: String = ""
var current_intensity: float = 0.0
var music_volume: float = 0.8
var muted: bool = false

@onready var base_player: AudioStreamPlayer = $BaseTrackPlayer
@onready var layer_player_1: AudioStreamPlayer = $LayerPlayer1
@onready var layer_player_2: AudioStreamPlayer = $LayerPlayer2
@onready var stinger_player: AudioStreamPlayer = $StingerPlayer

func play_track(track_id: String, fade_time := default_crossfade_time) -> void:
	if current_track_id == track_id:
		return

	current_track_id = track_id
	_crossfade_to_track(track_id, fade_time)

func set_intensity(value: float) -> void:
	current_intensity = clampf(value, 0.0, 1.0)
	_update_layers()

func play_stinger(stinger_id: String) -> void:
	var stream := StingerRegistry.get_stinger_stream(stinger_id)
	if stream:
		stinger_player.stream = stream
		stinger_player.play()

func duck_for_dialogue(enabled: bool) -> void:
	var target_db := -8.0 if enabled else 0.0
	var tween := create_tween()
	tween.tween_property(base_player, "volume_db", target_db, 0.25)
	tween.parallel().tween_property(layer_player_1, "volume_db", target_db, 0.25)

func set_music_volume(value: float) -> void:
	music_volume = clampf(value, 0.0, 1.0)
	AudioServer.set_bus_volume_db(AudioServer.get_bus_index("Music"), linear_to_db(music_volume))

func set_music_muted(value: bool) -> void:
	muted = value
	AudioServer.set_bus_mute(AudioServer.get_bus_index("Music"), muted)
```

## Stinger system

Stingers are short musical phrases that play over or briefly interrupt the current track.

Required stingers:

```text
quest_started
quest_updated
evidence_found
argument_unlocked
ally_recruited
wrong_choice
correct_argument
contradiction_hit
court_round_won
court_victory
badge_earned
chapter_complete
strong_victory
weak_victory
printer_warning
final_judgment
```

Rules:

```text
Stingers should be short: 0.4–2.0 seconds.
They should not restart the background track.
They should be mixed through Music_Stingers.
They should have priority rules.
They should reuse chapter motifs.
```

Priority example:

```text
final_judgment > court_victory > contradiction_hit > evidence_found > quest_updated
```

If a high-priority stinger plays, lower-priority stingers should be delayed or skipped.

## Adaptive music layers

Each track should support at least three intensity states:

```text
0 — calm / exploration
1 — investigation / uncertainty
2 — pressure / court / crisis
```

Later, use five states:

```text
0.00 calm
0.25 active
0.50 investigation
0.75 pressure
1.00 climax
```

Example:

```gdscript
MusicManager.play_track("office")
MusicManager.set_intensity(0.25)

# Mr. Pig reveals crisis
MusicManager.set_intensity(0.75)

# Asia gives calm guidance
MusicManager.set_intensity(0.35)

# Court readiness confirmed
MusicManager.play_stinger("quest_updated")
MusicManager.crossfade_to("overworld_day")
```

Dialogue ducking:

```text
Normal dialogue:
music ducks slightly

Important legal explanation:
music ducks more, percussion removed

Court argument:
music stays present but avoids masking text ticks

Victory line:
music briefly clears space for final sentence, then stinger
```

## Room-to-music routing

Each Godot scene should declare its music metadata.

Example:

```gdscript
# res://world/areas/office_street.gd
@export var music_track := "overworld_day"
@export var music_intensity := 0.25

```

Interior example:

```gdscript
# res://interiors/pig_swine_office.gd
@export var music_track := "office"
@export var ambience := "office_hum"

func _ready() -> void:
	MusicManager.play_track(get_office_track())
	AmbienceManager.play(ambience)

func get_office_track() -> String:
	if GameState.chapter6.started:
		return "office_final_brief"
	if GameState.chapter4.printer_incident_seen:
		return "office_printer_suspicion"
	if GameState.chapter1.complete:
		return "office_after_victory"
	return "office"
```

Court example:

```gdscript
func enter_court_mode(court_id: String) -> void:
	match court_id:
		"district_court":
			MusicManager.crossfade_to("court_argument")
		"housing_court":
			MusicManager.crossfade_to("housing_court")
		"supreme_court":
			MusicManager.crossfade_to("supreme_court")
```

## Music and ambience separation

Do not bake ambience into music.

Music:

```text

```

Ambience:

```text

paper shuffling

```

The old interior plan already separates room ambience from music, with examples such as office hum, café chatter, court murmurs, server fans, airport announcements, and industrial printer machinery.  Keep that distinction in Godot.

This lets the player lower music but keep the world audible.

## Minimum viable procedural music system

The first useful Godot version needs only:

```text
MusicManager autoload
Music bus + Stinger bus
play_track()
crossfade_to()
stop_music()
set_intensity()
play_stinger()
duck_for_dialogue()
mute/volume persistence

office loop
overworld loop
court loop
café loop
archive loop

evidence_found stinger
argument_correct stinger
argument_wrong stinger
court_victory stinger
badge_earned stinger
```

That is enough to replace silence or generic looping with a real sound identity.

Do not start by composing all five chapters. Start with Chapter 1 and make it feel finished.

## Acceptance criteria

The Procedural Music System is complete when:

```text
Godot controls music through a MusicManager autoload
music uses procedural or semi-procedural pattern data
tracks loop cleanly without obvious gaps
room transitions trigger appropriate music changes
crossfades work between exploration, interiors, courts, and mini-games
music volume, mute, and ambience settings persist
dialogue can duck or thin the music
court scenes can raise intensity
stingers play over background music without restarting tracks
evidence collection and argument success have distinct musical feedback
the Pig & Swine motif appears in office, court, victory, and ending forms
each chapter has at least one distinctive musical identity
Office, Overworld, Court, Café, Archive, Tenants' Row, Server Room, Protest Square, Final Court, and Ending feel musically distinct
```

Final standard:

> The game should sound like a legal RPG built out of paper, stamps, nervous deadlines, cheap office equipment, and one unexpectedly sincere theme.
